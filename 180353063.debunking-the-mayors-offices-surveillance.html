TL/DR

The Mayor’s Office is asking Metro Council to approve an MOU with The Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP) for a $15M state funded public safety grant that has earmarks for MNPD and Metro Nashville generally to receive highly controversial surveillance technology. In a brief email to Metro Council last week, the administration discredits concerns about this resolution as “misinformation.” The administration claims this resolution is actually an exercise in oversight, allegedly designed to “limit” how NDP can spend this public safety money, preventing NDP from using it in ways Nashvillians may find undesirable.

This reading of the resolution’s purpose, to constrain the NDP from overreaching with surveillance technology, is not consistent with the language of the MOU or the resolution itself. This perspective is also contradicted by recollections of some who are familiar with the origin of this MOU with NDP.

Here are three alternative explanations of what this MOU and resolution actually accomplish. If one, two, or all three of these plausible functions is correct, this fundamentally contradicts what the administration is saying the purpose of the MOU is — and it changes the political calculus of what it means to approve this resolution.

The Three Plausible Functions of this MOU

  1. Letter of Agreement: It serves as the required “Letter of Agreement” for NDP’s grant application (meaning if they have indeed already been “awarded” this grant, it is possibly contingent on the approval of this document). In point of fact, the resolution explicitly states that this agreement is part of the submission packet for this grant. This makes it hard to square with the administration’s statement that “NDP has [already] been awarded a state grant,” and that this MOU is not necessary for them to receive the funds.

  2. Standing for Applying: It makes NDP eligible to apply for the grant. Given that NDP is not the organization cited by Metro code as actually overseeing the operations of a Business Improvement District (and is merely a vendor of the District Management Corporation [DMC]). Without this document signed by Metro and the DMC, NDP very plausibly may not qualify to receive these funds.

  3. Obligates purchases for Metro outside procurement: Rather than restricting what NDP can do with the funds, it clearly requires NDP to purchase specific surveillance tech (including LeoSight, a Fusus-adjacent surveillance integration platform), the AI-intelligence gathering tool Fivecast, an armored vehicle, and digital command post, and mandates they provide access to these to Metro (without any specifics on how this transfer of ownership would take place).

If any of these three are accurate, it accomplishes this result: The effect is that this resolution, by enabling this grant to NDP, bypasses normal Metro Council procurement oversight by routing surveillance equipment through a private nonprofit

The bottom line: This isn’t oversight—it’s an end-run around Metro Council’s authority to approve surveillance technology purchases. Voting FOR this resolution doesn’t limit surveillance, it enables it in a wholly new, unaccountable way.

Voting for this resolution enables the Mayor’s Office to acquire surveillance infrastructure without direct Council approval, and in fact likely obligates NDP to purchase that equipment and provide access to Metro in informal ways that will not require future Council approval — a banned surveillance tech via nonprofit pipeline that has been implemented in cities like New Orleans.

The only reasonable actions given this information is either:

  1. The Mayor’s Office retracting this resolution until such time as they are willing to provide candid, transparent information about the origin of this grant application, MOU and its Exhibit A, as well as candor regarding the actual procedural purpose of resolution 2025-1659;

  2. An immediate deferral in committee until good faith, logically consistent information can be obtained from the administration, the DMC, NDP, MNPD and any other other parties that seem to have known about this grant application and MOU; or

  3. A down vote on the resolution by the full Metro Council in the event that it some how escapes committee.

Any other action by the Mayor’s Office or Metro Council would be a blatant dereliction of civic duty. Ungovernable surveillance technology in the hands of private corporations—corporations that can share data with law enforcement without warrants or subpoenas under Tennessee’s Information Protection Act—pose a clear and present threat to civil liberties and public safety. There is no excuse to flippantly gamble with people’s lives and civil liberties, no matter what big games they think they can play in Nashville.

There is a national pattern of using non-profits to circumvent surveillance oversight and put millions into private pockets for spying on citizens.
 Other cities are seeing non-profits being used to circumvent local oversight on surveillance technology. The model of this grant is not operating in a vacuum — it is an example of a national pattern of side-stepping public oversight using private organizations that work hand-in-hand with local law enforcement, giving them access to tools they would not otherwise be allowed to use.

Even Elected Officials are in the Dark About this $15M Grant

The public is doing its best to play catch-up with this memorandum of understanding (MOU) and its related resolution, working overtime to understand what is actually happening here.

The Mayor’s Office, operating with information asymmetry, is denigrating people like me who are trying our best to inform the public about this. It is hard to take them seriously when they speak out against “misinformation” when they are the ones that are submitting resolutions aimed at steering $15M to a highly controversial private organization over a holiday without so much as a press release, and when caught in the act, claim they are doing this in the name of public oversight.

The Mayor’s Office is saying they’re looking out for the public’s best interest with this MOU. If that is true, why was their way of going about that explicitly designed to forego any and all public discussion and oversight?

Even the Council Member whose district this money and millions of dollars in surveillance technology would flow into is playing catch-up. Some council members appear disturbed that all of Metro Council (and seemingly everyone but the Mayor’s Office) has been in the dark on this.

“Misinformation”

Subsequent to this feed publishing information about an MOU between the Mayor’s Office and the Nashville Downtown Partnership, the administration has done some outreach to the Metro Council. They’ve called the information shared here and in other places like on the Instagram for the Nashville Community Safety Network “misinformation.”

As a reminder, the first time content on {Rich Text} was called misinformation by a Mayor’s Office appointee was when the Office of Homeless Services director referred to reporting she didn’t like as “fake news.” The result of that reporting was a unanimous approval of an internal audit of her office, signed off on even by Mayor O’Connell’s Director of Finance.

Let’s look at the latest accusation that we are spreading “misinformation.” Dave Rosenberg, O’Connell’s Director of Legislative Affairs, chose to respond to the outpouring of community concern about the surveillance tech being earmarked for Metro Nashville with this brief missive addressed only to Metro Council, the body he needs to convince that MOU is a good idea:

RS2025-1659 NDP MOU

This resolution has been the subject of a significant amount of misinformation. NDP has been awarded a state grant of $15 million and expects to receive the money from the state by the end of December. Under this MOU, NDP agrees to limit the uses of the funds to a finite list, all of which are already permitted by Metro. (Absent the MOU, NDP has the option to apply the funds to a wider list of uses, though they’ve stated they will not do so.) Additionally, NDP will donate most of the grant to Metro, and those donations will be subject to future Council legislation.

This explanation may or may not be an honest explanation of the administration’s intent behind the submission of this resolution. Unfortunately, if this was their intent, they did not write the resolution or MOU in a way that accomplishes these goals.

Resolution RS2025-1659 is not written in latin, or even any complicated legalese. The Mayor’s Office has been framing this resolution as something that Council should support if they are concerned about surveillance, and CMs are communicating this back to concerned constituents — that this resolution is actually anti-surveillance.

CMs need to be extremely careful about perpetuating this argument uncritically. They should not take my word, or Mr. Rosenberg’s, on what this MOU and its resolution accomplishes. They should read the resolution, the MOU, and the attached grant for themselves.

When it comes to the resolution, please draw your attention to these two passages:

Section 1 clearly states that this is a document that is to be submitted as part of the application for this grant with the Office of Criminal Justice Programs. This would be a submission as part of the application that Mr. Rosenberg is claiming “has already been awarded.”

Section 2 when reading in the context of the attached Exhibit A, states that if NDP were to receive this grant, they would essentially be required to provide Metro with the laundry list of surveillance tools that, reports have it, the Mayor’s Office wrote into the exhibit. It does not in any meaningful way “limit” how these funds are used except that it obligates NDP to provide surveillance tools allegedly added into this MOU by the Mayor’s Office. It also stipulates that it must be submitted as part of the application to the state of Tennessee, which was due Oct 24, 2025.

Assertion 1: This MOU is a document NDP is required to submit to get the grant, not extra oversight from the Mayor’s Office.

Mr. Rosenberg asserts that “NDP has been awarded a state grant of $15 million and expects to receive the money from the state by the end of December.” That this grant is a done deal is hard to square with the fact that NDP does not yet appear to be in possession of a Metro Council approved “letter of agreement” as the grant requires. A document that is extremely similar, if not exactly what this MOU in point of fact is.

Note: In my personal experience applying for and winning a $2M federal grant while working at the Mayor’s Office, grants of this nature sometimes allow documents such as this to be submitted after the due date, with a contingent approval being granted pending the provision of full documentation. The fact that NDP has allegedly been told that they otherwise “will receive this grant” can be in alignment with the fact that this MOU is a pre-requisite for the final approval of their application.

It is also explicit in the resolution itself that this memorandum is to be submitted “as part of the application to the State of Tennessee, Office Criminal Justice Programs.” Mr. Rosenberg is framing the award determination of this grant, which was due on Oct 24, in the past tense. Yet he offers no explanation for why one of the required documents for the grant to be approved was seemingly submitted to Council (with no prior notice that this grant application even existed) on Nov 21.

Here is the documentation of what is needed to get approved for this grant. Please turn your attention to the bullet detailing a required “Letter of Agreement” that appears in the grant solicitation. The details of this requirement are the following:

Local governments must coordinate with the eligible BIDs and BID management corporation within their respective jurisdictions to ensure that their grant applications, collectively, do not request funds exceeding the maximum dollar amount available to a respective jurisdiction. All applicants with the same municipal jurisdiction must sign and return the Letter of Agreement to demonstrate their understanding of the funding limit applicable to their respective jurisdiction.

As evidence that this resolution and the attached MOU are an attempt to fulfill this documentation requirement of a “letter of agreement”, please now turn your attention to the MOU submitted by the Mayor’s Office for Council approval via RS2025-1659. You will note that the MOU signature page is entitled “Letter of Agreement, One Per Municipal Jurisdiction” and that it explicitly states what percentage of the funding each of these entities will be requesting.

The grant application requires submission of a “letter of agreement” per “respective jurisdictions.” The MOU being submitted to Metro Council for approval is titled “letter of agreement, one per municipal jurisdiction” The conclusion is straightforward: this MOU serves as the mandatory documentation needed to complete NDP’s grant application.

So the assertion that NDP “already has the grant” seems like less of a given than Mr. Rosenberg has stated. It is highly plausible that NDP’s approval is pending the submission of the required “letter of agreement.”

But this takes us to another issue: How is NDP even eligible for this grant?

Nashville Downtown Partnership ≠ District Management Corporation

The MOU likely serves another critical function: making NDP eligible to apply in the first place. According to the grant solicitation, only metropolitan governments and business improvement district management corporations can apply. NDP is neither of these.

Assertion 2 : Without this MOU authorizing them to apply on behalf of Metro and the DMC, NDP is not qualified to apply for this grant.

The Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP) is neither the Metropolitan Government of Nashville, nor a business improvement district management corporation. NDP is a vendor hired by Nashville’s “District Management Corporation” (DMC).

The DMC, with the EIN 62-1747009 is the actual entity that “manages or oversee[s] the operations of [a]… Central Business Improvement District.”

The Nashville Downtown Partnership, with the EIN 62-1774641, is simply a vendor hired to perform the operational tasks of the CBID. Despite passing off many, if not all of the tasks of the DMC to NDP, this does not actually render NDP the operator of the Central Business Improvement District (CBID). If I am incorrect in this assertion, please provide me with the documentation that demonstrates otherwise and I will post a correction.

As evidence that the DMC is the organization that actually oversees the operations of the CBID and not NDP, please see the CBID’s founding ordinance from 1998.

You will note the curious condition that 8 of the 12 members of the DMC’s board shall be appointed by the Nashville Downtown Partnership. This strange arrangement has led many, perhaps even the CEO, President and Executive Director of the two organizations (who are all the same person, Tom Turner) to be confused about this matter. But they are, in point of fact, two different organizations.

One organization has responsibility dictated by Metro Code to actually oversee the central business improvement district. The other is a non-profit that pays its chief executive over $450k a year, hires lobbyists, contracts with two vendors to do the bulk of their actual labor. They are not the same organization, even though they share many people at the leadership and board levels. You can learn more about this strange arrangement here:

From my research, the Nashville Downtown Partnership has no special standing in respect to the business improvement district that is eligible for this grant. Their standing hardly seems much different than the vendors that in turn work for NDP, such as Solaren and Block by Block. None of those three organizations technically “manage or oversee operations” of the CBID. And therefore, none of them appear eligible to apply for these funds. To do so, they would likely need something such as an MOU entitling them to apply on behalf of the DMC and Metro Nashville. And that is precisely what this document appears to be.

“Limit” Expenditures, or require they buy what the Mayor’s Office wants outside of public oversight?

Mr. Rosenberg’s explanation that this resolution is designed to limit NDP’s use of these funds stretches all credulity. The fact that he submitted this “fact check” to the people whose votes he’s trying to whip, rather than those who are trying to inform the public of what is office is doing, is very telling.

If you were to take his word for it (and not actually read the resolution and look at the MOU and its “Exhibit A”), you might think that voting FOR this resolution was a way to provide oversight on these funds. That does not align with how they wrote this resolution, however.

According to Mr. Rosenberg:

Under this MOU, NDP agrees to limit the uses of the funds to a finite list, all of which are already permitted by Metro. (Absent the MOU, NDP has the option to apply the funds to a wider list of uses, though they’ve stated they will not do so.)

Firstly, if this is a list of resources that Metro is ok with NDP procuring, what didn’t make the list? Plutonium?

Secondly, the MOU is not written to “limit” NDP.

Assertion 3: this MOU is clearly written so that NDP is required to purchase surveillance technology and equipment on behalf of Metro, allocations that would be unlikely to pass through Metro Council.

Take, for example, Exhibit A’s line item for "LeoSight.”

LeoSight is a surveillance integration tool that was founded in March 2025 by someone who three months prior was the Chief Revenue Officer of Fusus. It is a rebrand of a company called LiveEarth, revamped as a surveillance integration tool for law enforcement agencies. One could understand why the Mayor’s Office was not eager to present a contract for this tool (brought to market literally by the makers of Fusus) directly to Council. In this light, we can see this MOU functioning as a way to get this technology without asking permission.

Reports are that it was not NDP who wanted these high end surveillance tools. It was allegedly the Mayor’s Office who put these into the MOU, and they seemed intent on getting these things without asking Metro Council to approve them.

It should also be noted that the grant from the state of TN actually has language about unallowable costs. You’ll note that it is written as things that NDP is not allowed to buy, rather than as things that they are obligated to buy and share with Metro. This is how you limit use cases, by listing what one may not buy, not by adding things that you want them to buy for you.

Which brings us back to the question: “What is the purpose of this MOU.”

What’s It All Mean?

Mr. Rosenberg claims that the purpose of this MOU and resolution is the following:

  • To limit NDP’s use of the $15M grant to items already permitted by Metro

  • To ensure NDP donates most of the grant to Metro

  • To subject those donations to future Council legislation

However, the actual documents reveal a different function entirely:

What this MOU may actually accomplish:

  1. Makes NDP eligible to apply for the grant - Without this Letter of Agreement, NDP (as a vendor, not the actual District Management Corporation) would likely not qualify under the grant solicitation’s eligibility requirements.

  2. Fulfills a required grant application component - The MOU titled “Letter of Agreement” directly corresponds to a mandatory element in the state grant solicitation, suggesting NDP has not yet been “awarded” the grant as Mr. Rosenberg claims, or perhaps has been awarded it contingent on this MOU’s approval.

  3. Obligates NDP to purchase and provide Metro with specific surveillance technologies - Rather than “limiting” NDP, Exhibit A creates a shopping list of surveillance tools including LeoSight (a Fusus-adjacent integration platform) that would likely face scrutiny if presented to Metro Council directly. These would only need Metro approval if they were formally gifted to Metro, but if MNPD were, for example, allowed to use this technology informally while it is owned and operated by NDP, this would side step any legitimate oversight. As extraordinary as a situation like that sounds, this is precisely the model of avoiding oversight using nonprofits that we see in city’s like New Orleans.

  4. All of this accomplishes the overall goal: to bypasses standard Metro Council procurement oversight - By structuring this as a private entity’s grant application that then “donates” equipment to Metro, the administration avoids the usual legislative approval process for surveillance technology contracts.

The resolution does not provide oversight. It provides cover—allowing the Mayor’s Office to acquire surveillance infrastructure through an indirect procurement mechanism that circumvents the Council’s typical role in approving such purchases.

If Metro Council intended to provide oversight on these funds, they would not vote to approve this MOU. They would instead insist on standard procurement processes for any surveillance technology Metro ultimately receives. And they would ask a fundamental question that the Mayor’s Office seems unwilling to address:

If this grant is to purchase public safety technology that Nashvillians would have no problem with them purchasing, and isn’t just being jammed through the side door to satisfy the same powerful lobby of political operators that forced Mayor O’Connell to do a 180 on surveillance with Fusus, then why didn’t Metro just apply for these funds and work on a grant application collectively with relevant stakeholders?

Why was this application and the list of what Metro was going to get out of it completed in secret, with required documents being submitted surreptitiously over a holiday break?

The Mayor’s Office is saying they’re looking out for the public’s best interest with this MOU. If that is true, why was their way of going about that explicitly designed to forego any and all public discussion and oversight?

I am not one to throw around accusations of acting in bad faith lightly. I suppose I left that behind when I resigned from the O’Connell administration, and so I will leave it to his appointees to accuse people of spreading fake news any time they say things that are inconvenient for their political ambitions.

I’m not sure what to call Mr. Rosenberg’s explanation. It doesn’t match the actual text of the resolution and MOU. It also contradicts what sources familiar with the document’s creation say about how surveillance technology line items earmarked for Metro ended up in Exhibit A. I am not as creative a writer as Mr. Rosenberg appears to be. If only I could think of a term for when people provide information in a way that is misleading…
