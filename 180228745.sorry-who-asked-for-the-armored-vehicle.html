And from what some Council Members are telling concerned Nashvillians about the surveillance MOU up for vote on Dec. 4, it sounds like some of them are buying the “fake news” angle from the Mayor’s Office hook, line and sinker.

As a reminder, the first time content here was called misinformation was when the Office of Homeless Services director referred to reporting she didn’t like as “fake news,”. The result of that reporting was a unanimously approved internal audit of her office, signed off on even by Mayor O’Connell’s Director of Finance.

This time, reports about the contents of the MOU his office submitted to Council are being called “misinformation,” and Council is being told to ignore its actual text and to take their word on what they're being asked to sign off on.

Similar to when OHS wasn’t a fan of what was being said, no corrections were actually submitted to {Rich Text}. Nor were any corrections sent to the Instagram account that broke the news, the Nashville Community Safety Network.

Instead, like with OHS, the messaging went exclusively to the people whose opinion they want to influence: the Metro Council. The Mayor’s Office doesn't seem concerned with the public understanding the truth, just making sure those with the power of the purse believe their version of things.

Dave Rosenberg, O’Connell’s Director of Legislative Affairs, chose to respond to the outpouring of community concern about the surveillance tech being earmarked for Nashville with this brief missive addressed only to Metro Council:

RS2025-1659 NDP MOU

This resolution has been the subject of a significant amount of misinformation. NDP has been awarded a state grant of $15 million and expects to receive the money from the state by the end of December. Under this MOU, NDP agrees to limit the uses of the funds to a finite list, all of which are already permitted by Metro. (Absent the MOU, NDP has the option to apply the funds to a wider list of uses, though they’ve stated they will not do so.) Additionally, NDP will donate most of the grant to Metro, and those donations will be subject to future Council legislation.

I think this paragraph is a master class in political doublespeak. You can find literally anything you want in this messaging except a clear understanding of the purpose of this MOU that aligns with what they are actually asking Council to sign.

Are you anti-surveillance? You should vote for this because it allegedly “limits” NDP. Are you pro-surveillance? You should vote for this because it's funding for Metro to do the type of surveillance your colleagues in Council already shot down.

Like any political messaging, they are pretending this resolution is a win for everyone's agenda, except, I’d argue, the NDP. The Mayor’s Office hasn't figured out how to get what they want without making them look like bad actors. And I can't imagine they're pleased about being put in this position.

Throwing NDP Under the Bus

Read that again carefully. According to the Mayor’s Office, the Nashville Downtown Partnership needs to be “limited.” Without this MOU, they tell us, NDP “has the option to apply the funds to a wider list of uses”—implying NDP might do something Metro wouldn’t approve. Rosenberg claims that “they’ve stated they will not do so”, but it seems like an urgent enough concern that they slipped in an MOU just before a holiday break.

The shockingly obvious flaw in this logic is that if “NDP will donate most of the grant to Metro” why in the world did Metro itself not apply for these funds? Metro Nashville is the entity that the State of Tennessee indicated was eligible to apply, the purpose of this MOU seems to clearly to entitle NDP to apply for these funds.

Without this MOU, NDP—which is essentially a random nonprofit rather than the actual entity overseeing the Central Business Improvement District—likely wouldn’t be eligible for this funding. My best guess is that this is a required document that was initially overlooked and now needs to be passed to allow the grant to move forward. It’s not unusual for grants to be awarded contingent on submitting required documentation after the fact. That said, I could be wrong—and regardless, that’s not the focus of this post.

But Rosenberg’s explanation of the purpose of MOU is extremely convoluted, and seems aimed to make surveillance skeptics think this agreement is actually in their best they claim that its purpose is just limit how NDP uses these. That is hard to align with the actual text of the resolution.

Here’s why that is nonsense.

Firstly, looking at the list of what Mayor O’Connell is cool with NDP applying for, is shocking.

  • Surveillance from the CRO of Fusus

  • AI dossier creation software Fivecast

  • A mobile command post

  • an armored vehicle

And all, as written, to be purchased and controlled by NDP despite whatever hands shake deals do not appear here in black and white.

I can’t image what didn’t make the list. Plutonium?

But talking about this misses the point entirely.

This framing paints NDP as a potential threat that needs restraining. As if, absent this agreement, the Nashville Downtown Partnership might go rogue and buy even worse surveillance equipment than what’s already in the MOU (much of it earmarked for use by Metro, put there, it's is being said, by the Mayor’s Office and/or MNPD).

I’ve written a lot about problems with NDP and the lack of accountability placed on them. But I haven’t come across an ambition to start their own intelligence agency.

I gotta say, I think the Mayor’s Office is really playing fast and loose with NDP’s reputation on this one.

I would be pretty angry if I had signed on for this arrangement with Metro and was being treated this way. From what I can see, NDP is doing the Mayor’s Office a favor, helping them launder surveillance equipment through a state grant with little to no oversight. And this is how the Mayor’s Office treats people who do them a favor? I'd hate to be someone they retaliated against.

Blaming NDP like this, it would be like agreeing to hold someone’s bag while they tie their shoe, and then having them loudly announce to everyone nearby that they need to watch you carefully to make sure you don’t steal anything.

Yet they seem happy to accuse NDP of all kinds of scary things if we don’t put oversight on them for this grant.

What “Limit” Actually Means Here

Rosenberg’s framing is deliberately backwards. He wants you to think the MOU prevents NDP from buying dangerous things. But that’s not what’s happening at all.

Here’s what the “limit” actually does:

Without the MOU, NDP might still receive $15 million and could theoretically spend it on any allowable grant items—or even change their mind about what to purchase. They could decide the surveillance equipment is too controversial. They could pivot to other public safety priorities that actually prevent crime and blight in public spaces. They have options.

I do want to say that I'm not convinced NDP will receive these funds anyway if this MOU is not passed. The resolution’s attachment looks suspiciously like a requirement of the grant itself which would allow NDP, a vendor hired by the business improvement district’s District Management Corporation, to be the exclusive entity to apply for this grant.

The plain text of this resolution says that Metro is declining to apply for this grant and that the vendor of the District Management Corporation’s vendor, the Nashville downtown partnership, will be able to apply for 100% of these funds.

Without this MOU, NDP—which is essentially a random nonprofit rather than the actual entity overseeing the Central Business Improvement District—likely wouldn’t be eligible for this funding. My best guess is that this is a required document that was initially overlooked and now needs to be passed to allow the grant to move forward. It’s not unusual for grants to be awarded contingent on submitting required documentation after the fact. That said, I could be wrong—and regardless, that’s not the focus of this post.

The MOU removes those options. It legally binds NDP to purchase specific surveillance equipment and transfer it to Metro.

That’s what “limit” means in this context. The MOU constrains NDP so they HAVE to buy the surveillance tech. If this gets passed, NDP can’t change their mind. They can’t say “actually, looking at the public outcry against this and the fact that we’re not exactly popular with the public after our vendor exploded the library parking garage, this seems like a bad idea.”

Read Exhibit A—it’s not a list of things NDP cannot buy. It’s a list of things NDP is now legally obligated to purchase and, though the details don’t appear in any detail, need to turn over to NDP.

  • Armored rescue vehicles for Metro

  • LeoSight surveillance integration for Metro

  • Fivecast AI monitoring for Metro

  • Integrated camera systems for Metro

  • Mobile command posts for Metro

  • Tactical communications equipment for Metro

Why This Matters

The Mayor’s Office is framing this as protective oversight—”We’re limiting NDP from doing something dangerous.” But the constraint actually works in the opposite direction.

The constraint protects Metro’s interests, not the public’s. It ensures that NDP cannot change course, cannot reconsider, cannot decide this surveillance tech is a bad idea. They’re locked in. They must purchase it and they must give it to Metro.

That’s why calling it “limit” is so dishonest. When you limit someone from doing what they want to do anyway, you’re not limiting them—you’re forcing them to do what you want.

And that’s why this throws NDP under the bus so badly. The Mayor’s Office is publicly claiming NDP needs to be constrained because they might buy dangerous surveillance tech—when actually, the limiting forces NDP to buy surveillance tech for Metro and removes their ability to reconsider without getting an update to this MOU.

Or perhaps the board of NDP doesn’t like the bad publicity this is giving them and might not want to roll into Trump year two being seen as enabling surveillance tech that once installed cannot be kept out of the hands of federal agents to do with as they wish. For some reason the Mayor who championed data privacy while on Council can’t see why that might be a bad thing to connect to his legacy. But I bet not everyone on the board of NDP and pays into the District Management Corporation is quite so eager to align themselves federal surveillance dragnet that has never met a civil right they won’t break.

NDP isn’t the threat that needs constraining. It’s Metro that needs to be constrained. And that starts with rejecting this MOU. And asking how the f#*k it got this far without any public discussion or it.

The Grant Already Limits Everything

Here’s the other thing Rosenberg doesn’t tell you: The state grant already limits how these funds can be used. Tennessee’s grant solicitation document specifies exactly what’s allowed—surveillance cameras, command posts, tactical equipment, public safety software. NDP had to apply under those restrictions.

So the MOU isn’t actually constraining NDP’s options. The state grant already did that. What the MOU does is commit NDP to purchase specific surveillance equipment from that pre-approved list—equipment that serves Metro’s law enforcement interests, not NDP’s downtown beautification mission.

The Real Story

Strip away the misdirection:

  1. Metro wanted surveillance equipment Council has repeatedly rejected

  2. Metro had NDP apply for the full $15 million grant Metro was eligible for

  3. The grant application included tactical and surveillance equipment that serves Metro’s purposes

  4. Now Metro is framing the MOU as “constraining” NDP—implying NDP is the problem

  5. This allows them to claim oversight while getting exactly what they wanted

The characterization of NDP as needing constraint is classic psychological projection. The Mayor’s Office is describing their own desires and attributing them to NDP.

The Question Council Should Ask

Before voting on December 4, Metro Council should ask:

“Does the Nashville Downtown Partnership’s mission of downtown events and cleanliness require armored rescue vehicles, AI surveillance monitoring billions of people, and mobile tactical command posts?”

The answer is obviously no. Which means someone else wanted these items. And that someone is using NDP as a pass-through to acquire surveillance technology without Council’s appropriations oversight.

The Mayor’s Office can call reporting “misinformation” all they want. And so long as they act in the shadows with information asymmetry, they're always going to have nitpicks to claim people are not 100% right and acting in bad faith.

But reporting you're best understanding of an incomplete picture for the public good is not acting in bad faith. But keeping vital, high controversial surveillance expenditures a secret and denigrating those who try to educate the public on it as engaging in “misinformation”, without actually even explaining in any coherent way how they are incorrect? That feels much closer to “acting in bad faith.”

Just like with OHS, the documents speak for themselves. I'm not asking anyone to take my word on anything, just read for yourself. And notice when they do ask you to take their word on things. When they say “there’s nothing to see here,” ask why they submitted it last minute before a holiday with precisely zero public notice or discussion?

Ask why the process was followed in Memphis and the other cities these funding flowed to, slipping in under the radar via MOUs. This isn't an accident, there's clear coordination between the state and local agencies, all outside democratic oversight.

What this reveals is an administration willing to characterize a downtown events nonprofit as a surveillance threat needing constraint, an argument that doesn't hold up to 5th grade reading level of this resolution—all to avoid they obvious truth: they’re using NDP to circumvent democratic accountability to purchase highly controversial surveillance tech they knew the community would not approve.

That’s not oversight. That’s an end-run around Council, dressed up in the language of responsible governance, and they think you're too stupid or scared of them to tell the difference. If only there was a word for when you present information in a misleading way…
