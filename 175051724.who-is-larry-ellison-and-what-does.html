The Road to Nashville, Or:
What’s so wrong with Texas?

For nearly fifty years Oracle was based in California, home to the strongest consumer privacy protections in the U.S. In 2020, Oracle left for Austin, Texas. On paper, Texas looked perfect: low taxes, pro-business politics, a libertarian vibe.

In 2023, Texas and Tennessee both passed consumer protection laws that directly impacted Oracle. Texas adopted Attorney General-only enforcement (no private lawsuits) and a 30-day cure period before action. Importantly, Texas required companies to comply with formal legal process—subpoenas, court orders, official investigations—before sharing data with law enforcement. It was pretty lenient.

But Tennessee, never to be outdone in lack of oversight, said “hold my whiskey.”

In 2023, Governor Bill Lee signed the Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA). No single provision made Tennessee unique—Virginia, Colorado, and other states had adopted similar language in various forms. But Tennessee assembled them into the extraordinarily permissive combination available:

  • Banned private lawsuits entirely

  • 60-day grace period before enforcement—the 2nd longest in the nation

  • An “affirmative defense” for companies claiming compliance with voluntary standards like NIST

  • A processor/controller split borrowed from GDPR but stripped of meaningful oversight

  • And critically: carve-outs allowing data sharing with law enforcement based on a company’s own “reasonable and good faith belief” rather than formal legal process

Individually, none of these provisions were unprecedented. Together, they created something new: a regulatory environment where a data processor could operate with maximum autonomy and minimum accountability.

The Processor Loophole

TIPA distinguishes between data “controllers”— e.g. hospitals, retailers, insurers who interact with consumers—and “processors”—infrastructure vendors like Oracle who run the databases and cloud systems. Controllers carry consumer-facing responsibilities. Processors get narrower duties and more protection.

Oracle specifically identifies itself in customer facing documents with this exact framing. As per its “Data Processing Agreement for Oracle Services” dated August 14, 2025, “You are a Controller and Oracle is a Processor for the Processing of Personal Information as part of the provision of the Services.”

When TN was discussing TIPA in House and Senate hearings, Oracle’s own trade group, BSA | The Software Alliance (where it holds a board seat), submitted letters asking for exactly this type of terminology. BSA | Software Alliance submitted this letter of support for his legislation, stating that it succeeded in: “recognizing the unique role of data processors and creating privacy protections that are interoperable with other state laws.”

Privacy scholars call the stark separation between “controllers” and “processors” a “protection gap.” It mirrors language used in EU data regulating GDPR language, but often lacks the teeth of the EU standards.

In practice, ‘data processors’ aren’t powerless bystanders. They can, intentionally or not, often read data, share it, analyze it, even delete or corrupt it. But in the way laws like the Tennessee Information Protection Act frames it, they’re couched as just sitting in the background doing what the “data controller tell them to do.” As we’ll explore in a moment, they may do much more than that.

Tennessee didn’t invent any of these phrases or legal clauses. But whoever wrote our legislation seems to have cherry-picked the most business-friendly version of each one and assembled them into a package that gave data processors unprecedented latitude.

Law Enforcement Collaboration

Oracle wasn’t just moving offices. It was assembling the precise regulatory conditions under which a data processor could operate with maximum autonomy.

And in Nashville, it found them.

Tucked into the fine print1 of TIPA is something extraordinary: data controllers and processors may “cooperate with law enforcement agencies” whenever they believe the data they store might demonstrate unlawful activity. But actually, it’s not just “unlawful” behavior that entitles them to collaborate with law enforcement. The precise wording is: “This part does not restrict a controller’s or processor’s ability to:”

(3) Cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations;

TIPA meticulously defines 30 terms — but leaves “laws, rules, or regulations” completely undefined. Without definition, the phrase becomes infinitely elastic: could federal statutes, municipal ordinances, agency guidelines, tax codes, zoning rules, professional licensing requirements, even internal compliance policies qualify?

In practice, that gives infrastructure vendors extraordinary discretion to share data with law enforcement, even when the controller is the one with consumer-facing obligations. And these data processors have no obligation to let you know they’re doing this.

The contrast between the TN law (above) and the TX law (below) is startling. The carve outs for sharing data outside normal business purposes are virtually identical for the first few bullets — with the exception of number (3). TN specifically gives data controllers and processors (how Oracle likes to designate itself) the right to share data based on reasonable good faith that a law, rule or regulation has been broken.

Texas requires formal legal process: “comply with a civil, criminal, or regulatory inquiry, investigation, subpoena, or summons.”

Tennessee grants autonomous discretion: processors can “cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations.”

TIPA formally enshrines plausible deniability. Processors claim passivity for accountability—”we just follow the controller’s instructions”—while exercising agency for law enforcement—”we reasonably believed this data might be relevant.” The processor role becomes whatever is most convenient: passive infrastructure when liability looms, active partner when they decide it is warranted.

Oracle’s Intelligence DNA

Oracle takes its name from a 1977 CIA project codename, and the CIA was Oracle’s first customer. From these beginnings, Oracle databases spread through U.S. defense and intelligence systems. By 2003, the federal government accounted for 23 percent of Oracle’s licensing revenue, roughly $2.5 billion annually. Today, according to Orcale’s marketing materials, 1,000+ public sector organizations and 100% of federal cabinet agencies rely on Oracle systems.

The company now provides accredited cloud regions usable for Top Secret and Sensitive Compartmented Information. Through the Pentagon’s “Golden Dome for America” initiative—described as a “digital shield”—Oracle delivers AI-enabled infrastructure for intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance, as well as command and control operations on active battlefields.

After 9/11, Ellison called for a national identification card to be issued to all U.S. citizens and offered to give the necessary software to the U.S. government for free. Ellison argued that we needed just one large national security database, one with national ID cards and mandatory iris scans, writing that:

“The single greatest step we Americans could take to make life tougher for terrorists would be to ensure that all the information in myriad government databases was copied into a single, comprehensive national security database.”

Most controversially, Oracle became deeply embedded in China’s surveillance apparatus. By the mid-2010s, Oracle’s database and enterprise management tools were ubiquitous across Chinese government agencies and state-owned enterprises, forming a critical backbone of official IT systems. Oracle’s technology helped power the internet surveillance project Golden Shield, along with China’s grid management system, a network of neighborhood-level social control.

Oracle in Xinjiang

Independent reporting and human rights investigations show that Chinese public-security systems in Xinjiang incorporated technologies from a range of global vendors, including Oracle.

According to procurement records and leaked databases, Oracle software was used in policing systems in Xinjiang alongside products from other U.S. companies. Company records also show that Oracle-certified hardware — localized Exadata servers marketed in China as Dengyun servers — was purchased by Beijing public-security bureaus for “smart policing” projects (Georgetown CSET). Oracle itself acknowledged these Dengyun servers as localized versions of its flagship hardware in marketing materials.

Human Rights Watch’s reverse engineering of the Integrated Joint Operations Platform (IJOP) — a core policing app used in Xinjiang — found that the system aggregated vast categories of personal data (facial images, license plate numbers, travel histories, financial transactions) and flagged entirely lawful behaviors as suspicious, such as entering a home through the back door or consuming unusual amounts of electricity. Financial documents for the related Police Cloud program described monitoring “people of certain ethnicity,” explicitly including Uyghurs from South Xinjiang (Human Rights Watch / ecoi.net).

Oracle’s Defense re: China

When confronted with evidence that its software was being used inside China’s security state, Oracle’s defense hinged on a familiar distinction: it claimed to provide only the tools, not the policing itself. Oracle spokesperson Jessica Moore told The Intercept that the company’s materials were merely demonstrations of possibility — “what our products could do if others built on top of them” — and insisted they were “aspirational business development ideas” that “do not indicate any targeted or intended sales/support execution.”

Moore pressed this point further, saying that such activities “would be considered inconsistent with Oracle’s core Corporate Citizenship Values, including our Human Rights statement.” Asked directly about slides showing police applications, she doubled down: “We have no known implementation with a ‘Chinese Police Department’ … the definition of ‘use case’ is very different than an actual, implementable product or service, which Oracle does not have.”

Even where Oracle acknowledged ties, it was careful to limit the admission. The company conceded “limited authorized transactions with Xinjiang Public Security Bureau from 2011–2019,” but emphasized that it had not done business with the bureau since U.S. sanctions took effect. The message was consistent: Oracle may have marketed, demonstrated, and even sold its products, but it was not accountable for how they were applied by Chinese security services.

The “Processor” Defense In Real Life

This line of argument echoes almost perfectly the processor vs. controller framework baked into U.S. privacy laws. Under statutes like Tennessee’s Information Protection Act, a processor can claim it does not determine the “purpose and means” of data use — and therefore cannot be held responsible for outcomes. That burden falls on the controller. At the same time, Tennessee’s law explicitly allows processors to “cooperate with law enforcement” in good faith, creating space for them to hand over data when it suits their interests.

In China, Oracle claimed it was “just the platform,” disclaiming responsibility for surveillance outcomes. In Tennessee, the law now formalizes that very same shield: Oracle can both deny accountability to consumers and exercise discretion in sharing data with the state. In both settings, the company is able to present itself as neutral infrastructure, while still profiting from the uses its technology makes possible.

Coming Home To Roost

We have already seen what the collaboration of what companies like Oracle and state intelligence services looks like abroad.

In China, Oracle insisted it was only the processor, not the controller, as its databases and analytics were layered into policing platforms that targeted dissidents and disappeared Uyghurs into camps. In Tennessee, that same shield has now been written into law. Oracle can disclaim responsibility for how its systems are used, while still marketing their power to anyone with the budget and the will to build on top of them.

Which brings us to Nashville. What, precisely, do we think this will look like? What tools will be used to wage the “war on cities” the actual President has promised — with, we can only assume, the Shadow President of Silicon Valley providing the technical backbone?

The regulatory framework Tennessee has adopted effectively ensures that neither controllers nor processors face meaningful limits when it comes to law enforcement and intelligence access. By explicitly carving out exemptions that allow both parties to “comply” or “cooperate” with authorities outside of ordinary oversight, the law constructs a permissive environment where data flows toward state actors with little accountability. In practice, this makes it nearly impossible to erect safeguards against the emergence of a domestic surveillance state.

China has already provided the test run. There, Oracle and other Western firms presented themselves as neutral “platform providers” — distancing themselves from how police or security agencies deployed their tools — while actively marketing those very tools for predictive policing and repression. Tennessee’s statutory carve-outs replicate that same logic at the legal level: processors can disclaim responsibility while still facilitating the surveillance demands of government. The result is a regulatory structure that functionally endorses surveillance by default, making the construction of a broad, state-aligned data monitoring regime not just possible but almost inevitable.

Many people will say: “I’m not gonna knock someone for selling tools to people in another country. It’s not their fault what they do with those tools. And yeah, that’s what happens in an authoritarian state like China. But this is America.”

But, what, exactly, are we claiming could never happen here?
