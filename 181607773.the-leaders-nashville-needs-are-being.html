THREE OF NASHVILLE’S YOUNG LEADERS
have been highlighted in major ways recently for their contributions to a new wave of organizing and designing a city we want to stay in.

You’ll note I’m not one to give out praise to leaders out of politeness (I’d be much more employable if I was). When it comes to giving positive feedback to leaders, I am much more of the Hollywood Handshake type of praise giver: it’s rare, but I mean it all the more. Here are some folks who did some damn good work in 2025, and I’m not the first to notice it.

Cathy Carillo: Nashville Banner Best of 2025

The Nashville Banner highlighted Cathy Carillo today in its Best of 2025, reposting an August podcast interview it did with the former Metro employee now prominent immigration rights activist.

Kelly Chieng - Rising Advocate Award Winner

Kelly Chieng received the Rising Advocate Award from Tennessee United for Human Rights (TUHR) for her fearless grassroots advocacy for Nashville’s most vulnerable communities.

TUHR’s statement notes that:

Kelly Chieng is a dedicated advocate for Nashville’s homeless and immigrant communities, providing direct outreach and raising emergency housing funds for those in crisis. She is also a fearless voice at Metro Council, pushing for pedestrian safety and public transit improvements to protect the city’s most vulnerable residents.

Sean Parker: Nashville’s Municipal Socialist Goes National

In an issue riding the wave of Zohran Mamdani’s stunning NYC mayoral victory, Jacobin magazine profiled the 100+ democratic socialists now holding elected office across America—including Nashville’s own Sean Parker.

The magazine credits Nashville’s emerging openness to real housing policy not to the depoliticized technocratic management that characterizes our current Mayor’s Office, but to actual political struggle led by the left. While establishment Democrats work overtime to take the polity—the community of citizens—out of politics, Jacobin shows how precisely those kinds of fights, like the “stadium boondoggle” Parker vocally opposed, bring people back into public life and build the solidarity needed to make Nashville, and all of America, a place where people want to and can stay.

Three Nashville Leaders Show How We Rise All Boats

Three of Nashville’s young organizers have been recognized by prominent organizations for embodying a new approach to politics—one that transcends stale partisan categories by focusing on what actually works.

Two of these three hold no official titles or explicit power. Yet they’re taking up the space the times are giving them—listening to what’s needed and acting. They’ve taken the best of what our culture values right now—the ability to flexibly iterate, communicate quickly, explain ourselves clearly—and they’re using it for good over profit, truth over confusion, solidarity over domination.

It’s the kind of message that is resonating, kitchen table ideas like: feeding kids, fixing roads, funding hospitals. In 2025, that means: house the homeless, protect immigrants, make streets safe to walk, make buses accessible, stop developer giveaways. These aren’t “leftist” demands when you strip away the electoralist packaging. They’re the simple building blocks of communities where people want to stay.

The times are making Tennessee. And Tennessee is learning that what binds us isn’t partisan—it’s the kitchen table basics that let us help each other thrive. What these three leaders show is that far more important that what we accomplish politically is how we get there — and making decisions collectively, transparently, keeping the “demos” (the “people”) in democracy, that is the only way we make Nashville, and America, a place we can, and want to, stay.
