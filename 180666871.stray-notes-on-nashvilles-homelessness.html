The Structure Raises Questions

OHS operates with unprecedented consolidated authority. It serves as:

  • Collaborative Applicant for the Continuum of Care (transferred from MDHA in November 2023)

  • HMIS Lead Agency (managing the data system since 2018)

  • Coordinated Entry Lead (controlling access to housing resources)

  • Staff support provider to the Homelessness Planning Council

The mayor appears to have direct hire/fire authority over the OHS director with no independent board providing oversight. The HPC—which might be expected to provide governance—functions in what appears to be more of an advisory capacity.

HPC Chair Kennetha Patterson stated publicly in 2025: “It feels like, to be honest, April Calvin has been operating as the chair of the Homeless Planning Council.”

What’s unclear:

  • Whether this power consolidation was an intentional part of O’Connell’s original legislation or emerged during implementation

  • How this structure compares to national best practices for CoC governance

  • Whether having the same office control data, entry, funding applications, and performance reporting creates accountability gaps

  • What percentage of Continuum of Care organizations nationally operate with this level of consolidation

Key Questions:

  • Did Koch’s study recommend the specific governance structure that was ultimately implemented?

  • Was O’Connell involved in drafting or sponsoring the June 2022 OHS legislation that followed Koch’s May presentation?

  • Was the consolidated power structure (mayor’s direct authority, no independent oversight board) part of Koch’s recommendations?

  • How does this compare to the previous Metro Homeless Impact Division under Metro Social Services?

  • What accountability mechanisms exist when one office controls all aspects of the system?

Atlanta’s Model - A Concerning Track Record

What research shows about Atlanta’s approach:

According to a May 2025 case study by the National Alliance to End Homelessness examining Atlanta’s homelessness response:

  • Unsheltered homelessness “fluctuated on an upward trajectory, climbing to over 1,000 people” as of January 2024

  • The share experiencing chronic homelessness grew from 17% in 2020 to 29% in 2024

  • 62% of people with chronic homelessness were unsheltered in 2024, up from 54% in 2020

The study notes Atlanta’s approach emphasized “reducing street homelessness”—which some advocates distinguish from actually housing people—and relied heavily on its Downtown Improvement District for enforcement and “quality of life” responses.

What this might suggest for Nashville:

Nashville appears to be implementing structural elements similar to Atlanta’s approach:

  • Consolidated government control of CoC functions

  • Business Improvement District involvement in enforcement

  • Encampment closure strategies that appear timed to development interests

  • Emphasis on coordination and system improvement alongside enforcement

Nashville’s chronic homelessness reportedly increased 43% from March 2023 to March 2024—a trajectory that some observers note parallels Atlanta’s documented struggles.

Questions worth examining:

  • Did Koch recommend importing specific elements of Atlanta’s approach?

  • Was Atlanta’s track record discussed during the planning process?

  • What aspects of the $500,000 study specifically came from Atlanta’s experience?

  • Are the similar outcomes coincidental or predictable based on similar structures?

Source: National Alliance to End Homelessness - “Housing-Focused Responses to Unsheltered Homelessness: Spotlight on Atlanta, GA” (May 2025)

The PSH Program Results - Questions About Effectiveness

Metro Resolution RS 2022-1696 reportedly appropriated $25 million in ARPA funds for Permanent Supportive Housing gap financing.

According to available documentation:

  • $24.17 million awarded to developers

  • $830,879 (5%) to MDHA as administrative fee

  • Units produced: 124 PSH units + 58 additional units at 0-30% AMI = 182 total

A critical question: Units were allegedly created by converting planned LIHTC units to deeper affordability—described in some analysis as “not a net addition to the stock.” If accurate, this would mean developers accessed COVID funding by converting units they were already planning to build rather than creating new capacity.

Cost per unit appears to be $163,000-$195,000 (which may be reasonable for gap financing compared to $350K-$850K for full PSH development nationally)

But Nashville’s United Housing Strategy calls for 900 PSH units annually. The program reportedly produced 124-182 units over 2+ years—significantly short of the stated goal.

And despite this $25M investment as part of the broader $50M package, chronic homelessness reportedly grew 43%.

Key Questions:

  • Did the program actually add PSH capacity, or just access COVID funds for unit conversions?

  • Who approved this approach and evaluated its effectiveness?

  • Is this outcome acceptable for $25 million in public investment?

  • What changes are planned to actually meet the 900 units/year goal?

Sources: Metro Council Resolution RS 2022-1696; MDHA program documentation; United Housing Strategy

Section 9: Trying to Understand the Bigger Picture

Is This System Working As Designed?

This is perhaps the most uncomfortable question to ask: Could Nashville’s homelessness system be producing outcomes consistent with its actual design, even if those outcomes differ from its stated purpose?

What the system appears to do effectively:

  • Remove visible homelessness from areas targeted for development

  • Empower business improvement district enforcement

  • Consolidate control without independent oversight structures

  • Create financial arrangements that appear to benefit private developers

  • Maintain an appearance of action through increased funding and placement statistics

  • Avoid clear accountability through complexity and limited transparency

What the system doesn’t appear to do effectively:

  • Reduce chronic homelessness (reportedly up 43%)

  • Create adequate permanent supportive housing (124-182 units vs 900/year documented need)

  • Maintain proper financial controls (alleged $3.2M irregularities, apparent $86K procurement issue)

  • Operate with full transparency (alleged shadow programs, missing documentation, questioned fund usage)

  • Follow through on commitments to vulnerable families (alleged voucher promises unfulfilled)

The uncomfortable question: Is this continuous operational struggle, or might the system be producing its intended outcomes—just not the ones being publicly described?

Is There a Template Being Applied?

The Atlanta connection raises questions about whether Nashville imported a specific model:

Structural elements that appear similar between Atlanta and Nashville:

  • Consolidated government control of CoC functions

  • Business Improvement District involvement in enforcement activities

  • Emphasis on “reducing street homelessness” (visibility management) rather than housing outcomes

  • Encampment closure strategies that appear timed to development interests

  • Heavy reliance on enforcement and “quality of life” response mechanisms

Atlanta’s outcomes might predict Nashville’s trajectory:

  • Unsheltered homelessness climbing to over 1,000 people (Jan 2024)

  • Chronic homelessness share growing: 29% in 2024, up from 17% in 2020

  • 62% of chronically homeless unsheltered in 2024, up from 54% in 2020

Nashville already shows similar patterns: chronic homelessness reportedly up 43%, enforcement through business improvement district contracting, encampment closures that appear aligned with development timelines rather than housing availability.

Key Questions:

  • Did Koch recommend importing specific elements of Atlanta’s approach?

  • Were Atlanta’s documented struggles discussed during planning?

  • Is Nashville implementing a proven model, or replicating proven challenges?

  • What does research say about consolidated government control of CoCs without independent oversight?

Source: National Alliance to End Homelessness - Atlanta case study (May 2025)

Reform or Continuity?

Documented patterns under O’Connell’s administration:

  • Consolidated control creating potential conflicts of interest

  • Alleged $3.2M in financial irregularities without apparent public accountability

  • Reported 43% increase in chronic homelessness despite 340% funding increase

  • Encampment closures that appear timed to development interests

  • NDP’s alleged $40+ million in potentially unauthorized funding

  • NDP’s reported enforcement role (200+ arrests, “profiling is what it is”)

  • Retention of Cooper-era administrators

  • Atlanta connection importing what appears to be a similar approach

  • Expansion of allegedly failed programs

The core question: What measurable outcomes distinguish O’Connell’s administration from Cooper administration practices?

The evidence available suggests O’Connell was involved in the system’s creation—he sponsored Koch’s $500,000 grant contract, and legislation implementing what appears to be Koch’s recommendations followed one month later. Whether he also sponsored or drafted that legislation isn’t clear from public records. Koch had undisclosed professional ties to Wilson, whom O’Connell retained when he became mayor. He continues to defend the system despite what appear to be significant documented challenges.
