Oracle Eyes Nashville — but why?

When Oracle announced in April 2024 that it would relocate its global headquarters to Nashville, headlines focused on jobs, subsidies, and the East Bank project. But this relocation is not just about real estate or economic growth. Oracle moved to Austin in 2020 after nearly five decades in California. Something is prompting the quick relocation.

To my eye, the move has less to do with our wonderful skyline and more to do with Larry Ellison’s half-century obsession with control through data.

At the World Governments Summit in Dubai in February 2025, Ellison told Tony Blair:

“I have to tell [the] AI model as much about my country as I can. We need to unify all the national data, put it into a database where it’s easily consumable by the AI model, and then ask whatever question you like. That’s the missing link.”
Fortune, Feb 14, 2025

And in a 2024 financial analyst meeting, he made the social stakes clear:

“We’re going to have supervision. Every police officer is going to be supervised at all times, and if there’s a problem, AI will report that problem and report it to the appropriate person. Citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on.”

Fortune, Sept 17, 2024

These are not offhand comments. They are the blueprint for the data citadel Oracle is now building in Tennessee. And with tax rebates and glowing press releases and generous rezoning, we’ve been rolling out the red carpet for them.

The Road to Nashville

Oracle’s move to Tennessee unfolded over years:

  • 2021: Oracle announced an operations hub in Nashville, promising 8,500 jobs and a $1.35 billion campus. Metro Council approved $175 million in incentives; the state added another $65 million.

  • 2022: Oracle acquired Cerner for $28.3 billion, taking over vast troves of U.S. health records, including VA and DoD contracts.

  • 2023: Tennessee passed the Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA), considered one of the weakest privacy laws in the country. Presented as a consumer protection bill protecting against data misuse by corporations, it functions more to protect corporations from their consumers. It bans private lawsuits, grants safe harbor to companies using bare minimum standards, and blocks local governments from enacting stronger rules. While Oracle didn’t appear to write or directly lobby for the bill, it belongs to TechNet, the trade group that promoted similar laws nationwide.

    • TIPA, like other data protection laws, does not apply to HIPPA processors (leaving Federal law to oversee that). But it likely would apply to all other data processing operations of Oracle if the company can argue its operations are in Tennessee.

    • This is a highly beneficial regulatory environment for Oracle, who has previously been sued for selling user data. A 2022 class action lawsuit was filed on behalf of 5 billion users. As per TechForge:

  • 2024 (April): Oracle declared Nashville to be its future global headquarters, citing the city’s healthcare industry.

  • 2025 (July): TIPA took effect. This same summer, Oracle suffered major health-record breaches exposing hundreds of thousands of patients meant to be protected under federal HIPPA regulations.

  • 2025 (September): Oracle is part of a consortium acquiring TikTok U.S., and will reportedly be in control of its algorithm, gaining a direct channel into the daily feeds of 170 million Americans. Nashville would, presumably, now the the HQ for the greatest competition to Meta (formerly Facebook).

The sequence is telling: Oracle plants a flag, Tennessee implements a consumer privacy law that protections corporations from its consumers, Ellison elevates Nashville to HQ, and Oracle expands into social media. Each step builds the citadel. A Tower of Babel, an Eye of Sauron, on the East Bank of the Cumberland.

Oracle’s Intelligence DNA

Oracle takes its name from a 1977 CIA project codename, and the CIA was Oracle’s first customer. From these beginnings, Oracle databases spread through U.S. defense and intelligence systems. By 2003, the federal government accounted for 23 percent of Oracle’s licensing revenue, roughly $2.5 billion annually. Today, 1,000+ public sector organizations and 100% of federal cabinet agencies rely on Oracle systems.

The company now provides accredited cloud regions usable for Top Secret andSensitive Compartmented Information, including air-gapped National Security Regions. Through the Pentagon’s “Golden Dome for America” initiative—described as a “digital shield”—Oracle delivers AI-enabled infrastructure for intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance, as well as command and control operations spanning from U.S. territory to foreign deployments to active battlefields.

After 9/11, Ellison called for a national identification card to be issued to all U.S. citizens and offered to give the necessary software to the U.S. government for free. Ellison argued that we needed just one large national security database, one with national ID cards and mandatory iris scans, writing that “The single greatest step we Americans could take to make life tougher for terrorists would be to ensure that all the information in myriad government databases was copied into a single, comprehensive national security database.”

Oracle’s reach extends globally. The company is among the awardees on two massive enterprise cloud contracts: the Department of Defense’s Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability and the CIA’s Commercial Cloud Enterprise. NATO’s Communications and Information Agency (NCIA) moved its mission-critical workloads to Oracle Cloud Infrastructure.

Most controversially, Oracle became deeply embedded in China’s surveillance apparatus. By the mid-2010s, Oracle’s database and enterprise management tools wereubiquitous across Chinese government agencies and state-owned enterprises, forming a critical backbone of official IT systems. Oracle’s technology helped power the internet surveillance project Golden Shield, along with China’s grid management system, a network of neighborhood-level social control.

In Xinjiang, Oracle’s technology aided what may be the most dystopian surveillance campaign on Earth, with internal documents revealing that the company’s data analytics and security software were used by Xinjiang police to process facial recognition images, license plate numbers, DNA profiles, and religious activity logs. In 2018—the same year reports of mass detention in Xinjiang began to surface—Oracle crowned Digital China its global “Partner of the Year,” just after Digital China helped implement a $10 million surveillance system for Chinese authorities.

Ellison’s Psychology: From Abandonment to Control

Ellison’s fixation with control has roots in childhood trauma. Born in 1944, he was given up for adoption and raised in Chicago by a harsh adoptive father and a doting mother who died in his adolescence. At age twelve, he learned the truth of his adoption, a revelation he later said scarred him deeply.

Biographers argue this wound forged his obsession with domination. As one colleague observed: “Ellison always needs an enemy.”

Unlike Steve Jobs, who obsessed over design, Ellison built his empire on data centralization.

It’s not the process at the center, it’s the data. It’s the ‘information age.’ … All your customer information is in one place. All your product information’s in one place. All your applications share the same data. If you draw a picture of Oracle’s application architecture, the database is at the center and the applications are attached around the periphery. This is a radically different view of the world. … A single global database connected to a single global network—the Internet—is the ideal architecture.”

(Softwar, pp 62)

Softwar captures his core philosophy. Centralize the data. Turn it all into static 1s and 0s. Not just for the sake of building a new Library of Alexandria. It’s about who controls it. This is how Ellison put it on a recent earnings call:

Most of the world’s most valuable data is stored in an Oracle database,” Ellison said on the call, “and all of those databases are moving to the cloud — Oracle’s cloud, Microsoft’s Azure cloud, Amazon’s cloud, or Google’s cloud — and Oracle runs everywhere.”

CloudWars.com

The point is not that data, now accessible anywhere via the cloud, is convenient. It’s that once it is centralized, it can be used. It can be processed. It can make us safe against the slings and arrows that cause us fear and trembling. And god kings should never fear anything nor tremble before anyone.

At Oracle’s financial analyst meeting in September 2024, he envisioned a world of total recording:

“We’re going to have supervision. Every police officer is going to be supervised at all times, and if there’s a problem, AI will report that problem and report it to the appropriate person. Citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on.”
Fortune

For Ellison, data isn’t just about business. It is about using it to shape a society that makes him feel safe.

But for someone who Trump administration officials are calling “the shadow president of the United States”, whatever makes Ellison feel safe may not align with what makes the rest of us feel that way.

Tennessee as a Techno-Authoritarian Test Bed

Tennessee cities are building extensive law-enforcement surveillance systems while courting big-tech investment. While Nashville’s Metro Council authorized automated license plate readers in 2023, implementation has stalled due to political opposition. Meanwhile, surrounding cities like La Vergne and Mt. Juliet have moved forward with LPR networks that feed vehicle data into shared investigative databases. A January 2025 investigation revealed a security lapse in Motorola’s systems that exposed live video and vehicle tracking nationwide — including potential feeds from Tennessee — highlighting the risks of rapid surveillance rollouts.

At the state level, the Tennessee Fusion Center integrates intelligence from local, state, and federal partners, while Memphis operates the long-running SkyCop camera program with over 2,100 surveillance units streaming to a Real-Time Crime Center. These infrastructures illustrate the blurring line between public policing and private surveillance technology, with Tennessee’s patchwork of local systems creating an uneven but expanding surveillance landscape across the state.As scholars of surveillance warn,

“Big Tech companies have commodified personal data for profit … using data to predict what people will do … and to modify the behaviors of unknowing consumers. According to critics, this erodes individual dignity and freedom while also threatening democracy.” (Alshamy, Coyne, Hall, & Owens, 2024). When paired with government surveillance, the risks intensify: “The surveillance state differs from surveillance capitalism in that data collection is often coercive, nonconsensual, and insulated from market pressures.”

Oracle’s Nashville deal epitomizes this convergence. Metro and state officials awarded $240 million in direct incentives — $175 million from Nashville and $65 million from the state — for what became Tennessee’s largest economic development deal in history. The company promised 8,500 jobs and $1.2 billion in capital investment by 2031. Yet by 2025, only about 700 of those promised jobs had materialized, and despite nearly four years passing since the incentives were approved, no campus construction had begun. The project, initially budgeted at $1.35 billion, has since ballooned to an estimated $2 billion, though Oracle continues expanding its Nashville presence through office subleases while its East Bank headquarters remains stalled.

At the same time, the Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA) — effective July 1, 2025 — bars private lawsuits, leaves enforcement to the Attorney General, and provides a safe-harbor defense for corporations that follow NIST standards. Analysts describe it as one of the nation’s weakest privacy laws, leaving Tennesseans with virtually no recourse against misuse of their personal data.

As one of the world’s largest enterprise software companies, Oracle occupies a unique position in the data ecosystem—managing vast databases for both corporate clients and government agencies, including sensitive contracts with defense and intelligence organizations. The company’s deep integration with government infrastructure makes it a prime example of what researchers Alshamy et al. (2024) describe as the “parasitic” relationship between private tech firms and the surveillance state, where “private data firms become purposeful or unwitting appendages of the national-security surveillance state.”

Tennessee’s growing reputation as a hub for both healthcare data (with its massive hospital systems) and defense contractors, combined with its business-friendly regulatory environment, creates an ecosystem where the boundaries between private-sector data collection and government surveillance capabilities become increasingly porous. This geographic consolidation of tech and government interests raises critical questions about accountability, oversight, and the concentration of surveillance power that the academic literature warns could “exacerbate the potential for harming individuals in ways that exchanges between surveillance capitalists in purely private exchange cannot.”

Oracle Health and the VA: A Warning

Ellison promised the Cerner acquisition would revolutionize healthcare. Instead, it became a cautionary tale.

At the VA, the Oracle-Cerner system caused outages, lost orders, and patient harm. The VA Inspector General tied the failures to delayed treatments and even deaths. By 2025, Oracle had lost dozens of hospital clients, with many defecting to Epic and MEDITECH.

Even in Nashville, HCA and Vanderbilt avoided Oracle’s systems, relying on Epic instead.

This is the paradox: Oracle markets itself as a guarantor of stability, but its record shows centralization can magnify risks — especially under weak oversight.
