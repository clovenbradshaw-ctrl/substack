A request for comment on questions regarding a potential contract for the Axon Fusus survenillance system, submitted to Metro Legal, MNPD, the Legal Office for Metro Council, The Nashville Metro Mayors Office, or Representatives from Axon Corporate, remains unanswered and unacknowledged for over 36 hours — as of 10 AM on Friday March 7, 2025.

This despite all of Metro Council being CC’d, and a handful of Council Members reaching out saying they would attempt to help obtain answers. I had several phone calls with them as well, validating my efforts to get straight answers on these things.

What I see in many of Nashville local elected officials is fear — fear of voicing their concerns, of being labelled as “anti-safety”, as being on the “wrong side” the Administration.

Fear of being sidelined for the 1 out of 10 times they dissented, instead of getting credit for the 9/10 times they supported.

Fear that policy trajectories that are likely to long outlast any terms limits are being made not based on what wins Nashville the best possible future, but rather what prevents the administration from ever appearing that is losing.

There is, I gather, a fear that an all too familiar pedestrian gamesmanship that infects politics is the motive force behind many otherwise confounding decisions. The game of politics where governing is secondary to the chief occupation of power — to be the ones that does, and not the one that receives, the (pardon my beltway talk) ratfucking.

Not exactly the conditions to have calm and collected conversations about the delicate balance of data-sharing protocols between residents, global corporations, and local law enforcement — and any potential third parties that might slip into that via the fine details of those pesky terms and conditions.

My “Role”

I talk to my therapist a lot about why I do things like this. It’s not for the pocket change from Substack subscribers who choose to donate despite no additional perks (though you are much appreciated, share with friends!).

I definitely have some compulsive sense of “duty” rooted in something I should work on, which has not a small dollop of savior complex mixed in there. It gets especially hairy for me when my compulsions to try to stop something bad from happening align with something that could very, very reasonably go very bad.

As I discussed with a Council Member yesterday, this situation could very, very easily go bad. And only under very precise circumstances go well. My intuition is that this is not a full-blown Dr. Stephen Strange 1 / 14,000,605 change of success. But the odds are not stacked in our favor that we won’t regret expanding surveillance tech in 2025.

The thing is, it’s helpful to ask questions for Metro Council sometimes.

TN regulations limit their ability to collaborate outside of noticed meetings. They can’t really just “talk it through” offline and come back into Committe and Council meetings deeply informed. And the folks with the most time for coffee and sharing of brochures and branded pens that can answer their questions are the registered Metro Nashville lobbyists there who are meant to steer them, not educate them.

So collectively asking questions relevant extraordinarily important decisions like “should we expand surveillance technology in an era of unprecedented suspension of rule of law” seems… like a no-brainer to me. Even though I know stuff like this is demonstrably hurting my employability in any local industry that needs to be on speaking terms with The Mayor’s Office.

I am, undoubtedly, very annoying to my former employer, the government of Metro Nashville-Davidson County. Last year, I got very up in their grill regarding the de-wokeification of Metro Arts (you have to give them credit — they were ahead of the curve on that one).

I would like to think of myself as a Socrates-like figure, like a gadfly. Arousing and reproaching the state so as to improve it through dialectical questioning — awakening the deeper virtues of our collective natures which lie chained in darkness to the cave walls of our unacknowledged ignorance.

But my long emails with scores of “CC” recipients probably are come off less as welcome Socratic dialogue, and more like that chick at the school assembly in Mean Girls.

As readers have seen,

for the past month or so, I’ve really been on one about one particular necrotic boomerang of a Council resolution—the proposed contract to expand Axon’s Fusus surveillance product from a hiatused trial run (implemented by MNPD outside of procurement rules), into yet another real world example of a people taking the wrong lessons from Batman.

The intense discussions online and in Council Meetings have been mostly about the oversight of MNPD and what they do with the data they receive from resident-owned “donor” cameras. Tuesday’s Council meeting focused on implementing guardrails for how MNPD could use any cameras that are integrated into Axon’s Fusus system if Metro were to approve one.

But there has not been, in my opinion, sufficient discussion about the 360 degrees of this proposed system.

There’s been a lot of product marketing fluff thrown around about this system. And a lot of it has found itself on to .gov websites in a way that may well warrant a hard look, if not formal inquiries. But really, we just need to get to brass tax. What the hell really is this thing they trying to sell us on?

I’ve latched onto that sexiest of document types, the “Terms and Conditions Agreements” for the various relationships at work in this proposed system. What was clear to me is that there are three relationships in this system, and I could only find the terms for two of those three.

Relationship 1: Axon ←→ Metro Nashville Government
Relationship 2: Metro Nashville Government ←→ Donor Camera Providers
Relationship 3: Donor Camera Providers ←→ Axon

The data-sharing terms for Relationship 1 are pretty clear from the contract as it was last presented to Council, developed in the no-bid contract between Axon and Metro Legal and MNPD.

The data-sharing terms for Relationship 2 are pretty clear from the templatized terms and conditions agreement that Axon appears to provide for police departments to give to residents who provide donor cameras.1 Axon generously crafts these agreements to ensure that law enforcement faces zero legal consequences—not just for any harm caused by their actions after accessing donor camera footage, but also for choosing to do nothing with what they see.

The data-sharing terms for Relationship 3 have escaped by clutches. It is unclear what rights, control, ownership, data-sharing, data reselling, etc Axon has over the footage sent to it via donor cameras throughout a major metropolitan city.

This is a rather BIG, and perhaps most important, piece of this puzzle. And as I asked Axon and Metro, if you just have this document, send it my way. But as I said — crickets.
