In October, 2025, a case manager at the support services provider Step Up’s put in her notice. She turned in a letter explaining her resignation.

The letter was brief, but pointed:

When I was hired, I understood the role to involve providing supportive services for permanently housed individuals. However, since partnering with the Office of Homeless Services, the expectations and responsibilities have shifted significantly. These changes no longer align with the values and principles of social work.

I have witnessed clients repeatedly re-victimized—through late rent payments, 14-day notices being placed on their doors, and not receiving the opportunities that were initially promised to them. Additionally, I was recently asked to perform tasks that I feel conflict with my professional ethics and core values as a social worker.

The letter concluded:

As social workers, we are called to advocate against injustice and support vulnerable populations. I cannot compromise my integrity or professional values in order to accommodate practices that conflict with those principles.

{Rich Text} is redacting the case manager’s name to protect them from potential retaliation, but has obtained the resignation letter, contemporaneous documentation, and recorded interviews detailing the events that led to this resignation.

The Context

Regular readers will recognize the backdrop: What OHS calls “Non-Traditional Rapid Rehousing” or now “MRRF,” the shadow housing program I’ve been documenting where Director April Calvin personally selected families from Nashville Rescue Mission, promised them a year of paid rent and Section 8 vouchers, then systematically failed to deliver.1

In a November 10 response to the Homelessness Planning Council obtained by {Rich Text}, OHS has now stated that these families were not actually housed using the Metro Rapid Response Funding program, but rather using “bridge funding (Emergency Specific Assistance to Individuals)”—emergency assistance funds that OHS diverted to pay rent throughout 2024 and early 2025.

What Changed When OHS Partnered with Step Up

The case manager was hired in June 2024 to provide supportive services to permanently housed individuals. Step Up had assured her they were no longer doing housing placements—a function they’d attempted previously without adequate funding when she was employed with them previously. Now that the scope of work was limited to just providing support, she was happy to return.

At first things went well. And then they didn’t.

According to the case manager’s account in a recorded interview, she suddenly found herself interfacing with property managers at The Avenue and Hillside Crossing about why rent wasn’t being paid. She watched families receive 14-day eviction notices despite being told their rent would be covered for a year. She documented families who complied with everything OHS asked but were denied the vouchers they were promised.

“We argue that three mutually reinforcing processes underlie normalization: (1) institutionalization, where an initial corrupt decision or act becomes embedded in structures and processes and thereby routinized; (2) rationalization, where self-serving ideologies develop to justify and perhaps even valorize corruption; and (3) socialization, where naïve newcomers are induced to view corruption as permissible if not desirable.”

Ashforth, B.E., & Anand, V. (2003). “The Normalization of Corruption in Organizations.” Research in Organizational Behavior

OHS’s November response to the Homelessness Planning Council appears to confirm this arrangement, stating: “There was miscommunication between the landlord and the service provider that agreed to provide the rental assistance. When that happened, OHS stepped in to rectify the situation and paid the landlord to assist the clients in maintaining their housing.”

This claim is false. Documents from April, 2025 under Step Up letterhead state the following in plain fact: “In our partnership with the Office of Homeless Services, Step Up provides supportive wrap-around services while OHS provided funding assistance with housing options for families at Hillside Crossing and The Avenue.”

Financial records obtained by {Rich Text} show OHS was directly paying rent to properties like Hillside Crossing, starting as early as October 2024. For example, an invoice dated October 17, 2024 shows OHS paying $9,584.40 directly to Hillside Crossing LLC. A November payment to Hillside Crossing is even more explicit, with the memo line in the Metro Nashville ledger reading: “INV#INVHILLSIDE112024 NOV 2024 RENT OHS”.

These payments came after OHS had already paid landlord engagement fees (incentive payments to secure the placements). There was no “miscommunication” that required OHS to “step in”— for Hillside Crossing at the very least, financial records indicate that OHS was always the entity making rent payments. They were paying rent nearly half a year before the former Step Up case manager states that her organization was required to break the news to the tenants in the April memo, that OHS would not be making good on their offer of a year of free housing.

According to the case manager, her organization was instructed to represent that the funding the families had been promised was actually “temporary”, even though the leases they had been told to sign by OHS officials were not temporary, but had put them on the hook for 12 months of rent at $1,400 a month. For families that had been moved out of homeless shelters, this was simply not feasible.

Here is the central tension in the case manager’s account: Step Up was contracted only to provide supportive services, yet the case manager suddenly found herself dealing with landlords about unpaid rent. This wasn’t because Step Up had agreed to provide rental assistance and failed—it was because OHS was the one responsible for paying rent, and when those payments were late or didn’t arrive, landlords contacted the case manager. Step Up became entangled in a rental assistance arrangement that was never part of their contract, placing the case manager in a role that extended far beyond her job description or her organization’s contracted scope.

Reviewing the organization’s contract with Metro Nashville (which dates back to the Cooper Administration) the scope of program makes it clear that paying rent and housing navigation were not things that Step Up was tasked with providing.

What is confounding is if OHS was not planning on paying rent, and there was no arrangement for Step Up to pay rent, why on earth were families placed in housing while they were in abject poverty with no reasonable ability to pay $1,400 a month?

If people like the case manager and myself had not reached out proactively to the Mayor’s Office and OHS, all signs indicate that many of these families would have been served legal documents for evictions and summary judgements against them, requiring them to pay the back rent in garnished wages with interest, and making their prospect of finding future housing immeasurably more difficult.

Why would OHS put them in such a situation, without them even asking to be a part of this program (a program which appears to have been completely off the books and does not seem to even have a name)?

The case manager asserts that scope creep was consistently being pushed on her role, with responsibilities for rehousing the families that had lost housing falling onto her to deal with, rather than on OHS, the org retracting their rent promises and had a full-time staff in a low barrier housing collective unit.

These recollections align with reports from other case managers I spoke to at two different organizations who had been placed in housing by OHS. When the housing fell apart, despite OHS being the ones who arranged it, it was now up to these orgs, without the training or connections, to find a new place for the client to live.

Two Families, Two Extraordinary Stories

In summer 2025, two families that were placed in housing by OHS filed complaints with Metro Human Relations Commission. They had voluntarily left their housing when OHS asked them to move and were promised Section 8 vouchers as accommodation. They didn’t receive them—while several other families who hadn’t complied eventually got vouchers (something confirmed by OHS’s statement on Nov 10).

The case manager from Step Up relates that a lawyer from Metro’s Human Relations Commission reached out to the case manager for a statement about what had happened. On July 28, 2025, she’d attended a meeting with OHS staff about these cases. She documented what happened in a contemporaneous statement.

According to that statement, OHS staff told the struggling families they “had 12 months of free rent during which they could have saved” (though they had not, in fact, received 12 months of rent from Metro) and suggested sacrifices these families should make to afford their rent, including one client finding different employment and another stopping homeschooling her daughter so she could work more.

The statement documented that the families were “struggling financially and in need of additional support (Section 8) they had previously been told they would receive.”

OHS’s characterization of these same two families in their November 2025 response tells a very different story. According to that document: “Two clients chose to find their own housing after rental assistance was phased out. They could afford to pay their own rent, which should be celebrated. For this reason, they no longer met HUD’s definition of homelessness and did not qualify for a voucher.”

“The essence of totalitarian government, and perhaps the nature of every bureaucracy, is to make functionaries and mere cogs in the administrative machinery out of men.”

Hannah Arendt

The contradiction is stark: In July, according to the case manager’s contemporaneous documentation, OHS staff were telling these families they needed to find different employment and make sacrifices because they were struggling financially. By November, OHS characterized the same families as comfortably able to afford their rent and choosing to leave voluntarily.

The catch-22 these families faced is also evident: By placing families in permanent housing with leases in their own names (at April Calvin’s direct direction), OHS immediately disqualified them from the homeless-specific vouchers they’d been promised. Once you have permanent housing under HUD definitions, you’re no longer homeless. Once you’re no longer homeless, you can’t access homeless vouchers.

Whether through deliberate design or incompetence, families were trapped in a bureaucratic catch-22 where the help they received prevented them from getting the help they were promised. When families couldn’t afford rent after OHS stopped paying consistently, OHS blamed them for not saving—despite rent that was supposed to be covered by OHS often arriving late or not at all.

The Coordinated Entry Question

OHS’s November response to the Homelessness Planning Council also addresses questions about why families were placed outside the Coordinated Entry system. Their answer: “Dedicated CoC-funded programs are required to receive all referrals through Coordinated Entry. Metro funding has no such requirement. Metro reserves the right to act swiftly on behalf of our citizens, Metro Departments, and district Council Members in case of an emergency.”

While OHS is technically correct that federal regulations only require Coordinated Entry for federally funded programs, this position undermines the purpose of having a coordinated system at all. OHS serves as the Coordinated Entry Lead Agency, responsible for ensuring equitable, prioritized access to homeless services across the community. By claiming the right to bypass that system whenever using local dollars, OHS has created a two-tier system where some families get help through the coordinated process designed for fairness, while others get help based on who can get the director’s attention.

It also completely torpedoes Metro Legal’s persistent argument that the Homelessness Planning Council (HPC) is an extension of Metro Government, and therefore subject to Tennessee Open Meetings Act (TOMA) regulations, which many members of the HPC have said to me are being used to silence dissent and discourage coordination between agencies.

The response also claims OHS “did not hand-pick these individuals,” stating instead that “The Nashville Rescue Mission reached out to the Office of Homeless Services asking for help while in a capacity crisis.”

However, text messages I’ve previously published show Director April Calvin personally reviewing HMIS records via text on a Saturday morning, removing families from consideration based on family size, and approving which specific families would be placed. Whether the initial list came from the Mission or not, Calvin made the final selections—exactly the kind of decision-making Coordinated Entry exists to prevent.

What This Reveals

This resignation letter—just 213 words—documents the moment when a professional social worker chose her integrity over her employment. But combined with OHS’ own admissions in their November response to the HPC, it reveals something larger about how the Office Freddie O’Connell created and directly oversees functions.

OHS now admits the 2024 placements of these families weren’t done through any approved housing program, but rather through diverted “bridge funding” meant for emergency assistance. As someone whose obsessive tendencies have cursed me with being so familiar now with OHS’ finances, likely more so than its Director at this point, if you are not sure what that means, its because it means nothing.

OHS was never allocated money to pay people’s rents beyond initial move-in fees. It’s not a terrible idea for funds to go towards long term rent, but its not a way they were allocated funds in 2024, and MRRF isn’t designed (if we can call it “designed” at all) with the clients’ best interest in mind. Its function is to serve the needs of people who have problems with the unhoused, not the unhoused who have problems.

They admit there was “miscommunication” about rental assistance arrangements involving Step Up. They characterize families as voluntarily leaving and comfortably affording rent—families that their own staff discussed as financially struggling just months earlier.

This is what breaking a system, rather than fixing it, looks like.
