The Basic Facts

Let’s establish some simple facts before we get into the weeds:

The State of Tennessee offered $15,028,698 to the Metropolitan Government of Nashville and Davidson County through the Downtown Public Safety Grant program. The official allocation document from Tennessee’s Department of Finance and Administration makes this clear. The Governor’s announcement explicitly lists “Metropolitan Government of Nashville and Davidson County” as eligible.

The Nashville Downtown Partnership, despite whatever feelings they may have about this, is not part of Metro government. They are a private 501(c)(6) corporation - essentially a trade association for downtown businesses. They’re not even the entity directly responsible for managing Nashville’s Business Improvement Districts. As per the founding ordinance, in the case of the Central Business Improvement District (CBID), that entity is the Central Business Improvement District Management Corporation.

The Nashville Downtown Partnership is a vendor contracted by the CBID to provide management services. This distinction may be confusing for many - potentially even for Tom Turner, as he serves as the highest-ranking executive of both organizations. This dual role makes it rather difficult for the CBID to hold NDP accountable when they fail to manage public property effectively (see: the Downtown Library parking garage explosion), but that’s a substack for another day.

Perhaps because the same CEO/President sits atop both organizations, it seemed logical to someone to put NDP on the grant application. But what the grant process expressly requires is for eligible entities within the same jurisdiction to acknowledge that they won’t be applying for competing portions of the allocation. Metro and the CBID management corporation needed to confirm they weren’t seeking these funds so NDP could apply for 100% of the allocation.

A Memorandum of Understanding to that effect is what Mayor O’Connell signed. Which raises an important question: can the Mayor unilaterally commit Metro to not pursuing $15 million in public safety funding? That’s a constitutional question for the Metro Attorney to address.

The Stakes: This Isn’t Political Theater

Before we get into the document analysis, let’s be clear about what’s at stake. This can easily be misconstrued as some tit-for-tat local politics lovers’ quarrel - partisan squabbling over process and procedure. It is not.

We are discussing the permanent infrastructure of surveillance in Nashville. Once deployed, these systems are nearly impossible to use with meaningful oversight, and critically, once the technology is in place, we actually don’t have control over it.

Consider just one example: Flock Safety’s standard contract language gives the company sole right to touch their cameras. When Greensboro, North Carolina canceled their Flock contract on September 30th, 2024, the cameras remained up and operational because the police department legally couldn’t remove them - only Flock could. The cameras continued monitoring and collecting data on Greensboro residents with no active contract in place.

These contracts also typically grant companies broad rights to use collected data however they wish. Under Tennessee’s Information Protection Act, which went into effect in July 2025, data processors have legal autonomy to share data they collect with law enforcement at any level - federal, state, or local - at their own discretion based on “reasonable belief” that a law was violated. This right cannot be restricted at the local level.

This means cameras and software ostensibly purchased for downtown Nashville become infrastructure that can feed data to Immigration and Customs Enforcement, the FBI, the NSA, or any federal agency these companies contract with - and under Tennessee law, Nashville cannot prevent this.

Understanding the Structure

The MOU establishes a three-party signature structure that’s worth examining:

  1. Freddie O’Connell signs as Mayor (Metro takes 0% and agrees not to apply)

  2. Thomas D. Turner signs as Executive Director of District Manager for Nashville District Management Corporation (the CBID takes 0% and agrees not to apply)

  3. Thomas D. Turner signs again as President & Chief Executive Officer of Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP takes 100%)

This structure is significant because NDP is not itself a Business Improvement District. According to the state grant solicitation, eligible entities include “Business Improvement Districts (BIDs) and Business Improvement District Management Corporations.” The CBID is a BID created by Metro ordinance. NDP is a private 501(c)(6) corporation that contracts with the CBID to provide management services—essentially, NDP is a vendor to the CBID, not a governmental or quasi-governmental entity itself.

Why This Arrangement

To understand why the administration might structure the grant this way rather than having Metro apply directly, it’s helpful to look at what’s being purchased.

Several items in Exhibit A have appeared in recent Metro budget discussions and failed to gain Council approval or faced significant public opposition:

The FUSUS Video Integration System: In 2024, Metro Council rejected expansion of the FUSUS surveillance system after learning MNPD had been operating it for 16 months without required democratic approval. The “CSC Video Wall” and camera integration systems listed in Exhibit A represent similar surveillance infrastructure.

ShotSpotter Technology: Previous attempts to expand acoustic gunshot detection have faced Council scrutiny over effectiveness and cost.

Armored Vehicles and Tactical Equipment: Military-style equipment purchases have historically required Council debate and public input.

By routing these funds through NDP rather than Metro, the administration achieves several things:

  1. Bypasses Appropriations Process: Metro Council appropriates Metro’s budget. If Metro received these funds, Council would have line-item authority over how they’re spent. With NDP receiving the funds, Council’s only vote is to approve or reject the MOU as a whole.

  2. Limits Oversight: NDP is a private corporation. Even though it’s spending state grant money for public safety purposes, it’s not subject to the same transparency and oversight requirements as Metro departments.

  3. Speeds Procurement: Items that might require extensive Council vetting, public hearings, and approval processes can be purchased by NDP and then presumably deployed in partnership with Metro departments.

  4. Commits Future Councils: The grant runs through June 2028. Equipment purchased by NDP may create operational dependencies that effectively commit future Councils to ongoing costs or programs they never voted to establish.

The Legal Question

The Tennessee grant solicitation (pages 10-22) states that Metro’s maximum available allocation is $15,028,698. The Letter of Agreement shows Metro and the CBID taking 0%, with NDP taking 100%. The MOU requires Metro to “refrain from applying for any amount.”

If the intent truly is for “most of the grant to be donated to Metro” and for these donations to be “subject to future Council legislation,” why doesn’t the MOU say this explicitly? Legal documents routinely specify payment terms, transfer mechanisms, and timing.

More importantly: If this arrangement involved NDP donating funds or equipment back to Metro, that would require a gift acceptance resolution with specific figures, terms, and conditions. That’s how Metro legally accepts gifts.

Here’s what an actual gift acceptance resolution looks like in Metro Nashville:

A recent resolution (D-25-13445) accepting a $50,000 donation from BNA Woodland OZ Business LLC demonstrates the proper structure:

  • Title: “A resolution accepting a donation from [donor]”

  • Legal Authority: Cites Metropolitan Code § 5.04.120, which authorizes Council to approve donations

  • Specific Amount: “$50,000”

  • Purpose: Clearly states what the donation will fund

  • Operative Language: “BNA Woodland OZ Business LLC’s proposed donation of $50,000...is approved”

  • Required Signatures: Recommended by department head, Introduced by Council member, approved by Finance and Legal

The NDP MOU contains none of these elements:

  • Never uses the words “donation” or “gift”

  • Doesn’t cite Metro Code § 5.04.120 or any gift acceptance authority

  • Specifies no amounts being donated to Metro

  • Contains no acceptance language for any donation

  • States Metro will “refrain from applying” rather than accepting future donations

The plain language of the MOU states precisely what it is about - it’s a Memorandum of Understanding regarding the grant application process, not a gift acceptance agreement with donation terms.

The absence of such language and structure suggests the arrangement is exactly what it appears to be: NDP receives and controls the funds, spending them according to Exhibit A, with Metro’s role limited to enabling the application.

If There Are Additional Agreements, Disclose Them

It’s possible that side letters, supplemental agreements, or additional state grant requirements exist that weren’t included in the public MOU. If such documents exist and they do establish donation mechanisms or pass-through arrangements, the administration should disclose them immediately.

But even if such agreements exist, they would not change the fundamental legal requirement: For Metro to accept donations from NDP, Council would need to pass gift acceptance resolutions under Metro Code § 5.04.120. Those resolutions would need to specify amounts, purposes, and terms - just like the $50,000 BNA Woodland donation example above. No such resolutions have been filed or mentioned.

The fundamental critique remains valid: Why weren’t donation terms in the MOU presented to Council? Why not include them in the public record?
