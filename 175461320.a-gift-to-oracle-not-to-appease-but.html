My last big piece was a “take down” of sorts of Oracle —though I don’t think just typing up what its co-founder has said for decades what they want to do is a “take down” per se. It’s more of a: “uh, hey guys, we cool with this?”

If I were to judge by the responses I got from every day Tennesseans, I’d say the answer is a deafening “no.” If I were judge based on what the politicians who I can see have read my emails have said, the vibe is more: “let me talk to their lobbyist and get back to you.”

But I’m not really here to fulfill some David and Goliath archetype. Partly because my rock slinging game just ain’t that good, and this isn’t Goliath (who, let’s be honest, was probably just a guy in the ancient world who was over 6 feet tall). Oracle is not a Goliath, they are more of a Galactus, an eater of worlds.

Oracle, if we’re to take its Founder and CTO Larry Ellison at his word, eats data. Specially, all the data.

“I have to tell [the] AI model as much about my country as I can. We need to unify all the national data, put it into a database where it’s easily consumable by the AI model, and then ask whatever question you like. That’s the missing link.”

(Fortune, Feb 14, 2025).

And here is where I am supposed to terrify you about the dangers of this, how this is everything we trained on to be afraid of via young adult dystopian novels for the past half century. And, in point of fact, it is.

But there’s something curious about this hunger to eat the world that Oracle and Ellison won’t publicly admit to. It’s a problem that they, and the big data aggregators keep coming across, and are not going to solve. This isn’t to say that we shouldn’t be worried about what they’re going to do with our health records, of cellular data, our bank transactions, all able to be provided through “collaboration” with law enforcement of their own accord, but it is something that I know keeps them up at night.

The problem is that they can’t actually consume the data. The can scrape it into their mouth, chomping down on it like Galactus taking a bit out of the Baxter Building, but they can’t actually make much sense out of what they’re consuming.

Sure, can they run a query through their vast datasets and create a join table of personal identifying information of people who ask ChatGPT things that put them on a watch list.

SELECT DISTINCT 
    u.user_id, 
    u.username, 
    u.email,
    u.address,
    u.ssn
FROM users u
JOIN messages m ON u.user_id IN (m.sender_id, m.receiver_id)
WHERE UPPER(m.message_content) LIKE ‘%Is fascism bad?%’;

But promises like Ellison is making, that it can help see patterns with agriculture and cure diseases, on a small scale, will absolutely prove fruitful. But only where they augment the pursuits of human inquiries that are already shaped by the rules that given domains have proven out, where there are observations of emergent properties and tendencies that hold true when taken in context.

What their AI can definitely can do is export an error-riddled report of anyone is doesn’t fit certain categories of desirability for the state. What no AI currently can do is do things like this, autonomously, and at scale:

The AI model needs to be provided with comprehensive data for context to predict outputs. For agricultural purposes, for example, this means providing all the data of a country’s farms, the utility infrastructures, borders, and climate. If you want to improve population health, for instance, you have to provide all your healthcare and diagnostic data, as well as your electronic health records and your genomic data.

Larry Ellison

And it’s not a brute force problem. It’s a metaphysical problem. The problem is that the deep, foundational structure of the relational database, the core of what Oracle built it’s original application for the CIA, and whose logic is actually still at work in the machine learning of AI models, is incapable of dealing with a simple thing:

Context. Databases erase all context, they store information as if it were the thing in itself. And in many ways, it often is the thing in itself.

Context isn’t extra data; it’s what makes data mean anything in the first place. Strip out context and what you have isn’t knowledge—it’s raw coordinates, ready for whoever has power to decide what they “mean.”

A database might store the latest of your heart rate. It captures your systole and diastole, e.g. 120/80. It records this number as if it is your heartbeat, and presents it to doctors through an interface, telling them: “at Richard’s last blood pressure was 120/80.” What it usually doesn’t include is the context of this reading. Who took this reading? With what blood pressure cuff? In which electronic health record (EHR) system did they record this number? What field names does that system use? How confident is the system that these metrics mean the same thing in that system vs this one.

Data is flattened into atomic, discrete facts in databases, the mechanistic aesthetic of data systems leading us to treat everything like precise Newtonian equations. And eventually we stop thinking about Richard’s heart rate as the thing happening in his body, but the thing recorded in his EHR. If a fit bit records a heart beat after you’re dead, are we more likely to think the Fitbit is broken or a person has come back to life?

But if you delete the record of your heart beat from the database, your heart keep beating. If you leave behind electronic health records entirely, you still have health, even if it isn’t being recorded by the interactions of electrons within transistors. But there are other things that do live only in 1s and 0s.

But if you erase your credit card debt from every instance of your bank’s databases, your debt is gone.

The ground, the landscape, the frame of reference, the phase space in which things and events unfold, is indifferent to the modernist metaphysics at the heart of most data science.

Now, data nerds will say: “Actually, AI overcomes that problem. Machine learning doesn’t rely on relational databases. It uses statistical modeling to generate emergent answers. It’s trained on impossibly large datasets. It mimics the random collisions of meaning that happen in the human mind. It’s not a database—it’s something new, maybe even more complex than the real world.”

To which I say: yeah, but not really.

When you look at what an AI model is actually trained on—typically JSONL format, rows of text paired with labels or probabilities—it’s the same flat, datafied collapse of reality into supposedly “objective” parameters. Every token, every vector, is a coordinate in a space of meaning that’s been forcibly quantized. It’s like someone compressed Shakespeare into a spreadsheet and then taught the spreadsheet to dream [AI wrote that joke]. Each human sentence, each act of sense-making, has been atomized into numeric representations that can be statistically recombined—but never understood.

What the AI is great at is recombination—not cognition. It can blend parameters in dazzling ways, and those combinations can sound uncannily truthy. A large language model is, in effect, a massive semantic join table, one where the join condition isn’t user_id or order_number, but the probability that one token should follow another in human-like text.

That’s why it feels contextual—it’s doing pattern mimicry, not contextual reasoning. It produces the appearance of understanding by sampling from distributions that have already stripped away the conditions of knowing.

And in that sense, it’s not a fix for the database problem. It’s the next logical phase of it.
Where the database flattened the world into structured rows, the model liquifies it—turning meaning into a probabilistic slurry. There’s no ground, no provenance, no epistemic position. Just endless surface pattern.

Ask a model for a precise number with a verified source and watch it slip through your fingers. It can asymptotically approach honesty—it can sound right—but it can’t be right in any ontological sense, because it fundamentally lacks what Experiential Ontology would call frame-indexing: the ability to recognize from where and under what conditions a claim is true.

A model can imitate infinite perspectives, but it can’t see perspective. There’s no “who,” no “when,” no “why here.” Just an endless “what next?”

And that’s why Oracle—like plenty of other corporations—keep face-planting when they try to stitch whole universes of context into one neat database.

When the VA switched to a new Oracle Cerner medical records system, the software couldn’t keep track of patient information correctly—and the results were deadly. The old and new systems spoke slightly different “languages,” so when doctors entered orders for tests, medications, or follow-up visits, the computer sometimes didn’t know where to put them. Instead of warning anyone, it quietly dumped them into a hidden digital drawer called the “unknown queue,” where more than 11,000 medical orders disappeared without a trace.

At the same time, mismatched data between the systems mixed up medication records for hundreds of thousands of veterans—some prescriptions vanished, others showed up wrong, and some patients were given the wrong information entirely. Doctors thought they were looking at accurate records when, in fact, the system had scrambled them. Veterans missed critical treatments and at least four died. In the end, the tragedy wasn’t caused by bad doctors—it was caused by a computer system that erased context, hid its own mistakes, and pretended everything was fine.

The problems Oracle is facing with integrate hundreds of different instances of electronic health care records system isn’t, at least not primarily, tied to them being bad at their job. From those with insider access to this precise issue, it is a legitimately Herculean undertaking to do this, replete with the most headache inducing bureaucratic nightmares of government middle managers with red light / green light authority that seem more interested in making things complicated than making them make sense.

The urge to say fuck it, throw it all in a large language model is tempting. Let the AI deal with all these unique use cases and weirdness. And this is increasingly the conclusion of those with authoritarian ambitions — when seeing that the world is full of a bunch of context that they can’t be bothered with and their unqualified flunkies can’t make heads or tails of, ask the robits to figure it out.

But, spoiler alert, they will not. There will be many more than four dead veterans at the end of this TechnoFutilist attempt to impose order or else.

Ellison: “WHICH IS IT!” The TechnoFutilist will demand of me, Larry Ellison grabbing me by the collar while I’m absentmindedly picking at my nails.

No amount of brute force—no database joins, no LLM embeddings—can collapse them into one clean line. The best you can do is trace the hypotenuse, the emergent path between them, √2 long and always diagonal. That’s where meaning actually lives: not in the structure, not in the context, but in the tension that connects them. The problem with Oracle, with AI, with every techno-saviorist project that promises to eat the world, is that they keep mistaking the sides for the solution.

The hypotenuse is the world.”

The Hypotenuse Manual

A Field Guide for Systems That Can’t Swallow the World

(v0.1 — Released Publicly, On the House)

1. Purpose

To demonstrate how a system can function without pretending to be God.
This document offers a practical design pattern—the hypotenuse architecture—for any system attempting to integrate heterogeneous realities (e.g., Oracle Cerner’s VA EHR modernization, national health data lakes, or AI models that claim omniscience).

It is a theory rendered as interface, a metaphysical repair kit that can be implemented in code.

2. Problem Statement

Modern data systems—relational databases, data warehouses, machine learning models—are built on a shared metaphysical error:

They treat structure as sufficient to contain context.

That assumption collapses when the world pushes back. Orders vanish, meaning drifts, veterans die.
The missing piece isn’t better AI, faster compute, or more data.
It’s ground—the conditions under which information becomes true.

3. Conceptual Model

     (Structure)
          |
          |⟂     Meaning lives here
          |      — along the diagonal —
          |           / 
          |          /
          |         /
          |        /
   (Context) ----/----- (The Hypotenuse: the world)

  • Structure = data models, schemas, APIs.

  • Context = origin, method, frame, and purpose of data.

  • Hypotenuse = the relational tension between them; where meaning actually lives.

4. The Nine Stilts of Ground

Every valid data claim rests on nine supports (three triplets):

Epistemic (How it was witnessed) Semantic (What it means) Situational (Where it lives) Agent – who observed Term – what it’s called Scale – how large the context Mode – how it was produced Definition – how it’s defined Time – when it occurred Source – where it entered the record Jurisdiction – in which system or coding standard Field – in what cultural/political space it matters

Without all nine, numbers float. With them, they stand on ground.

5. Design Principle: Membranes, Not Joins

Traditional integration relies on joins—forced equivalence between systems.
Hypotenuse architecture uses membranes—negotiated relationships that can say “no.”

Membrane Rule:

When two systems disagree, stop pretending.
Create a discrepancy, not a disaster.

Membranes preserve uncertainty, trace provenance, and require human acknowledgment before translation.

Each record carries its context.

6.2 Membrane Contract

Defines how two frames of meaning negotiate.

{
  “membrane”: {
    “id”: “vistA_to_millennium_v3.7.2”,
    “source_frame”: “VistA-WallaWalla”,
    “target_frame”: “Millennium-Spokane”,
    “rules”: [
      {
        “term”: “medication”,
        “map”: {”vista_drug_id”: “rxnorm_code”},

    “safety_policy”: {
      “MED_ID_UNRESOLVED”: “block”,

  }
}

If a number is wrong, it must at least be wrong with a return address.

11. The Hypotenuse Clause

A system may:

  1. Record structure faithfully.

  2. Preserve context rigorously.

  3. Negotiate the diagonal between them.

It may not:

  • Pretend context is data.

  • Act when it does not know.

12. Implementation Readiness

  • Cost: trivial (<1KB metadata per record).

  • Timeline: immediate — no schema rewrite required; wrap at interface layer.

  • Tooling: compatible with JSON, XML, HL7v2, FHIR, CSV, or conscience.

  • Testing: if your “unknown queue” ever reappears, you have broken the membrane.

14. Coda: On the Hypotenuse

Structure and context are orthogonal.
Their product—the diagonal—is life.

You can’t own the diagonal.
You can only move along it with care, acknowledging what you don’t know.

That’s what this manual teaches:
how to make a system humble enough to work.
