In light of the upcoming third attempt to push a three-quarter million dollar contract between MNPD and the “technology and weapons” firm Axon, a contract to provide the police department with seamless real-time access to cameras owned by opted-in private businesses, it seems fit to think critically about surveillance and whom we share our data with.

Axon is a company twice sued by US cities for alleged unlawful monopoly practices, locking cities into long-term contracts and stifling competition for public safety equipment while buying up its competition. It has been lambasted by the ACLU for selling police hallucinatory AI software to write error-ridden incident reports. Its CEO has been accused of lying about his reason for founding the company, allegedly pretending to have been friends with two murdered teenagers from his high school, creating the fiction to provide him an inspirational backstory for the business that’s netted him hundreds of millions from selling tools to cops.

Meanwhile in America, agencies like ICE and DHS are partnering with local regional enforcement to use their local jurisdictions to perform interrogations that federal officials are legally barred from doing. The TN State Legislature has criminalized even voting for policies that put any limits on law enforcement collaborating with the federal government on immigration issues.

As I wrote previously: The Only Way For Metro To Keep Resident Data from the Feds is Not To Have It.

Yet Nashville’s Mayor is still supporting the contract for public/private surveillance cams, asserting that “guardrails” in the form of amendments, amendments that will be appearing at Tuesday’s Council Meeting, will address these issues.

Now more than ever, relatively unenforceable “guard rails” are not enough when it comes to something as critical as privacy, and holding back an encroaching surveillance state.

It would be great if there was a leader in Nashville that spoke openly, consistently, and intelligently on the very real dangers of a surveillance state, and the importance of the government upholding our right to privacy.

It would be great if there was a leader who would listen to criticism, take it in with humility, and pivot to other solutions that, just maybe, aren’t being aggressively sold to us by a company with a $49.83B market cap and four registered lobbyists on payroll to sway Metro Government.

If only there was a leader who knew when to cut their losses and choose what they know is right, even if it meant upsetting folks with deep pockets and long memories.

Luckily, Nashville has a leader who understands the importance of privacy and good governance: Former District 19 City Council Member Freddie O’Connell.

Former Metro Council Member O'Connell's Twitter account is a virtual undergrad course explicating the dangers of state overreach via surveillance technology. To a genuinely surprising degree for a medium-sized metropolis’ Council Member, O’Connell focused consistently on advocating for personal privacy from surveillance— surveillance in the form of law enforcement cameras, online data collection, COVID-19 personal health data, and license plate readers.

In 2021, Council Member O’Connell reposted an article chronicling the harm to vulnerable populations resulting from “SafeCam NOLA” — a live camera streaming system virtually identical to what the current Mayor of Nashville is supporting. Former Council Member O’Connell was staunchly opposed to this. In 2021, the Council Member for District 19 asked Nashville to not become the next New Orleans, to rise above “spying on one another.”

Speaking against the surveillance state wasn’t a hobby horse he picked up out of nowhere, it was more or less Council Member O’Connell’s thing.
