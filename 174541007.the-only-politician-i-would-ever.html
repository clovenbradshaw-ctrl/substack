Why I’m Aftyn All The Way

When State Rep. Aftyn Behn announced her run for Congress in Tennessee’s 7th District, I did what any self-respecting political observer does: I looked for the catch.

I found nothing. She’s the real deal.

Via Facebook: State Rep Afton Bain here running in the 7th Congressional District. I’ve had a blast a blast in Jolton today hanging out outside this hardware store. Shout out to Jolton Hardware Feed and Pharmacy and Chris, who’s been a lovely patron. Today’s been really interesting.

I’ve talked to a bunch of folks who voted for Trump and the common thread that we share with them, that I share with them, is that they do not want corporations rigging the system against us.

They have explicitly said that they don’t want the corporate oligarchy, corporate rule. These were terms that they use, not me. And they want someone who is against the status quo, who wants to unrig the system in favor of working people and against the greediest corporations and CEOs in this country. So, yeah, it’s been really interesting.

I do think I’m the candidate to not only win the primary, but to convince a bunch of Trump voters in the general that I, life is unaffordable and you can come to our side. You can vote for me, the candidate that will fight for affordability and fight against corporate greed.

This isn’t about left versus right anymore. It’s about up versus down, and right vs wrong, and not letting them confuse you on which is which. It’s about the people remembering that they are the power . Behn represents something rare: a candidate who threatens autocratic systems not because she’s ideologically pure, not because she’s got all the right opinions, but because of the values she operates with. From what I’ve seen, she insists that all power— people (the source of all power), as well as corporate, government, even law enforcement— they all must operate under constitutional constraints and public accountability.

What Makes Her Different

As soon as she announced for a federal seat, my fear was that national Democratic consultants would descend and sand off her edges, saying crap like —”Don’t say that! It doesn’t poll well with suburban swing voters!” That’s the usual playbook: take someone vibrate with the fire of righteousness and turn them into focus-grouped beige blob of bullshittery.

Behn hasn’t done that. She’s sharp, authentic, willing to say what she believes even when it makes consultants nervous. And boy how I love making consultants nervious. And that’s exactly why she can break through in TN-7.

The TN-7 Paradox

Tennessee’s 7th District shouldn’t make sense on paper. You’ve got gated mansions in Williamson County—the wealthiest county in Tennessee with a median household income of $131,202—where Teslas share lanes with mega-church traffic, sitting miles from rural towns where the hospital closed and Dollar General is the grocery store. There’s Clarksville’s military community, where families care less about ideology and more about whether Washington remembers that deployments break people.

Trump won here by 22 points in 2024. Establishment democrats will tell you its a “safe Republican seat”, but I think that’s because they’re not willing to actually hear what the constituents want. But underneath, something’s shifting—because people across this district are exhausted with politicians who’ve been run through a messaging machine.

They’re exhausted with power that doesn’t answer to anyone. That exhaustion cuts party lines — because its never been about parties. It’s why Behn can talk to Trump voters about corporate tax dodging and find common ground. The question isn’t “are you liberal or conservative?” It’s “does power serve you, or do you serve power?” Tennesseans aren’t ones to bow before nobody, but I see a lot of dirty trousers around this state — whether its people excited for the wholesale defunding of our future, or a phony resistance clutching their pearls saying “Golly this sure is dark. Please donate!” But Aftyn has been out here in the trenches, talking to real people, and coming up with real ideas that’ll work for real Tennesseans.

What She Actually Stands For

She believes in results. Her campaign is built around three solutions: Feed Kids. Fix Roads. Fund Hospitals. Not ideology, not hash tags or virtue signals or dog whistles—concrete services. As a healthcare community organizer, she worked on rural hospital closures when 15 rural hospitals closed in Tennessee—second-most in the nation. At least 9 are “at-risk” of closing now due to federal budget cuts. She’s proposed repealing the grocery tax, funding rural hospitals, fixing infrastructure. These aren’t culture-war slogans. They’re what Tennesseans worry about when they look at their bills.

She believes in accountability that flows upward, not just downward. From confronting Speaker Casada about protecting legislators accused of sexual assault to camping outside the Governor’s office demanding accountability to drafting bipartisan harassment reforms—she’s been consistent about one thing: making power answer for its actions. When GOP legislators refused to support transparency legislation because a Democrat co-sponsored it, that proved her point.

She believes corporate power shouldn’t operate above the law. Did you know that 60% of Tennessee corporations pay less in taxes than working families pay on groceries. Many billion-dollar corporations pay nothing. When she proposed eliminating the grocery tax and closing corporate loopholes, she wasn’t proposing “socialism”—she was proposing accountability. Why should working families pay more than Fortune 500 companies? Don’t let them get you hung up on $5 political science words they don’t understand. Look at the concrete solutions and more importantly the values behind them. That’s the politics of the future.

Why Her Critics Actually Make The Case for Her

Conservative media makes Behn a regular target. They’re obsessed with her. They tend to do that with women you are young, compelling, and right. Their criticisms aren’t wrong on the facts—but they reveal exactly what autocratic systems fear: accountability flowing upward.

“Her economic policies would hurt business?” When 60% of Tennessee corporations pay less in taxes than working families pay on groceries, the question isn’t whether her policies “hurt business.” It’s whether corporations should operate in a separate system with separate rules. Her critics defend a two-tiered structure where capital doesn’t answer to the same laws as labor.

Not to mention, businesses do better when their employees can actually afford to buy their products.

“She’s confrontational and breaks norms?” Sorry, but “breaking norms” is not really shocking for politicians any more. And when the way the system is running is crazy, you’re crazy to act like its normal.

When there are out and out creeps in our legislature, I expect, I demand people to speak out about that. And that’s precisely what Aftyn did in 2019.

She was thrown out of the House gallery for confronting Speaker Casada as he protected a legislator who apologized on a recorded call for sexually assaulting teenage girls. Casada later received 36 months in federal prison for fraud and bribery. Her critics want only institutional insiders to decide when systems need changing—even when those systems protect sexual predators and operate through corruption.

“She documented ICE operations?” You know y’all, I come from a family of military lawyers. The way I see it, if the only way you can do your job is in the dead of night with a mask on — maybe someone should keep an eye out that you’re doing it right. We got MNPD and the Sheriff’s departments serving warrants all day long, and they got not problem doing it by the book in the light of day.

Here’s what documenting ICE means to me: She ensured federal agents operated under constitutional constraints and formal legal agreements. Law and Order goes both ways. Her critics believe enforcement agencies should operate above oversight. That’s not law and order— that’s chaos.

I’ve written previously about how DHS and ICE are violating the Constitution on a daily basis on our city streets, and that makes all of us less safe. You got a problem with people filming ICE to make sure they’re doing their job right? Well friend, give me a better way to stop these types of problems I’ve listed below, and I’ll help ya do it.

But in the meantime, the best solution I can think of is getting someone in the US Congress who takes doing things by the book seriously — and that’s Aftyn.

The pattern is clear: her critics defend systems where only certain people—corporate executives, political insiders, unaccountable bureaucrats—get to decide what rules apply to whom. She threatens that monopoly not because she’s radical, but because she insists the same rules apply to everyone, especially those with power.

What I See in Her

Here’s what cuts across partisan lines: the belief that institutions should serve people, not the other way around.

A farmer who values tradition also expects the government he pays for to maintain the roads he takes to the market. A suburban parent who prizes stability also expects corporations to pay their fair share. A veteran who fought for the constitution expects everyone, especially law enforcement, to follow the Constitution.

Most politicians code-switch between values, performing different versions of themselves for different audiences. What’s different about Aftyn is she applies the same principle everywhere: power must answer to the people it governs, because the the people are the power (and don’t let them tell you otherwise).

This is why she can talk to Trump voters about the system being rigged and find agreement. She posted on Facebook: “TRUMP VOTERS KNOW THE SYSTEM IS RIGGED. Talked with Trump voters in Joelton today. We agree: life is unaffordable + corporations are rigging the system. I’m running to fight for working people, not CEOs.”

That’s not pandering. That’s recognizing that the real divide isn’t left versus right—it’s whether you believe power should be accountable to ordinary people or whether you believe institutions get to operate above the rules.

Why Now Matters

I don’t go out on a limb for just anyone. And if she wins and slips into Democrat business as usual, I’ll be first in line to call her out. You know I like taking people to task for not being who they say they are, and I don’t carry water for people just cuz they’re on the blue ticket. So it means something when I say this is a real one, folks.

You can’t fix old problems with old solutions. TN-7 has a rare chance—maybe their only chance for a long time—to be represented in all their complexity. Not just the wealthy suburbs or rural towns, not just the churches or the bases, not just the anger or the hope, but the full, contradictory, nuanced community people actually live in.

That’s why I’m endorsing Aftyn Behn. And that’s why you should vote for her.
