Legal Basis, Agency Excuses, and Your Rights Under Tennessee Public Records Law

Right of Access Under Tennessee Law
Tennessee Code Annotated (T.C.A.) § 10-7-503(a)(2)(A) grants all Tennessee citizens the right to inspect state, county, and municipal records during business hours. Records must be made available unless specifically exempt by law.
Source: T.C.A. § 10-7-503(a)(2)(A) – Justia

Prompt Response Requirement
T.C.A. § 10-7-503(a)(2)(B) requires agencies to respond to a request within seven business days by: (1) providing the record; (2) issuing a written denial with legal justification; or (3) providing a timeline for production. Failure to respond within this timeframe constitutes a denial under Tennessee law.
Source: T.C.A. § 10-7-503(a)(2)(B) – Justia

Agency Defense: “Too Burdensome” or “Too Large”
Agencies sometimes claim that a request is too voluminous or resource-intensive to fulfill.
Clarification: The Tennessee Public Records Act does not provide a blanket exemption for burdensome requests. While agencies are allowed to charge reasonable fees for labor and reproduction or work with requesters to narrow overly broad requests, volume alone is not a lawful reason to deny access. The Tennessee Office of Open Records Counsel encourages agencies to respond with timelines and fee estimates—not denials—when facing large requests.
Source: Open Records Counsel – Records Request Responses, MTAS Public Records Policy Guide (PDF)

Agency Defense: “Requires Creating a New Record”
Agencies may argue that exporting financial data requires creating a new record.
Counterpoint: T.C.A. § 10-7-503(a)(4) states that no new record must be created. However, exporting existing data from a financial system (like Oracle) using standard search or export functions is not considered "creating" a new record under Tennessee law. If the information exists in a searchable or extractable format, it must be provided.
Source: T.C.A. § 10-7-503(a)(4) – Justia

Broad Interpretation Requirement
Tennessee courts are required to interpret the TPRA "as broadly as possible" to ensure the fullest public access to government records. This principle supports presumptive disclosure unless a valid exemption applies.
Source: T.C.A. § 10-7-505(d) – Justia

No Requirement to State Purpose or Justify Use
Requesters do not need to explain why they want public records or what they plan to do with them. Agencies cannot deny or delay requests based on lack of stated purpose.
Supporting Sources:
– Tennessee Office of Open Records Counsel: “There is no statutory requirement to provide a purpose.” (foiabasics.org)
– MTAS: “This Schedule does not distinguish… based on the purpose of a record request.” (MTAS Policy Manual PDF)
– “TPRA does not require you to state the purpose of your request.” (FOIAbasicis.org)

Bulk Data Disclosure Precedent: OpenTheBooks.com
OpenTheBooks.com has obtained and published extensive expenditure data from Tennessee agencies, including line-item financial details from local and state entities. This demonstrates that large-scale data exports are both technically feasible and previously fulfilled.
Source: OpenTheBooks.com – Tennessee Expenditures
