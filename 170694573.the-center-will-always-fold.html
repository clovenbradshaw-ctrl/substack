Nashville calls itself progressive. A blue dot in a red state. But there's a difference between saying you're progressive and actually building progressive systems. Real progressivism decentralizes power, empowers communities, and advances equity through structural change. What Nashville actually practices is centrism dressed in progressive language — and that gap between the brand and the build is where authoritarianism takes root.

This isn't about one mayor or one administration. Mayor Freddie O'Connell inherited most of these systems, just as his predecessors did. The question isn't whether he created them — it's whether he's willing to challenge them. So far, the answer has been to perfect the performance while leaving the machinery completely intact.

Mayor Freddie O'Connell won with 64% running as "the only truly progressive candidate." His 67% approval rating suggests the brand is working. But when, in the face of unparalleled dangers for society at large, your "progressive" administration maintains the same hierarchies, enables the same exploitation, and claims the same helplessness as every administration before it, you're not progressive — at best you are a centrist whose progress-nullifying position actively accelerates the slide toward authoritarian control.

The Centrist Delusion

There's a narcissistic tendency of centrist leaders to see themselves as misunderstood martyrs, caught between "extremes." They imagine themselves as wise moderators between screeching liberals and the plutocratic professional class (replete with genocidal tendencies and pathological entitlement syndrome). It's the political science of a 14-year-old, making decisions by "taking the average."

But humans and politics aren't two-dimensional. You cannot understand society's real complexity and be a "progressive centrist" — the term is an oxymoron. When you flatten all politics to Left vs Right, you imagine a landscape where no true progress is possible. Your function as centrist is to nullify all progress.

When centrism masquerades as progressivism, it gives cover to policies that keep inequity in place while claiming moral high ground. The public believes progressive work is being done, when the machinery of change is actually running backward.

I think society and politics can’t be summed up by a single axis, Left vs Right — things which don’t really mean anything too stable. But I also don’t think it’s just too complicated to talk about. I think an incomplete but much more useful way to think of things is not with one axis, but with three axes that actually describe the way these ideologies want to make the world different.

Combining these gives you 27 different styles of arranging things (instead of just a 2D spectrum of Left vs Right) and it gives you much more meaningful ways to ask things like “are we actually a progressive city?”

But I digress…

So before we say “we tried to be Leftist and Progressivist” let’s be honest: we haven’t. And that’s important, because when people think they’d collectively tried something they haven’t, they often want to swing to what they perceive to be the opposite.

And we can’t afford that right now.

What Progressivism Actually Means

Mayor Freddie O’Connell’s popularity amongst liberals — and his campaign as “the only truly progressive candidate” is Nashville telling a story to itself. The problem isn’t his slogans; it’s the system he’s chosen to run without challenging in any meaningful way. The same hierarchies remain in place, the same evasions are rehearsed, the same helplessness is performed, and the outcome is the same: a city branded as progressive that functions as a centrist machine with a palpably rightward bent, clearing the path for authoritarian patterns to harden.

Progressivism is structural reorganization toward shared power — transfers of agency from the few to the many. It doesn't politely manage existing hierarchies. It builds new ones, collectivist hierarchies (sometimes called holarchies) that make the old ones obsolete. True Progressivism makes rigid, brittle hierarchies obsolete because integrative hierarchies are more effective. But they don’t come about by just “splitting the difference.”

Progressivism has a specific history in the United States, tied to the last great era of gilded age inequality. Even at its best, it is not without its faults. But what can’t be stripped from it is its clear-eyed critique of unfettered capitalism’s oppression of working class people and the role that the government can play to level the playing field.

What real progressivism does is simple: it shifts power away from the few, advances equity, and gives communities real control over the decisions that shape their lives.

To be effective, I’d argue there are three tests a modern progressive agenda needs to pass in order to determine if it is performative progressivism, or if it is actually working to effectively accomplish agenda.

The test is simple:

  • Does it decentralize power?

  • Does it advance equity?

  • Does it give communities real control?

If the answer is no, it's not progressivism — it's PR.

Nashville's Brand vs. Build

The city brands itself as a "blue island in a sea of red" — progressive, welcoming, creative. But look at what's actually being built. See how many of these things would pass the Progressivism Test.

The Homelessness Shell Game

When Metro's Office of Homeless Services gave Old Tent City residents 60 days' notice with virtually none of the $25M for permanent housing available, they called it "trauma-informed." Ignoring best practices to involve unhoused people in their relocation planning, the 120+ people were nearly all relocated to a motel — just in time for an unfunded $70 million waterfront development. The July 4th sweep? "Last minute Fire Marshal safety requirements."

The “gold standard” of Nashville’s permanent supportive housing, Strobel House? It appears to have been operating with out a contract since last September. What’s more, Metro’s Office of Homeless services appears to have been neglecting to pay the rent for people it rehoused from Old Tent City, leading to at least one “intention to evict” notice. What about any of this is “Progressive”?

To the heart of capitalism and its oppressive tendencies: The Nashville Downtown Partnership. Between 2023-2024, their publicly funded off-duty THP officers arrested 209 homeless people for sitting on sidewalks. Officers were explicitly instructed to leave tourists alone because they're the ones bringing in revenue. The pattern is clear: enforcement timed to development schedules and tourist seasons, wrapped in the language of care.

Find me the Progressivism.

The Shadow Government Nobody Talks About

Here's where it gets truly absurd: The Downtown Partnership is technically a nonprofit that's the primary vendor of another nonprofit called the District Management Corporation (DMC), which operates as a pass-through for Metro Government funds. These funds come from mandatory "special assessments" — I’d call them taxes, because if you don't pay, you face the same legal consequences as tax evasion.

According to ordinance O98-1037, the DMC must annually submit their budget to Metro Council for review and approval. They submit budgets — but Council hasn't reviewed or approved them since 2004. For twenty years, tens of millions of dollars have flowed through this system with zero actual oversight. The CEO makes $485,196 a year from these mandatory taxes.

This is choreographed negligence: everyone submits paperwork, everyone receives it, then everyone collectively ignores the actual legal requirements. It's not corruption — it's complicity wrapped in compliance. O'Connell inherited this system, but he's done nothing to challenge it. Neither did his predecessors.

Surveillance and ICE Collaboration

O'Connell made a name on Metro Council opposing police surveillance, then backed the Fusus network as mayor — it failed by one vote, not because his administration fought against it, but because privacy advocates (cough cough) asked questions the firm's lobbyists couldn't answer.

After ICE and Tennessee Highway Patrol swept through Latino neighborhoods — 369 traffic stops, 196 arrests — Nashville created the "Belonging Fund." Besides a one time cash payout for a select few victims, the most the city could offer its citizens was a spreadsheet logging calls from ICE, something that varnished our reputation as a progressive #SanctuaryCity while doing nothing of substance. It’d be nice if in exchange for our leaders drawing the eye of Sauron we actually got substantive support, meaningful support from our leaders.

The Police Destroy Their Own Oversight

In 2018, over 134,000 Nashvillians voted to create the Community Oversight Board. According to a whistleblower complaint from Lt. Garet Davidson, who worked in the department for two years, Deputy Chief Chris Gilder and Assistant Chief Mike Hagar worked with state Republicans to craft legislation eliminating the board. After the law passed, Gilder was allegedly celebrated at an internal police meeting for his role in overturning the will of 134,000 voters. Huzzah.

Meanwhile, Metro Nashville refused to file a lawsuit challenging the law, with Legal Director Wally Dietz saying the city wouldn't challenge it. O'Connell's administration has done nothing to restore its capacity, and has yet to release a Community Safety Plan it promised on “Day One.” The new Community Review Board that replaced it has significantly diminished oversight capacity — it can only review internal investigations, not conduct its own.

Sabotaging Democratic Participation

O'Connell inherited a $10 million participatory budgeting program but let it die through deliberate neglect. Despite a $600,000 marketing budget, the program yielded only 13,365 votes — well shy of the 20,000 goal. The steering committee chair said O'Connell's decision not to keep the program director and poor communication from the mayor's office doomed outreach.

When given a genuine tool for democratic participation, the "progressive" mayor let it wither. Participatory Budgeting is THE form of redistributive power sharing that many cities would kill for the resources to implement. It seems like this was too progressive for Nashville, though.

Institutional Hypocrisy Across the Board

But I don’t mean to pick on the Mayor, exclusively. Vanderbilt University, despite a $10.2 billion endowment, pays service workers $16.50 per hour — roughly 65% of Nashville's living wage of $25.41. When graduate students attempted unionization in 2024, Vanderbilt hired Littler Mendelson, the world's largest anti-union law firm, declaring that "graduate students do not meet the definition of 'employee.'"

Vanderbilt Medical Center laid off their entire LGBTQ Health Program staff on June 24, 2025 — during Pride Month's final week. A life supporting trans support hotline, terminated. But here's what's worse: In 2022, VUMC released medical records of 174 transgender patients to Tennessee's Attorney General without patient notification, including intimate photographs and mental health records. This from an institution that won seven Human Rights Campaign awards for LGBTQ+ healthcare equality.

HCA Healthcare received $4.7 billion in total CARES Act benefits, then pulled nurses off shifts during COVID surges to attend mandatory anti-union meetings led by consultants paid $400 per hour. The company staffs hospitals 30% below national averages while donating over $7 million to Florida PACs supporting Ron DeSantis, a firebrand dead set on eliminating the Affordable Care Act.

The Victimhood Industrial Complex

Watch how quickly power becomes powerlessness when accountability arrives. Watch the blow back that will come for me from this very post. It’s strange how people fight to obtain powerful positions, how they negotiate with their consciences to get control and hold on to it — but when asked to take simple action for the common good, they sure seem like they can’t do much of anything (except complain about anyone who criticize them).

Metro leaders claim state law prevents sanctuary policies while choosing not to test those limits or mobilize resistance. Vanderbilt administrators cry poverty about wages while sitting on $10.2 billion. HCA executives claim nurse shortages are a "national crisis" while spending billions on stock buybacks.

This manufactured helplessness serves three functions:

  1. It deflects moral responsibility ("Don't blame us, blame the system")

  2. It discourages resistance ("If we can't change it, how could you?")

  3. It maintains "progressive" credibility ("We hate this as much as you do")

The most insidious part: sometimes they position themselves as suffering more than the people that are harmed.

The mayor is "heartbroken" about ICE raids. University presidents are "deeply troubled" by low wages. Healthcare CEOs "agonize" over staffing cuts. Their pain becomes the story. The actual victims — displaced unhoused Nashvillians, poverty-wage workers, patients without care — become supporting characters in the leader's heroic struggle against insurmountable forces.

Breaking the Pattern Before It Hardens

What's happening in Nashville isn't just a clash of political styles. It's the local front of a much bigger fight — between systems that adapt, share power, and learn, and systems that enforce power with the bluntest tools possible.

This battle is far deeper and more meaningful than any “culture war”, in fact the culture was invented to distract ourselves from this unconscious battle of deep, all powerful vibes that shape the human universe. Its salvos are easy to hear if you truly listen, its battle lines are clear if you look carefully. An eternal struggle of emergent systems, each certain they alone are fit to steer us, acting through the hands and speaking through the mouths of actors unknowing they’re on a stage and playing roles.

Authoritarianism is the simplest of these systems. Its players can be the least learned, always playing to the rafters. Its rules are simple because they don’t need to be efficient — their blunt brutality is the point. They’re applied selectively, bent or ignored for the powerful, and enforced with precision against the powerless. Complexity, nuance, and fairness aren't bugs to be fixed for Authoritarianism — they're threats to be eliminated.

Progressive centrism, as it's practiced here, doesn't challenge that logic. It dresses the city in progressive language, then runs the same hierarchy underneath. It drains energy from actual reform, teaches people that promises and results don't have to match, and normalizes the idea that power can say one thing while doing another. That gap doesn't just breed cynicism — it clears a path for the authoritarian pattern to move in, because it's the only one still moving.

This isn't about whether you prefer the left or the right. It's about which pattern wins: the one that self-corrects and broadens participation, or the one that punishes, concentrates power, and stops adapting altogether.

So now, I have said the thing. And I will pay the price for doing it. But now that it is said, the thing has been named, this pattern can become a “thing”. And once a thing is no longer a ghost, an unspoken intuition, it can transform, transmute, and become something new. Here are some ways to transform it, and us.

How to Tilt the Field

Step one: Real-time financial transparency as legal minimum. Not just budgets reviewed, but every dollar tracked and searchable. Atlanta has Checkbook Atlanta. New York City has Checkbook NYC. These platforms show every payment, every vendor, every contract in real-time. Nashville citizens can't even find out how their special assessment taxes are spent because the Downtown Partnership's twenty-year accountability dodge continues unchallenged.

Imagine if Nashville had what NYC has: every payment over $20 visible online, searchable by vendor, agency, or purpose. The Downtown Partnership's CEO making $485,196 from mandatory taxes? Visible. The contracts with private security harassing homeless people? Public. The money flowing through nonprofits-within-nonprofits? Traceable. This isn't radical — it's what actual progressive cities already do.

Step two: Use the system's own drivers:

  • Appeal to stated values with documented evidence — Nashville leaders have expressed commitments to progressive values. Support accountability by gathering data and stories that show where outcomes diverge from goals, creating opportunities for constructive dialogue about closing those gaps.

  • Turn routine processes into accountability moments — Every budget hearing, contract renewal, and public meeting is a chance to ask specific questions about where money goes and who benefits

  • Build reforms that grow by default — Design programs so they automatically expand coverage or funding each year unless someone actively votes to stop them in public view

  • Document the gap between words and actions — When they announce equity initiatives, track the actual budget. When they claim to support workers, show the wage data. Make the contradictions undeniable with facts

Three Roles in the Same Fight

Leaders who actually want to lead:

  • Implement real-time financial transparency like Checkbook Atlanta — every payment, contract, and vendor searchable online. Metro has recently refused to provide this information to reputable orgs that publish this data citing “confidentially” of vendor information.

  • Tie every pledge to hard numbers and deadlines.

  • Design reforms that automatically expand unless publicly overturned.

  • Create oversight bodies that can't be defunded or dissolved.

  • When the system drifts toward authoritarianism, drag it into sunlight and force a public choice.

Insiders resisting from within:

  • Familiarize yourself with the TN Open Meetings Act, and don't let people mislead you as to what the public is entitled to see.

  • Same with the Open Records Act — if your supervisors are denying access to public information or hiding the misuse of funds, you may have a legal requirement to report this to the TN State Comptroller.

  • Keep personal, secure copies of everything.

  • Use every official process to put uncomfortable facts where erasing them would be a crime.

  • Share information across departments before it can be sanitized.

  • Protect colleagues who risk exposure. Learn the difference between being a whistleblower and being a narc.

Outsiders applying pressure:

  • Demand Nashville adopt transparent financial systems like Atlanta and NYC have — make it a campaign issue

  • Expose hypocrisy with teeth — Pride flags next to staff layoffs, "progressive" mayors next to homeless sweeps

  • Attack where they care most — make injustice more expensive than fixing it

  • Build what they won't — tenant unions, mutual aid networks

  • Keep the fight material, not symbolic — demand budget shifts, not values statements

The Stakes

Authoritarianism doesn't have to be smart to win. Its simplicity is its strength. Its dumbness is its strength. Progressive centrism, when it shields rather than dismantles old hierarchies, paves the road for that simplicity to take over.

The counter-pattern requires rules that apply to everyone, transparency that makes hypocrisy costly, and reforms that grow stronger with time. It's messier and slower. But it's the only way to keep the dumb, brutal efficiency of authoritarian control from becoming the only game left.

The problem with being a centrist is that you define yourself by other people’s positions. And when one side moves the Overton Window catastrophically far towards class warfare and a genocidal war on non-binary people and immigrants, when all forms of institutions appease the tyrant in ways that never stop the bleeding, playing towards the middle does not save lives. Or maybe we should say (more to the frame that our leaders will listen) it does not win votes.

The center will move. The question is whether it moves toward systems that adapt and share power — or toward systems where the rules are simple because no one in charge cares if they make sense, and they never apply to the people writing them.
