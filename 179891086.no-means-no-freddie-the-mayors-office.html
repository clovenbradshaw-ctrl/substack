Their New Tactic

Well it seems like when its proponents didn’t get what they wanted, they went back to the drawing board, and came back with a new plan. A plan they hoped we wouldn’t notice.

A strange document found its way into the legislative file for the next Metro Council meeting, scheduled to take place on Thursday, December 4.

Mayor O’Connell has signed an MOU indicating the Metro Nashville government would be willing to pass over [clarification edit Nov 27, 2025] not apply for$15M in state funds for surveillance technology and hardware to the Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP).

The Nashville Downtown Partnership would receive $15 million in state funds to purchase armored tactical vehicles, Fivecast social media surveillance software (used by ICE and CBP), mobile command posts, rapid-deploy camera networks, audio monitoring equipment, and real-time fusion centers—all operated by a private nonprofit with zero Metro Council oversight and no public accountability.

The way the grant from the state of TN is written, it is a virtual certainty the NDP will get this money if Metro Council approves it on Thursday, Dec 4.

What They Are Buying

The few people who have been made aware of this thus far have asked questions like “What is this really?” and “Isn’t this probably just some basic security equipment? There’s probably nothing too weird going on here.”

Read. Read “Exhibit A” in the MOU to see exactly what the Nashville Downtown Partnership is planning to buy with this funding.

They Submitted Last Minute Before Thanksgiving

The Mayor’s Office gave no one a heads up. Few people who are usually apprised of these things were even aware this grant opportunity even existed. Reading the grant, the city is able to apply for these funds themselves, but O’Connell, likely licking his wounds from the last surveillance battle, isn’t even trying to have the city apply for the grant.

The MOU he has already signed says we won’t compete with NDP for the money. But the city will, by legal requirement, be the pass through for this money [clarification edit Nov 27, 2025] agree it will not apply for these funds and let them go to NDP.

Unless your Metro Council Member says no on Dec 4.

Who is NDP?

NDP is the same non-profit that pays its CEO nearly half a million a year using city collected fees that have not been, by any normal definition, reviewed and approved by the Metro Council in 21 years. Since just 2019, they’ve collected over $40M that was never authorized to go to them (as per public records requests I’ve obtained).

Under this MOU, they would get access to an armored SWAT vehicle and a mobile command center. Their grant submission claims MNPD would be able to use this equipment. Under what agreements? With what oversight? This document present many, many questions, and it was submitted to Metro Council in a way that made it very unlikely people would have time to ask these questions.

Well now we’re asking.

Under this grant, NDP would also get an integrated camera system and mobile command center that makes the filmsy FUSUS-project pale in comparison.

NDP would get access to Fivecast, a nation-state level digital identity surveillance tool used by agencies like and ICE and CBP. Fivecast is multinational social media scraping platform whose marketing brags of having over 8 billion people in its database (there are, for reference, 8.2B alive on earth).

Why, precisely, a non-profit in charge cleaning up trash on lower broadway needs access to a tool that can create a dossier of anyone on earth based on their social media habits is anyone’s guess. It’s likely that, like in other cities such as New Orleans, this is simply an end run around democratic oversight.

Police not allowed to buy the toys they want? Get the neighbor kid to buy it and go play with it at his house, your parents can’t tell you what to do there.

Is the Nashville Downtown Partnership a trustworthy replacement for democratic oversight by the Metro Council and the community that collectively sets laws and limits for itself? Let’s review their recent track record.

This is the same Nashville Downtown Partnership whose main reason for being is to, in turn, hire two vendors to perform its tasks. They don’t actually do much of anything, their CEO makes half a million dollars a year to hire two companies to do all their work for them. Let’s check in on how they’re doing.

  • One of those vendors, Solaren, was cited recently for having its officers commit no less than 62 counts of impersonating police officers. Is a corporation that doesn’t know how to hire vendors who don’t know how to pretend how to be cops trust worthy with national-security level surveillance equipment?

  • The other vendor, Block by Block, exploded the Nashville Downtown Library’s parking garage through negligence, with neither Block by Block, NDP, nor Metro Nashville having enough insurance to cover the damage.

For some reason, Mayor O’Connell has deemed them trustworthy to hand over the type of software that requires top secret security clearances, and an armoured truck (which, the MOU states, they will generously allow MNPD to use).

There are two ways to approach public safety programs.

  • The first is to bring controversial programs to the public for democratic discussion. This is aligned with democratic norms, and “truly progressive” policies. The downside is you might not get what you want, or you might not get what the people who tell you what to do want.

  • And then there’s the other way, where you slide absolutely astounding levels of surveillance technology to a non-profit with deep economic ties through lobbysists to major political donors at a city and state level, obsequiously submitting it just before Thanksgiving break with little fan fair, hoping no one notices.
