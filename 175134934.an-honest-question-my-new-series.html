If you have been following my writing, you have seen how I have been focusing for the last six months on how our city interacts with the most vulnerable members of our society.

Moving forward (likely with plenty of detours) I will be writing about how we interact with the most powerful members of our society.

Beginning tomorrow I will be presenting you a series I’m calling TechnoFutilism, an exploration of humanity’s attempt to manage chaos and control the world through data, optimization, and power. This is a global story, but also, in ways that I am frankly quite alarmed to uncover, and incredibly local story.

I’ll wait until tomorrow to reveal what people and organizations will be at the heart of this story, but I’ll leave you today with a question.

On May 11, 2023, Tennessee passed its first consumer data protection policy. Like a lot of these laws, it was cut + pasted from various version of this around the country — though TN seemed to go out of its way to use the least enforceable version of each provision.

It went into effect July of this year.

I am very interested in one clause, item (3) from the list of things that the law says it does not do.

The law says will not hinder the data controller’s or processor’s ability to:

“Cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations.”

What’s weird is it already stipulates in item (2) that they can comply with all forms of law enforcement investigations.

But this allows them to collaborate in this way, sharing data, if they, the corporation with our data, think we may have broken a law, rule, or regulation. No subpoena or court or needed. And what, precisely, is a rule or regulation?

Hm.

So here’s my question:

Why did we put this in there?
