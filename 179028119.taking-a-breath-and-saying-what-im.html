2) The Contributor’s New Homelessness Walking Tour.

This one-of-a-kind walking tour, hosted by Contributor vendors, helps put you in the shoes of those who call our streets their home. Called “Unseen Nashville,” it is led by people who know these streets far more honestly than most of us ever will. For anyone tasked with making decisions about the unhoused or who has strong opinions about things like encampments, shelters, bootstraps, or SNAP benefits, taking this tour is a requirement to engage on these topics in good faith.

https://thecontributor.org/unseen-nashville-tours/

To that end, it was genuinely heartening to see Hal Cato, CEO of the Community Foundation of Middle Tennessee—one of the region’s largest philanthropies—take the tour to mark his third anniversary leading the organization. He posted thoughtful commentary about proximity and letting someone’s story rearrange you, about seeing the unshakable goodness in people our city too often overlooks. He also made a point to post a photo of one of the guerrilla benches, which goes a long way to demonstrate that there are some leaders in our city who understand how to show solidarity for common causes.

4) Kelly Clarkson Featured Daybreak Arts.

So it turns out that secretly in September, The Kelly Clarkson Show flew Daybreak Arts founder Nicole Minyard and artist Kateri from Nashville to New York to film a segment showcasing Kateri’s artwork live on set.

The episode aired November 12 featuring interviews with Nicole and Kateri, along with work from Daybreak artists MAJ and Edwin Lockridge—bringing their talents to a national audience for the first time. Kateri shared her real-life moving from homelessness to becoming a professional artist thanks to the organization.

For those unfamiliar, Daybreak Arts operates an East Nashville studio and gallery (located along bus route #23, because of course accessibility matters) where artists impacted by homelessness and housing insecurity can create, store, and showcase their work. Since 2014, they’ve served over 125 artists and paid out more than $100,000 directly to those artists. They provide free supplies, equipment, workshops, and a marketplace where artists keep the majority of their sales—treating people experiencing homelessness as the professionals and creatives they are, not as charity cases.

When a national platform like Kelly Clarkson’s show shines a light on the good work happening here—work that treats people experiencing homelessness with actual dignity and recognizes their talents instead of just their trauma—it fucking matters.

6) FLOCK surveillance ruling in Washington.

This one made me laugh. Cackle, to be precise.

A recent Washington state court ruling vastly complicates the surveillance agenda that is unfurling in the United States, across cities like Nashville, pushed by people like Mayor O’Connell with his unwaivering support of FUSUS, only withdrawn when he clearly didn’t have the votes in council.

In a November 6 ruling, Skagit County Superior Court Judge Elizabeth Yost Neidzwski held that data generated through things like license plate readers, even when stored by a third party like Flock—the same vendor Nashville has been in discussions about using—is subject to public records requests under Washington’s Public Records Act.

I honestly cannot think of something that would scare the folks in our city who are desperately pushing for more surveillance than the fact that I, as a citizen journalist well-practiced in public records requests, would be able to obtain the information of, for example, where their cars were on any given night.

It has been clearly demonstrated that our privacy is not of concern to them. Mayor O’Connell, who made his bones in council as an out and out digital privacy champion, performed a complete heel turn once taking office, and famously (to my friends at least) told Nashvillians to throw their phones in the river if they wanted to not have their locations monitored.

I wonder if knowing that by creating a public/private partnership with surveillance firms, any of us could plausibly be able to trace anyone else’s location via public records requests would make him reconsider his stance of surveillance nihilism.

In case we do go in for any of these public/private partnerships at scale, here’s where you make a public records request for the data. But you have to pinky promise to not do anything bad with it, just like law enforcement promises it will only use it for good.
