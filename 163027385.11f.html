Things feel different here in Nashville after this weekend’s ICE raid. Things have ratcheted to the next level of — something. For almost a decade now, a valid response to the question: “How’re you doing?” has been an “uhhhh” face and eyes that said “not great! But what’s the point in complaining?”

Since Sunday, I’ve felt my eyes go a bit more feral when making that face.

I sense a profound anxiety migraine coming over the center-left Belle Meade Brahmin, punctuated by the intrusive thought of “What do we do now?” And some of the solutions proposed, like philanthropic funds for the affected immigrant communities, are being met with mixed enthusiasm Leftist feeds. And this reaction is being met with exasperation.

I’ve been functioning as some sort of bridge between worlds of late. World’s that could, you know, just text teach other directly (they usually have each other’s phone number).

But somehow — maybe because of the dynamic range of socioeconomic rungs I’ve been thrust onto; maybe because I love both Shakespeare’s “problem plays” and I have strong opinions about each of the Fast and Furious films — I’ve been able to do some translating from one side to another.

There was a conversation that went something like this:

Me: “I’m just saying—based on the reactions I’m seeing—you need to make it clear that the tension is about recognizing mutual aid as the system that actually enables human survival, not charity in its paternalistic, neoliberal form. If people think you’re just criticizing them personally, they’ll shut down and stop listening.”

Feedback: “Yeah, that sounds like a thing white people need to be taught by other white people, and hey, you’re a white people!”

And boy am I ever.

In terms of insight, I have some musings for us.

I think a lot about lines and circles. How they are opposites. How the define each other. How when, put into motion, they merge into spirals. How we all tend to think of the world as being either more linear, or more circular, but how it is, from both an objective and subjective perspective, clearly both.

MLK and Nietzsche didn’t have competing metaphysics. They just focused on different parts of the spiral.

MLK, in our fogged and sanitized false memory of the Civil Rights era, is a mouth piece for the unending, unconscious, deniable march towards progress that Liberalism mesmerizes us with. Liberalism presents the world of the line.

Liberalism argues: The right candidates with funding from the right billionaires in the right districts will be able to teach empathy to the right for once and for all. But… since the moral direction of the world is so undeniable, since everything’s headed that way anyway, it doesn’t hurt to be a little passive, to settle back into complacency and consumption. In the long run, there’s only one way the market goes — it might look like its bending towards a recession (or worse), but the arc of the financial universe bends towards… profit! And even if the worst happens, and we have nothing but student loans to our name, the wealthy have acorns nestled away, stored deep in philanthropic trees — far away from the redistributive rays of the IRS. Don’t worry, the 1% will bail you out when waters start to rise. Just fill out this grant application.

King would be embarrassed by this literal flattening of his philosophy. But he would be unsurprised. He knew the all consuming power of exploitation capitalism, and the false absolution that philanthropy can serve to keep it spinning.

Capitalism doesn’t breed consumption. Unfettered consumption creates the set of arrangements that we call Capitalism. And Capitalism, in turn, consumes anything that restrains it — even the criticism that its fantasy of infinite growth, its theology that boils down to “Number Go Up”, the raw data that demonstrates that the duopoly of Philanthrocapitalism only ever exacerbates inequity — gets reabsorbed into itself, a snake eating its own business model.

The unceasing straight line ascension towards progress is bounded only by that which limits profit. But these limits pull so hard, constrain the limits of the possible so effectively, that society reverts back, recurving into its past, back into its opposite, the circle.

But it’s not that capitalism aborts the infinite progress that would have been possible if not for it. Actually, only the failure of Capitalism to resolve the conflicts it creates can give the possibility of its transcendence.

Even “the arc of moral history” is, like all geometric arcs, just a part of a circle (or spiral). Martin Luther King Jr., the Liberal Artist who loved his Dialectical Philosophy knew this well.
