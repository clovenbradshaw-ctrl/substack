Before there Was Oracle, There was IBM

I want to pivot briefly to talk about another business executive that made history last century through mass data processing. We might say that Thomas J. Watson Sr. was the Larry Ellison of his day, and IBM was the Oracle of the last century. And it was IBM’s folly, a few decades after Watson’s death, that gave Oracle its great opportunity.

In 1977, Larry Ellison and his start-up co-founders spun a CIA contract called “Project Oracle” into the company it is today. The product they sold the CIA on was not something they invented. Oracle was merely first-to-market with the digital relational database, a concept invented by Edgar F. Codd, an employee of IBM.

IBM did not want to pivot to the untested relational database model, its hierarchical databases having made them the firm synonymous with efficient large data processing for most of the 20th century. Their systems had proven extraordinarily effective at organizing data about human beings, and the business case for changing course wasn’t obvious to the data processing monolith. Oracle seized this opening, becoming the dominant relational database vendor while IBM remained focused on its traditional markets — markets that had proven very profitable throughout the 20th century.

IBM evolved from Herman Hollerith’s company that first automated the US 1880s census using punch cards—some of the earliest computers that could pull up information about people at orders of magnitude faster than human paper sorting. After becoming International Business Machines in 1926, over the next decade IBM found its second-largest market in an unlikely country, one that had just lost a world war and was struggling to reorder itself. The American company found great success in helping the government of Germany keep precise track of who was where, and what, precisely, was their identity.

By 1933, as Hitler consolidated power, IBM’s punch card technology became essential infrastructure for identifying and tracking Jews, Roma, homosexuals, and political enemies.

The relationship to Nazism and what its punchcards were doing was no secret to IBM’s leadership. CEO Thomas J. Watson Sr. personally visited Berlin at least twice annually from 1933-1939, micromanaging operations so closely that German managers literally couldn’t paint a corridor without his permission.

In 1937, Hitler awarded Watson the Order of the German Eagle with Star—Nazi Germany’s second-highest honor for foreigners—which Watson kept through Kristallnacht and the invasion of Poland, only returning it in June 1940 after nearly three years as the US inched closer to war with the Axis.

IBM’s direct control of Nazi operations remained legal until December 1941 when America entered the war, but the company continued receiving profits through its Geneva office and recovered all its machines and accumulated earnings after the war.

IBM never stopped operating in Nazified Europe, it just rebranded.

IBM’s custom-designed punch card systems automated all six phases of the Holocaust: identification, exclusion, confiscation, ghettoization, deportation, and extermination. Concentration camps maintained “Hollerith Abteilung”—IBM customer sites with machines and card organizers. IBM’s prisoner codes included 8 for Jewish inmates and 6 for death by gas chamber, with Auschwitz designated as camp 001.

As per the Begin-Sedat Center for Strategic Studies,

The first thing Hitler wanted to know was exactly how many Jews resided in Germany. IBM hired thousands to execute a door-to-door racial census. Once Hitler had the names and locations of Germany’s Jews, IBM created systems to tabulate that data against professional and employment databases as well as financial institutions to help Germany systematically pauperize them. Location data was then used to organize the mass transfer of Jews from their homes into ghettos. Ghetto residents were then systematically forced onto trains to the camps.

IBM engineers had to create codes to differentiate between a Jew who had been worked to death and one who had been gassed, then print the cards, configure the machines, and service them twice monthly on-site in the concentration camps.

As Reinhard Heydrich, a principal architect of the Holocaust, wrote in his 1939 memo “Evacuation of the New Eastern Provinces”:

The census documents provide the basis for the evacuation... Quantifying and organizing the deportation of millions of people from various regions across Eastern Europe could take years using pencils and paper. Relying upon the lightning speed of Hollerith machines, it took just days.

There is virtually no way Watson did not know what his machines were enabling. On September 9, 1939—eight days after Germany invaded Poland—Dehomag manager Hermann Rottke wrote directly to Watson requesting equipment because “under today’s conditions, a certain need has arisen.”

Edwin Black’s research for his book IBM and the Holocaust: The Strategic Alliance Between Nazi Germany and America’s Most Powerful Corporation disclosed how deeply embedded Watson and IBM were with the Holocaust.

IBM was the sole source of all punch cards and spare parts, and it serviced the machines on-site whether at Dachau or in the heart of Berlin either directly or through its authorized dealer network or field trainees. There were no universal punch cards. Each series was custom-designed by IBM engineers not only to capture the information going in, but also to tabulate the information the Nazis wanted to come out.

IBM constantly updated its machinery and applications for the Nazis. For example, one series of punch cards was designed to record religion, national origin, and mother tongue, but by creating special columns and rows for Jew, Polish language, Polish nationality, the fur trade as an occupation, and then Berlin, Nazis could quickly cross-tabulate, at the rate of 25,000 cards per hour, exactly how many Berlin furriers were Jews of Polish extraction. Railroad cars, which could take two weeks to locate and route, could be swiftly dispatched in just 48 hours by means of a vast network of punch-card machines. Indeed, IBM services coursed through the entire German infrastructure in Europe.

The Village Voice
March 27. 2002
How IBM Helped Automate the Nazi Death Machine in Poland Final Solutions

By October 1941, IBM’s European manager Werner Lier was reporting to Watson about deploying machines for “the Jewish census” in Romania. Watson even named the Polish subsidiary after himself—”Watson Business Machines”—his name stamped on the English-labeled machines tracking trains to Auschwitz. Yet in thousands of IBM documents reviewed by historian Edwin Black, he found “not a single sentence written by IBM personnel questioning the morality of automating the Third Reich, even when headlines proclaimed the mass murder of Jews.”

Today, the image of a punch card is a footnote in computer science courses, unrecognizable to many as any form of a mass surveillance device. But another form of cataloguing from that era is more recognizable, in ink that has stained humanity’s memory more than anything contained on punchcards or associated with today’s IBM. The unique identifier of those IBM punch cards became the numbers tattooed on prisoners’ forearms at Auschwitz.

Black recorded the harrowing connection between IBMs systems and the infamous Auschwitz tattoos.

In August 1943, a timber merchant from Bendzin, Poland, arrived at Auschwitz. He was among a group of 400 inmates, mostly Jews. First, a doctor examined him briefly to determine his fitness for work. His physical information was noted on a medical record. Second, his full prisoner registration was completed with all personal details. Third, his name was checked against the indices of the Political Section to see if he would be subjected to special punishment. Finally, he was registered in the Labor Assignment Office and assigned a characteristic five-digit IBM Hollerith number, 44673.

The five-digit Hollerith number was part of a custom punch card system devised by IBM to track prisoners in Nazi concentration camps, including the slave labor at Auschwitz.

Oracle will tell you they are not IBM. And that is correct.

They will likely cite their Human Rights Statement and their position as being officially Anti-Slavery.

They may point out that unlike the “Watson Business Machines” that physically tracked trains to Auschwitz, Oracle primarily provides database software, not hardware (except, of course, for hardware like their Oracle-certified Exadata servers, purchased by Chinese police for “smart policing”that’ve led to over one million Uyghurs in camps where survivors testified to torture and sexual assault”).

But IBM also claimed it was just a technology company.

They didn’t do anything with data, they just gave clients the ability to process it better.

Their CEO’s name was literally on the machines tracking trains to Auschwitz, yet the company has never apologized. In 24 years since Edwin Black’s investigation, IBM has never requested a single correction. They still use the moniker “Watson” on their AI products. The defense in essence is that they only gave the technology, they only processed the data. The data controller was who did things with that data, so no apology or amends for enabling genocide is necessary.

Oracle employs the same defense today. When confronted about its software powering China’s surveillance of Uyghurs in Xinjiang, Oracle claimed it provided “only the tools, not the policing itself”. Oracle acknowledged “limited authorized transactions with Xinjiang Public Security Bureau from 2011-2019” even as over one million Uyghurs were detained in camps where survivors testified to torture and sexual assault. In 2018, as mass detention reports surfaced, Oracle awarded Digital China “Global Partner of the Year”—the same partner marketing Oracle-powered “integrated policing platforms.”

Now Oracle has found its perfect regulatory environment in Tennessee. The Tennessee Information Protection Act explicitly allows processors to “cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations”—with “rules or regulations” left completely undefined. Unlike Texas’s requirement for formal legal process, Tennessee grants Oracle autonomous discretion to share data without warrants or subpoenas.

Ellison himself promises this technology will ensure “citizens will be on their best behavior because we are constantly recording and reporting everything that’s going on”. At the 2025 World Governments Summit in Dubai, he told Tony Blair the solution requires unifying “all the national data” into databases “easily consumable by the AI model.”

The punch cards that conducted the U.S. census became the technology of genocide in Germany. An architect of the Holocaust confirmed that “relying upon the lightning speed of Hollerith machines” reduced years of deportation planning to days. Those IBM Hollerith numbers became the tattoos on prisoners’ arms at Auschwitz—Hollerith number 44673 inked permanently onto human skin.

Today Oracle’s databases—the same relational technology IBM rejected from its own employee Edgar F. Codd—power everything from Pentagon battlefield AI to Chinese police platforms to 100% of U.S. federal agencies. With Tennessee’s processor protections and $240 million in Nashville incentives, Oracle can now claim both passivity for accountability and agency for surveillance.

The distance between a database of citizens and a list of targets is measured not in technology, but in political will.
