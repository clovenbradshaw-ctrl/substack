What’s Been Accomplished

My work has been cited in The Nashville Scene and I guested on CityCast Nashville to talk about my reporting.

  • Taking this to the next level would mean hiring an editor/fact checker.

  • It would mean more consistent articles and projects, and less things rushed out with typos that end up in people’s inboxes at 1 am.

  • It would mean collaboration with other local writers, activists, and artists, with revenue-sharing that is build in and transparent.

In the past year, I’ve done a lot. I’ve been able to post the complete finances of two Metro Nashville Departments this past year, The Metro Arts Commission and The Office of Homeless Services, turning public data into publicly accessible data.

I’ve followed the thread of arts funding through the decades to help tell the story of entitlement colliding against equity with Strings Attached: The History of the Metro Arts Commission & The Nashville Symphony.

I’ve tracked how surveillance proposals like the FUSUS contract quietly moved through Council — not just reporting the public vote made on it, but expanding the circle of who should care about things like this. I showed how that system would have allowed police to tap into private cameras across the city with no binding oversight policy — and laid out how Nashville could spend that money instead to reduce violence, before the contract was ever brought to a vote.

I’ve used public records and frontline reporting to show how Nashville’s homelessness policy functions in practice—not just in press releases. I broke down how federal funds were used to warehouse people in motels without long-term plans, and reported on the July 4 sweep of Riverfront encampment residents that bypassed the city’s own Coordinated Entry system. I also obtained and published internal spending records from the Office of Homeless Services, revealing how much the city pays to relocate, shelter, or simply displace people. The goal isn’t to point fingers—it’s to make visible how public money is spent, what outcomes it produces, and who bears the cost when policy fails.

I exposed how Nashville’s Downtown Business Improvement District quietly steers public policy using private governance. In Specially Assessed Nonsense™, I traced how this decades-old “special tax zone” collects millions in assessments and directs services like security and sanitation with minimal public input. I mapped the BID’s board structure, funding mechanisms, and contract history to show how unelected actors influence policing, surveillance, and the use of public space—often without residents knowing the system exists.

What We Could Do With How Much

I found out recently there’s a term for what I’m doing here. It’s called “Movement Journalism.”

It very much seems like there’s an appetite locally for this type of truly independent movement journalism and in-depth analysis on local issues that are microcosms of our national and global meta-crises.

But it is getting to the point where I am feeling like I need to ask: if this is something you want, what does that look like to you, and what are you able and willing to pay for it?

This work has earned me some donations (which I’ll share the exact figure on later down), but for the most part it’s cost me in terms of local career options and hours spent on unpaid research and writing.
