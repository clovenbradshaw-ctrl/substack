There is no justice in a machine that feeds on its own failure.

Preface

Metro’s $50 million investment was supposed to solve chronic homelessness. But OHS can’t even say how many PSH units exist. Developers are returning funds. Providers say the reimbursement process is so broken they’re walking away from major awards. This isn’t underperformance—it’s system failure, at the time we can least afford it.

So, here’s my thesis statement of what’s been going on the past few years in Nashville when it comes to homelessness. This article isn’t meant to be the end-all-be-all defense of this analysis, but kind of a “does this sound right to you?”

Despite its “trauma-informed, evidence-based” branding, Nashville’s Office of Homeless Services (OHS) operates as a centralized enforcement arm for developers, NIMBYs, and the city’s housed elite. Under the banner of compassionate bureaucracy, Metro has seized control over housing referrals, service reimbursements, federal funding, and homelessness data—allowing it to bury failures, punish dissenters, and clear encampments in sync with tourism calendars and speculative development timelines.

This top-down consolidation is not just inefficient—it’s harmful. Unlike Houston, which decentralized its Continuum of Care and steadily reduced homelessness, Nashville’s numbers surge unpredictably: up nearly 12% in 2023 and 4% again this year. That’s after burning through $50 million and inflating a bureaucracy that still can’t—or won’t—answer basic questions like “How many permanent supportive housing units do we have?” Now, with looming federal cuts to core social services, Nashville is poised to face a dramatic increase in homelessness just as its system proves least capable of meeting the moment.

These failures disproportionately impact Black, Brown, disabled, and LGBTQIA+ Nashvillians—especially trans youth—whose overrepresentation among the unhoused reflects not accident but the cumulative impact of policing, displacement, criminalization, and policy neglect. Many nonprofit providers, structurally dependent on city funding, are effectively muzzled—discouraged from challenging policies even as they deepen the crisis. Meanwhile, data dashboards obscure dysfunction behind the illusion of progress, prioritizing optics over accountability and keeping the public at arm’s length from the metrics that matter.

But models for justice exist. Peer-led outreach, housing-first programs, and community-driven encampment support already operate in this city—starved for resources, undermined by bureaucracy, but working. Power must be reclaimed by independent members of the Continuum of Care: through democratic reinvestment in community governance, transparent redistribution of control over data and funding, and lawful resistance to a feral bureaucracy increasingly aligned with private gain over public need.

The city’s actions of late demonstrate vividly what this looks like. 120+ people were relocated from “Old Tent City” with 60 days notice without their participation in the camp’s closure plans and without being offered permanent housing — going against Metro’s stated “Housing First” model.

And events this week show exactly what it looks like for process to be completely abandoned for political expediency.

60+ people living in an encampment near Nissan Stadium were given less than a week because of a logistical oversight by the parties that booked Friday’s July 4 fireworks. The situation is so dire that even OHS, the prime culprit in not following Nashville’s existing homelessness best practices, has spoken out against. Director Calvin told the Banner: “It absolutely does upset the process when we don’t use our strategy, or there’s an emergency situation or crisis that interrupts that process.”

What the situation shows is that even though OHS has accumulated the systemic power, they have bosses (The Mayors Office) that who dictate how those powers will be exercised. And the Mayor’s Office, in tun, has forces they respond to who have priorities that have nothing to do with homelessness “Coordinated Entry” best practices.

Let’s be clear: getting 34 people indoors—if they’re on the path to permanent housing—is worth celebrating. But it’s telling that it only happened because the city suddenly realized a Fourth of July fireworks might set them on fire. The same wheels of bureaucracy that usually grind along at a glacial pace begin melting like polar ice caps once there’s a risk of bad optics and literal fire. Meanwhile, countless others who have been languishing on the Metro managed coordinated-entry list for months (or years) haven’t enjoyed the same burst of urgency—because their encampments aren’t in a tourist postcard.

That flash-flood response exposes how far Metro has drifted from the very guardrails it pledged to respect. Coordinated entry is supposed to allocate scarce housing based on vulnerability, not geography or political deadlines. When a riverfront camp is cleared outside the system, housing becomes a perk of being in the wrong place at the right—and highly public—time. Selective shortcuts like this don’t prove coordinated entry is broken; they prove the city is willing to sidestep it whenever the spotlight gets too hot, and that they’ve achieved the systemic power to do this.

Until the same urgency is applied to everyone waiting quietly in line, fireworks, literal or figurative, will keep dictating who gets housed first.

When powerful entities gets new tools, like the ability to close an encampment at any time for any reason — the often feel that they do not have the power to NOT use those tools. What this shows is that often, the only way for someone to stop using power they have accumulated is for others to take it from them.

What Problem Were We Supposed to Be Solving?

What this all seems to be about is this:

A deal was made, perhaps explicitly with some, but implicitly with most.

Over the past half decade, a quid pro quo of sorts happened between Metro and the Continuum of Care (CoC), the association of organizations sanctioned by the US Housing and Urban Development agency to receive funds to address homelessness. Going back a few years, Nashville’s CoC was promised a once-in-a-lifetime investment into housing and support services. In exchange Metro increasingly got controls over how this space operated. It gained control that would be the envy of any municipality in the country, handed over piecemeal, one essential component at a time, given over without much fanfare or objections.

Before you can understand what Metro promised — and what it quietly gained in return — you have to understand the crisis it claimed to be solving.

Homeless encampments had been growing visibly across Nashville for the better part of a decade. The roots go back even further—to policy failures at every level dating to the Reagan administration—but in the last five years, encampments became a political flashpoint.

This wasn't unique to Nashville. Across the country, unsheltered homelessness surged throughout the 2010s. A national report in 2017 recorded a 1,300% increase in reported encampments over the prior decade. Nashville’s own arc followed the national trend: after the 2010 flood displaced the (recently close) camp known as Old Tent City, encampments reemerged in new areas—wooded slopes, greenways, underpasses—often near the margins of rapidly developing neighborhoods. Fort Negley, Brookmeade Park, the I-24 overpass at Fifth and Main—each became a symbol in its own cycle of growth, public complaint, and attempted closure.

Vocal residents groups didn’t want to see Middle Tennessee’s unhoused Dalit caste near their homes or parks. Developers didn’t want them near future projects. And many city leaders didn’t want them visible near downtown landmarks or high-profile infrastructure.

At the same time, service providers and some Metro staff were pushing back. They pointed out what should be obvious: it’s not humane to disband a camp and displace people without giving them permanent housing options. Taking people who’ve already been failed by the system and stripping away what little structure they’ve built for themselves—their tents, their social ties, their survival networks—with nothing permanent to offer in return is not a solution.

The roadblocks Metro encountered in closing encampments weren’t just the usual bureaucratic drag. They came from the very architecture of the homelessness response system—a HUD-mandated framework called a “Continuum of Care,” governed by a principle known as coordinated entry. These were brakes installed intentionally on a municipalities that, while normally sloth-paced, have a tendency when it comes to the unhoused to move fast and break people.

Coordinated entry is HUD’s required system for prioritizing who gets housed, and when. It’s meant to ensure that limited housing goes to those with the highest need, not just those closest to City Hall or loudest in line. It slows down the wrong kind of relocating of unhoused on purpose, and is meant to expedite the right kind of sustainable rehousing. It puts equity before optics. And it gives service providers cover to say: we won’t move people just because it’s politically expedient—only if there’s real housing on offer.

That made encampments hard to close. But that was the point.

If cities want to shut down camps and house people quickly, it can be done—Houston did it. But they did it by actually investing in deeply affordable, permanent supportive housing first. Coordinated entry remained in place, but it had something to coordinate to.

Nashville tried to follow that model. Consultants were hired, a $750,000 report commissioned, and $50 million was pledged—half to build housing, half to fund services. In return, Metro was granted sweeping new control over the Continuum of Care. Quietly, and with little resistance, it gained the kind of influence over homelessness response that few cities ever do.

What we got was the power centralized inside Metro Government. What we didn’t get—at least not at the promised scale—was the housing.

Two.

The Deal

But Metro didn’t commit $50 million to ending homelessness without getting something in return.

Let’s be clear about what they gained.

They became the collaborative applicant for the Continuum of Care (CoC). That means all federal HUD dollars now pass through Metro first. That’s power over what is possible.

They became the HMIS lead, controlling the Homeless Management Information System — the backbone of all data, reporting, and metrics. That’s power over what is knowable.

They became the coordinated entry lead, which means they now influence or dictate who gets prioritized for housing and services. That’s power over who is served.

And perhaps most quietly — but most crucially — they rewrote the Coordinated Entry manual, so that simply living in an encampment now qualifies someone for priority access. That’s power over what is prioritized.

When Metro first announced its $50 million investment to “end chronic homelessness,” the focus was on housing and services. But the deeper transformation—the one with long-term structural consequences—was about who holds power over the system itself.

Over the course of eight years, every critical function in Nashville’s homelessness response quietly consolidated inside one Metro department: the Office of Homeless Services (OHS). What began as coordination has, over time, become control—over funding, data, referrals, and governance.

Over the span of eight years, Metro Nashville quietly consolidated nearly every major lever in its homelessness response system under one roof. It started with data: control of the Homeless Management Information System moved from the housing authority to Metro staff.

Then came housing access, with Coordinated Entry operations gradually falling under Metro’s direction. Soon, governance itself shifted, as Metro reshaped the Homelessness Planning Council into a staff-driven entity.

By the time the Office of Homeless Services (OHS) was formally created in 2023, it had already absorbed these functions in practice. Within two more years, OHS gained control of federal funding applications, reimbursement flows, and the ability to rewrite rules that dictate who gets housed and when. What was once a distributed system of checks, balances, and community participation became a single chokepoint of bureaucratic authority. A full timeline is available as a footnote.

What It All Adds Up To

Over time, five key levers—funding applications, payments, data, housing access, and governance—have ended up in one place: OHS.

Each shift may have made sense on its own. But together, they’ve created a system with:

  • No independent fiscal gatekeeper

  • No neutral data authority

  • No external oversight board

  • No separate entity controlling referrals or reimbursement

It’s not just about who holds power—it’s about what happens when all feedback loops point to the same place.

Systems like that don’t adapt. They stall—or break.

Three.

Systems, Power, and the Commons: A Better Way Was Always Known

What’s unfolding with Metro’s homelessness response isn’t just a policy failure — it’s a systems failure. But not a surprising one. Anyone with even passing familiarity with complex systems theory could have warned us: centralizing power over shared resources, especially ones as delicate and life-critical as housing and care, tends to choke the system it's meant to streamline.

The consultants Metro Officials hand selected to generate the reports to tell them what to do likely advised otherwise. Telling them that consolidating power was the best way to get the outcomes they wanted. “Who do you trust more than you? Isn’t the government, run ostensively by people elected by residents surely more accountable than various non-profits?”

There’s an argument to be made, and conversations about this to be had about accountability in Continuums of Care. But the arguments weren’t made in public, and the conversations occurred behind closed doors, and so the worst version of redesigning the homelessness system happened — which is precisely the problem consolidating power, the first (and usually worst) idea is the one that wins.

Metro officials didn't just consolidate operations — they redesigned the system architecture itself, bringing data, dollars, eligibility, and gatekeeping under one roof. And while that made for impressive press releases and talking points about coordination, it broke every principle known to work for managing common resources sustainably.

Elinor Ostrom, whose Nobel-winning work on governance of the commons laid the foundation for how shared resources can be equitably and efficiently managed, warned precisely against this. Her research shows that top-down control over communal assets almost always fails. Instead, what works — across forests, fisheries, and even digital knowledge systems — are distributed, participatory governance models that center collective input, local knowledge, and adaptive feedback loops.

In her framework, successful commons governance requires:

  • Clearly defined boundaries ( no one can say for certain if the HPC is part of Metro or what role OHS should play in setting committee agendas),

  • Rules adapted to local context (ours are defined by political expediency),

  • Decision-making by those affected (not a select few behind Metro office doors),

  • Independent monitoring and feedback systems (not reports filtered through OHS),

  • Graduated sanctions and low-cost dispute resolution (we got accusations of ‘fake news’ against critics and team building aimed at silencing dissent),

  • The right to self-organize (providers now fear retaliation or reimbursement paralysis),

  • And nested, federated systems (Metro built a monopoly instead).

This wasn’t just a tactical misstep — it was a fundamental misunderstanding of systems theory. The further power moved inward to a single department, the more brittle and less adaptive the entire network became.

And it’s not just theory. Distributed digital systems — from open-source communities to decentralized autonomous organizations — mirror these lessons. They succeed when decision-making is polycentric, when modularity allows parts of the system to function independently but coherently, and when reputation, transparency, and trust serve as the glue holding things together. None of that applies to how Nashville’s homelessness response currently operates.

If homelessness was truly seen as a shared crisis — a commons problem that demands collaborative stewardship — then Metro would’ve set up structures for distributed accountability, not central command.

Instead, what we got is a system that can’t take feedback. That hoards knowledge. That can’t answer the most basic questions — like how many homes we actually have — without months of pressure and legal maneuvering.

“Commons”, the shared resources we hold collectively, collapse when those in charge forget they are stewards, not sovereigns. And until we return governance of this system to the community it’s supposed to serve — people experiencing homelessness, providers on the ground, and the public whose dollars are funding it — we’ll keep getting results no better than the power structures that produce them.

And the results, indeed, are not getting better.

Over the same period that we’ve allegedly been following a Houston-style “housing first” strategy, Houston’s homelessness numbers have held steady. They’re not dropping dramatically, but they’re not rising either. That stability matters. It means their system, whatever its flaws, can keep up with demand. People fall into homelessness—but they also get out. Services are keeping pace. The pipes are flowing.

Nashville’s numbers, by contrast, are all over the place. One year down. The next year way up. Then a slight dip, then another spike. And this isn’t just noise—it reflects a system that can’t regulate itself. The money gets spent, but housing doesn’t follow. The services stall. Providers walk away. And people, more often than not, stay unhoused.

This isn’t just about falling short of our goals. It’s about being stuck in a pattern we can’t seem to break—a pattern where every new fix makes things more unpredictable, not less.

Another thing that’s important to consider is that adjusted for population size, we have way more people unhoused in Nashville than in Houston. Houston is 3.5 times larger than Nashville, but we have 2.3 times as many unhoused people “per capita.” That means in Houston, for every 100k people, 138 don’t have a home. In Nashville, for every 100k people, 318 don’t have a home.

Houston’s system, for all its flaws, is built to handle complexity. It’s not simple, but it’s steady. It uses lots of different moving parts—providers, housing teams, data systems, landlord engagement units, funders—each playing a role. They don’t run in a straight line. They loop back, adjust, correct. And somehow, that mix of messy parts produces a relatively stable outcome.

That’s the trick: they use a non-linear system to produce a steady, linear result.

Nashville did the opposite. We assumed the problem could be solved in a straight line: $50 million in, housing out. We centralized the process, streamlined the flow, and tried to control it from one hub. But housing people isn’t linear. It’s unpredictable, messy, human. When you try to run a complicated, adaptive problem through a rigid, one-track system, it doesn’t simplify things—it breaks them.

We tried to apply a linear solution to a non-linear problem—and what we got was chaos.

The result is what we see now: a system that jerks around instead of moving forward. Providers can’t get reimbursed. Units sit empty. Data goes unanswered. People remain unhoused. And the numbers jump up and down, not because the need changes overnight, but because the system keeps tripping over itself.

Until we design for the kind of complexity this crisis actually requires—shared control, feedback loops, and flexibility—we’ll keep getting unpredictable outcomes, no matter how clean our strategies look on paper.

Four.

Where the Money Went

Bottom line: $24.1 million bought 168 units, leaving 2,000+ people unhoused — and growing.

What’s strange about homelessness is that it is both a highly complex “wicked social problem”, and very simple at the same time. The causes of people not having housing and the social services required to keep people housed are multi-variate, multi-namic problems: that’s a $15 way of saying that it involves things that are often changing, and when one part changes it can simultaneous affect something else. It’s a hard problem to wrap your head around.

But at the same time, the simple and extraordinarily effective solution to the problem of homelessness is this: homes that people can actually access and afford.

$25M was supposed to be set aside in Nashville for this exact thing, a generational housing investment to help end chronic homelessness. It hasn’t panned out.

Even the most generous reading of how many deeply affordable and permanent supportive housing (PSH) units Metro paid for with $25M of COVID relief funds, passed through MDHA and later the Barnes fund, puts it at 168 units. This is subtracting 14 units that were taken out of this pool from “Shelby House” due to disagreements with OHS.

Various people familiar with the situation alleged that as of April 2025, another facility with 80 units of housing, I AM NEXT, had not been receiving referrals from OHS. However Mark Just, the Interim Executive Director (I am NEXT), was able to let me know on July 1 “I can confirm that we are receiving referrals from OHS.”

So with our best understanding of how many units Metro built with $25M, it looks to be that we got 168 units of Deeply Affordable Housing, or $143K per unit. And that just pays for it to be built. Paying the rent has to come from somewhere else.

To put that in context, the latest point in time (PIT) count, which researchers agree by definition undercounts homelessness but creates a stable year-over-year metric, put those experiencing sheltered and unsheltered homelessness at 2,180 people. These units would make only a small dent in the number of unhoused men, women and children — a number expected to grow starkly with coming cuts to social services.

The $50M was supposed to build new PSH and fund supportive services to radically address this problem, to house a large portion of the chronically homeless, giving them a stepping stone to move forward. Instead, most of it has vanished into overhead, soft launches, and solutions that don’t launch at all.

Five.

Power Can’t Help Itself

We’ve arrived at a moment where Metro isn’t even pretending to follow process. When a fireworks show becomes justification for camp displacement, you realize the system isn’t broken. It’s working exactly as designed: to consolidate power and make it easy to use.

Once you build a system that lets you bend the rules, it becomes impossible not to. Once you have the power, you can’t stop yourself when the forces around you insist you must act.

I don’t think the elected officials in Metro government ever intended to use their powers to intentionally harm the unhoused. But once they had the power to do so, and not providing the back-fill of support services and permanent housing was no longer a barrier, the quite literally can’t not.

The rodeway to hell is paved with good intentions.

The only recourse is for Metro to not have these powers.

When providing criticism about unhoused friends being relocated temporarily as the result of political expediency, systemic actors like OHS and the mayors office are known to respond to the effect of: “So you want people to live outside? Wow, seems like we’re the only ones who REALLY care for these people.”

A Metro Council Member told me that she believes some homeless serving nonprofits critique agencies like OHS because their business model depends on there being outdoor homelessness (as if OHS were on the verge of putting them out of business).

The poor shall always be with us. And the illogical bad take of the power blind will be right there beside them.

I’d like to offer a counter point to these narratives from politicians and bureaucrats who have been reaping the political rewards of circumventing best practices that have been shown to work in cities we claim to be emulating:

“Respectfully, shut the fuck up. You are too intelligent to not understand how short term ‘wins’ can break the system by which long term positive change can happen. And if you don’t understand that, you’re not the right person for this job.”

I have said throughout this piece that it is “Metro” that has done these things, and a logical question is “who at Metro did these things?” In some ways, it’s very easy to point to the people who have pushed for these changes, whose names are on resolutions and who advocated for particular system tweaks.

But at the same time, it isn’t clear that this wouldn’t have happened anyway without those people committing those particular acts. It’s very plausible other people, seeing the systemic benefits of consolidation, would have pushed for the same thing. So in a sense it is “Metro Nashville” as an entity, an organism, acting by and through people temporarily holding positions of authority within it, that has captured and colonized a system once capable of holding it mildly accountable. Expanding outward while accumulating powers and abilities to achieve its goals, that’s the nature of many organisms. It isn’t that remarkable really.

There’s actually more to unpack on the side of the Continuum of Care (CoC).

Why was the CoC so susceptible to infiltration? How were its boundaries not clearly defined enough to see the risk of giving so much power over to an entity that has unavoidable conflicts of interest? Why is its culture one that tolerates the expanding suffering of the powerless they serve to keep peace with powerful public servants? How many organizations in the CoC prioritized predictable payroll for their staff over actually serving their clients, incentivizing them to turn a blind eye to obvious and dramatic mismanagement of public resources?

There are few who come out of this without a black eye.

But there are some who never even threw a punch.

The Bargain Comes Due

The Continuum of Care has a choice to make. Keep enabling a structure that lets Metro rewrite best practices every time there’s a parade, a political fire, or a PR crisis — or demand the other half of the bargain.

I would love to hear them say:

“If you want the authority to clear encampments, then show us the housing. Show us the rent checks. Show us the contracts for care. Because until then, not only are people not moving — we’re not moving either.”

We need leaders who understand that the war on the unhoused isn’t a historical footnote — it’s happening now. And they need to be on the right side the fight. Consolidating power doesn’t lift communities. It shields institutions.

The time for polite requests is over. Petitions have been ignored. Peaceful protests have been punished. If those tasked with supporting the unhoused won’t say it, I will:

“You want to move someone from their home?
Then show me the housing.
Or I’m not moving either.”

What’s amazing is that there are no expensive consulting reports that need to be written. There’s no lengthy community engagement process that need to checked off before change can be scheduled to be planned to be considered. Nashville just needs to actually follow the design principles of a Continuum of Care that actually work.

  • Voluntary Governance Over Coercion
    CoCs should foster alignment through consent, not control — emphasizing mutual respect, shared norms, and non-punitive collaboration.

  • Truth-Telling as System Health
    Honest feedback, grievance processes, and dissent should be normalized as mechanisms of system learning, not threats to authority.

  • Decisions That Honor Complexity
    Avoid binary thinking; prioritize reflective timing, layered participation, and outcomes that emerge iteratively from collective wisdom.

  • Leadership as a Contextual Role
    Influence should flow where it’s most needed — elevating both technical expertise and lived legitimacy, regardless of hierarchy.

  • Cultural Meaning as Infrastructure
    Systems need more than policy — they need rituals, symbols, and shared narratives that reinforce identity and ethical direction.

  • Dynamic Circulation of Power and Resources
    Justice depends on flow — of housing, funding, data, and authority — not accumulation. Deep prosperity lives in redistribution.

  • Nested Stewardship Across Scales
    Local decisions should reflect broader collective care — linking street-level service to regional, national, and moral responsibilities.

Political winds and the god complexes of unelected bureaucrats aren’t unstoppable forces.

But the solidarity of people who know they’re on the right side of justice?

That’s an immovable object.
