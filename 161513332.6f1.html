Housing Last

Part 1

On March 31, Nashville’s Office of Homeless Services (OHS) informed the media that they would be closing Old Tent City, Nashville’s largest and oldest encampment of unhoused people. Several hours later they reached out to its residents and their elected officials to let them know their plan.

That day, at least 120 people were informed that for their home, Old Tent City, it was closing time. They weren’t guaranteed a new home, but they couldn’t stay here.

“Old Tent City” is a camp ground-esque riverside stretch of road running along the I-24 bridge on the West Bank of the Cumberland.

The land is owned by Metro Nashville, with the exception of just beneath I-24 which is controlled by TDOT. The 24 acre lot is unused public property, but has been earmarked for an as-yet unfunded “Wharf Park”. With a price tag estimated at $80M, Wharf Park is described in Metro Parks planning documents as “an opportunity to create great synergies between water-based program[sic], the site and the community.”

“Housing surges” is the new term of art for “encampment closures”. The switch is a typical enough liberal euphemism, refocusing on what is purportedly being provided over what is being taken away.

This type of homeless encampment closure, rebranded as “Housing Surges”, focuses on the most visible unhoused residents — often living in tight-knit communities in structures they’ve spent years building. For reasons that haven’t been made public, their status as living in these encampments allows them to take up shelter spots that would otherwise go to people who are empirically more at-risk.

Nashville’s broader homelessness support ecosystem uses a vulnerability scoring system for unhoused residents to determine whose is most at risk by living outdoors. It’s an important tool since the number of people needing a shelter is often three to five times higher than the number of available beds. But these scores seem to be set aside when a camp needs to be closed, with beds going unused while OHS targets these populations for removal.

The practice drew scrutiny at a Public Health and Safety Committee meeting last October.

It’s hard not to wonder what role Metro’s ambitions for the land they are living on plays into prioritizing who gets to sleep indoors. And it’s also hard to reconcile the process with the best practices of the “Housing First” model that Metro has touted adopting after commissioning an expensive consultant generated report on its homelessness strategies.

Nashville’s Office of Homeless Services (OHS) makes it their official policy to keep encampment plans, and the underlying decision making process a secret, both from residents of the camps and lawmakers. This despite Housing First best practices from U.S. Interagency Council on Homelessness explicit request that agencies “have clear, publicized protocols to guide when and how encampments will be closed.”

The Office of Homeless Services, like many agencies focused on improving the wicked problem of homelessness in America, has adopted a model called “Housing First”. Housing first is a set of best practices that aims to reorient a city’s homelessness solutions towards providing unconditional, permanent housing as soon as possible.

It’s helpful to understand what Housing First by looking at what it isn’t.

“Housing First” runs contrary to the assumptions and belief systems that many people and organizations who serve the unhoused have built into their worldviews and policies.

Housing First is grounded in the radical (yet deeply evidence-based) notion that the most important resource that unhoused people are missing is, well, a house.

Additional services, like additional recovery, spiritual coaching, cognitive behavioral therapy, job training – these are all vital aspects to keep people in housed, health, and whole. But, research tells us, the first thing people need is a permanent place to call home.

An opposite of this model is the Salvation Army (SA). SA centers sometimes do provide “permanent” group housing, but this housing is conditional, the antithesis of the “housing first” modus operandi. To maintain residency at the Salvation Army, participants often have to engage in Christian oriented “work therapy” program that requires up to 40-hours of unpaid labor a week and other mandatory programming.

The Salvation Army one of the most powerful players in the housing space, including here in Nashville. The flaws in this mode of operating have not only observed by researchers, academics working on understanding social problems, often from an agnostic or even atheist perspective. In fact, most powerful criticism of this approach, criticism that it neither leads to very positive material outcomes nor spiritual ones, comes from a theologian longtime Salvation Army insider.

In his thesis for a Masters of Theology from the Melbourne College of Divinity, Jason Davies-Kildea, a theologian and former Salvation Army officer who, after 18 years of involvement and over a decade in social services, critically examined the Army’s emphasis on spiritual over material salvation — and how that is a false dichotomy that provider neither lasting material nor spiritual benefits.

The Salvation Army’s fixation, like many evangelical organizations, on prioritizing spiritual growth for the afterlife allegedly “creates a missional dichotomy rooted in dualism; it sets ‘spiritual’ work over and above efforts to change conditions in this world.”

The popular view in The Salvation Army which sees salvation almost exclusively as an individual matter creates unhelpful barriers to wider social change. John Drane comments on the negative effects of this tendency when he says that “To view sin in exclusively personal terms is theologically shallow and inevitably leads to a deviant view of salvation, with the accompanying conclusion that the gospel is a commodity to be marketed, rather than an all-embracing truth that challenges the whole of life.”

Davis-Kildea talks about how this emphasis on personal sin over broader systemic inequities gives permission for people who view themselves as saving others to ignore more complex forms of sin. The sins of, say, a dysfunctional social services ecosystem. The sins of labor market exploitation. The sins of societies that value the comfort of the already comfortable over the security of the most vulnerable.

The reality of human need can be created by structural sin, just as it can by individual sin. It can be inflicted upon people through dysfunctional relationships, just as it can by intentional human evil or unavoidable natural disaster. Our view of salvation needs to be wide enough to encompass all of these situations – and it needs to affect this world, not just the next.
