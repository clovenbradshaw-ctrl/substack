Why We Should Study Larry Ellison

The psychology of history’s “great men” is rarely relevant because it teaches us useful lessons about how we can be more impactful. The circumstances that let them rise to power are not ones we can replicate, and their characters are often beyond the bounds of any morality we’d want to live by.

But the inner lives of the powerful is relevant to learn for another reason. When people gain great control over others, they extend their psyches across much of humanity, and their chaotic inner realms recursively design a terrifying psychic landscape we have to navigate.

When the powerful control our reality while not controlling themselves, we find ourselves, in our waking lives, moving within their dreams and nightmares. Understanding their psychology is never a master class in leadership, it is more a form of moral geography — we must learn to circumnavigate their anxieties in order to live.

Or we can rattle our cages loud enough to awaken them into the real.

It’s simple. Everyone who moves to Nashville changes its cultural fabric, for better or worse. What will it mean when Larry Ellison and Oracle move here, along with their “8,500” employees, bringing with them their contracts with virtually every part of our country’s surveillance industrial complex?

Tennessee’s gamble is that subsidies and corporate favor will yield growth, not authoritarianism, or perhaps that authoritarianism will yield safer streets.

But the stakes seem even higher: whoever controls the database controls the world — or so the TechnoFutilist believes. They are wrong in this assumption. But we will be living in a world designed by this belief for a long time to come.

Larry Ellison’s Psychology: From Abandonment to Control

The title of the first major book on Ellison, the one that pre-dated his authorized biography, is based on a joke that is alleged to have gone around Silicon Valley in the early ‘90s: “What’s the difference between God and Larry Ellison? God does not think he’s Larry Ellison.” The book did not seem to depict Ellison as he saw himself, and shortly after he co-authored an authorized biography on himself.

Larry Ellison’s authorized biographer makes the case that his fixation with control has roots in childhood trauma.

Born in New York City in August 1944—just before totalitarian fascism’s futile campaign to control the narrative of world history would finally surrender in Western Europe—Lawrence Joseph Ellison was given up for adoption to relatives. He was raised in Chicago by a harsh adoptive father and a doting adoptive mother (his biological great-aunt, Lillian, who died an early death while Larry was in college).

At age twelve, he learned the truth of his adoption, a revelation that appears to affected him deeply. His biography frames it as a wound that, among other things, forged his obsession with domination. Ellison describes the ethos he brought to Oracle: “We pick our enemies very carefully. It helps us focus.”

To an outside observer like me, the aesthetic of minimalism and control appeals to someone desperately seeking order after a chaotic childhood. It provides an honorable narrative for behavior that might otherwise seem pathological—he’s not a ruthless businessman, he’s not a sociopath, he’s a modern warrior following ancient codes.

His authorized 2003 biography is entitled ‘Softwar: An Intimate Portrait of Larry Ellison and Oracle.” It’s a genuinely fascinating book if only for its formatting. Written by Matthew Symonds after exhaustive interviews of Ellison, the book has footnotes by Ellison, who adds commentary — sometimes correcting Symonds, other times acknowledging Symond’s assessment is right and he initially was wrong.

It’s a strangely self-aware, even meta-aware book, revealing a mind fixated on comprehending everything, including himself. But his view on himself is perhaps from such a height that not only do the trees get lost for the forest, but the forest fades into the landscape and then into the background of the world itself.

Three core traumas seem to inform almost everything about Ellison. The first was the revelation of his adoption.

Until his twelfth birthday, it had never occurred to Larry Ellison that he was adopted. “I can remember exactly where I was. I was standing in the doorway between the dining room and the kitchen, and my father tells me I’m adopted. That was it. They didn’t give me any details. It was like ‘Tonight we’re having meat loaf, and, by the way, you’re adopted.’ It was many years down the road before I found out that my biological mother was my mother’s niece.”

Softwar, pp 353

The second thing that seems to have shaped him was the harsh treatment of his adopted father who did not hide his displeasure of having to raise a rebellious child that was not his own. Much of Ellison’s quest to accomplish more than others was to prove to his father he’d amount to something.

Not surprisingly, Lou Ellison concluded early on that Larry’s failure to comply with authority would be his undoing. Sadly, he could never resist any opportunity to remind his son that he would amount to nothing. Yet it would be wrong to say that as a boy Ellison was unloved. Lillian [his adopted mother] never gave him less than unconditional affection, and Doris, nineteen years older, was full of tough, wisecracking love for her wayward [adopted] baby brother.

pp. 357

And third, the loss of his adopted mother to cancer led him to drop out of college. His reflections on her death gets tied up in his mind with his quest for knowledge, and his rage at being boxed in by society’s normative values and obligations.

A pattern seen both in the 1997 book and the 2003 book is that it is difficult for Ellison to speak about himself and his emotions without jumping into larger complaints about the world, the systems that comprise it, and his thoughts on how it should be different.

Here is how he explains the impact of his adopted mother’s death.

“I went to the University of Illinois for a couple of years, but during finals week my mother passed away, so I just packed up and left without taking exams. I didn’t like exams or the university or the town it was in, for that matter. I recall one exam where I just sat there for an hour, unable to begin because I was so angry that I had to spend the next three hours writing answers to questions I cared nothing about. I never had the discipline to do things I didn’t like. The classes at the University of Chicago were better, but I had such a short attention span that it was simply impossible for me to finish anything. However, I was intrigued by physics because it seemed to answer the most basic questions about our world. Like a lot of young people, I was looking for answers.”

Softwar, pp. 69

Addressing the same topic in the 1997 book, he briefly references his mother’s death before jumping into a diatribe about Bill Clinton’s futile attempts to overhaul the nation’s healthcare system.

That the topic of physics, the Newtonian world where cause and effect and the future, at least for non-complex systems, can be foretold, and the past cleanly deduced, comes up when talking about his grief, says volumes. It makes all the sense that this preference for simple systems leads to his disdain for the messiness of political realities. Ellison scoffs at attempts to overhaul things like healthcare, regardless if you could save many lives untold grief.

If Ellison has a chief rival in the IT business, it is not Microsoft or Google or IBM, it is the very concept of complexity — systems and occurrences beyond the scope of human understanding. Like death.

His fixation on the consolidation of information and control are a fairly obvious symptom of his overwhelming sense of powerlessness and quest for esteem and love. But the pathology is even easier to see in Ellison’s philanthropic ambitions than in his professional will to power.

Ellison shares an obsession with longevity and anti-aging with notable contemporaries including Vladimir Putin, Xi Jinping, Peter Thiel of Palantir (a collaborator with Oracle on government surveillance projects), and Jeff Bezos (a chief rival of Oracle).

Even the convicted billionaire sex trafficker Jeffrey Epstein, not a friend or associate of Ellison by any public accounts, invested in transhumanism and genetic research—though his focus was on eugenics and to impregnate up to twenty women at a time via IVF to propagate his DNA, rather than life extension.

So archetypical is Ellison’s fixation on hoarding wealth and power to stave off death that PreachingToday.com has a templatized sermon built around his inability to accept life’s one inevitability. Yet Ellison’s quest, while still driven by anxieties around mortality, differs from the autocratic longevity pursuits of Putin and Xi, and bears little resemblance to Epstein’s darker eugenic ambitions.

Ellison’s desire to conquer death has a nobler, more relatable patina when compared with them.

On page 321 of Softwar, Ellison tells his biographer what he’d rather be doing with his life, and includes a footnote expanding on his reasons.

“If you want to understand why people do things, then take a course in evolutionary psychology. What we want to do with our lives is the most important question we all have to answer. So if I could do anything at all with my life, what would I do? I’d cure cancer.⁹ I’d much rather cure cancer than become the richest guy in the world. Why? Because I’d be a much happier person if I cured cancer. Why? Because I’d be loved. Why? Because people don’t want to suffer painful death. Why? You know why.”

⁹ LE [Larry Ellison] writes: Yes, I know that cancer is a collection of diseases and it is very unlikely that there will ever be a single cure. Still, my mother died of cancer and I want to cure cancer. That’s where most of my money and all of my time after Oracle are likely to go.

To date, Ellison has been (for a short time last month) the richest guy in the world.

His attempts to cure cancer has not, as yet, panned out.

In 2024, his cancer fighting start-up Project Ronin (named after the masterless class of samurai Rōnin) shuttered after middling client acquisition of its AI oncology services. He has many irons in the cancer fighting, longevity fire, most of which operate with the Silicon Valley move-fast and break things mentality that tends to be at-odds with the methodical empiricism that medical research requires.

It is unclear if any of his efforts to defeat cancer, or death itself, have made him feel loved. Or happy.

Now is as good a time as any to acknowledge that I deeply relate to this impulse to comprehend all things as a way to quell the demons of terror inside me. I have a complex PTSD diagnosis, and coming out of a very turbulent adolescence, I sought refuge in the Liberal Arts — western society’s wisdom tradition grounded in reason’s dominion over the unruly wild horses of our emotions.

I have flung myself into “theories of everything” like Integral Theory and MetaModernism, and traveled to far away countries trying to find people who definitively “get it”, it being everything. I didn't find them. But the search wasn't in vain.

The path of rational negation teaches you that there is no one thing to “get” — the path is about becoming transparent, revealing, as Dolly sang, that all things, in their own way, are beautiful.

Ellison is not the first person in the Western canon to try to control chaos through centralization and order — it's actually kind of the overall VIBE of western culture. And to a degree, it works. Until it doesn't. And then it fails spectacularly.

The lessons you need to learn about this will not be found in leadership text books or systems theory papers or biographies of tech gurus. The lessons vital for life on the limits of rationality are contained inside everyone, inside you, and it's only in integrating our traumas, and holding suffering and joy in equal measure, that we understand how to be a true master of the universe.

True masters of the universe do not constrain the world. They do not box it into rows and columns, nodes and edges. True masters allow it to unfold around them, in all of its complexity, their authority is self evident, their place in the great chain of being is unquestionable.

But what makes Ellison feel safe,– what seems like a solution to suffering, grief and powerlessness — it may not align with what makes the rest of us feel free and safe. And the thing about ‘shadow presidents’ is, no one elected them.

The Ersatz Samurai

Ellison, the computer programmer raised in Chicago, at some point developed a deep affinity for feudal Japanese culture, and deeply identifies with the lifestyle of a samurai.

Ellison hasn’t gone on record very much about what being a corporate samurai means to him. But for some context, we can borrow from another modern tech tycoon who shares the same affinity for the far east’s knightly class.

Ben Horowitz, co-founder of the venture capital house Andreessen-Horowitz, opened a keynote in 2017 with an excerpt from the bushido code of the samurai. It is something of a mission statement for the London-born computer science major.

“As long as you keep death in mind at all times, you will fulfill the ways of loyalty and familial duty. You will also avoid myriad evils and calamities, you will be physically sound and healthy, and you will live a long life. What is more, your character will improve and your virtue will grow.”

Horowitz authored a book on building effective corporate cultures, drawing inspiration from historical examples such as samurai traditions. During his promotional tour, he shared eyepoppingly violent insights about which leaders offer valuable lessons for those seeking to shape their company’s culture.

One of the more striking examples of that is Sun Tzu running a concubine army and changing the culture around [military] drills in an instant by cutting people’s heads off. It’s very possible. It’s a matter of technique and commitment to change.

Fortune

Ellison spent nearly a decade and over $200 million building a Japanese-style estate in Woodside, California—complete with hand-crafted Kyoto-style houses, arched bridges, cherry trees, and a five-acre lake designed according to Zen landscaping principles. His yachts—Ronin, Katana, and Musashi—are each named after legendary Japanese swords or warriors, mirroring his view of business as a form of disciplined combat.

Ellison also built one of America’s premier private collections of Edo-period art, showcased publicly in In the Moment: Japanese Art from the Larry Ellison Collection at the Asian Art Museum, where he financed the restoration of lacquerware and samurai armor.

Ellison has named both a yacht and a company after the Bushido term “rōnin”. A rōnin is explained on Wikipedia as follows.

According to the Bushido Shoshinshu (the “Code of the Warrior”), a samurai was supposed to commit seppuku (also harakiri, “belly cutting”, a form of ritual suicide) upon the loss of his master. One who chose not to honor the code was “on his own” and was meant to suffer great shame. The undesirability of rōnin status was mainly a discrimination imposed by other samurai and by daimyō, the feudal lords…
Because the former samurai could not legally take up a new trade, or because of pride were loath to do so, many rōnin looked for other ways to make a living with their swords.

The rōnin often became, in western parlance, a mercenary. The Oracle co-founder seems to identify with the masterless class of samurai, a lone wolf who does not acknowledge a higher authority, but fights when and where the opportunity provides or survival requires.

There’s something both nerdy and unsettling about purportedly dedicating oneself to the antiquated ethics of a foreign culture, one that was contemporaneous with America’s founding colonies. It would be as if a Japanese business person were fixated on aesthetics of the Pilgrims, fencing, and pistol dueling.

To an outside observer like me, the aesthetic of minimalism and control appeals to someone desperately seeking order after a chaotic childhood. It provides an honorable narrative for behavior that might otherwise seem pathological—he’s not a ruthless businessman, he’s not a sociopath, he’s a modern warrior following ancient codes.

Yet, it’s worth mentioning, samurais spent years training under masters to acquire the moral code and mental fortitude required to live out the ethics of their era. The Silicon Valley adoption of this ethos appears more like an excuse of behavior outside of contemporary ethics than a moral center earned through discipline, mindfulness, mentorship, and more than a little zen buddhism.

Ellison, it might be said, sees business as a form of war conducted with intellect rather than swords. His ruthless determinism fuses trauma, philosophy, and power — an ethos where compassion is weakness, and success, like survival, is an act of pure will.

Each man is his own ancestor

Oracle weathered a business crisis in the early ‘90s that nearly collapsed the company. Coming out the other side of that more resilient, Ellison felt he was prepared to learn the truth about his parentage, something he has as yet put off.

“After Oracle’s crisis, looking into the abyss and surviving, I felt emotionally strong enough to take a more realistic look at myself. I was tired of striving to be the person I thought I should be. If I was to have any chance at happiness, I had to understand and accept who I really was. That required me to ask some questions about my past and live with the answers, regardless of what they turned out to be.”

pp. 354

Symonds reveals to the reader Ellison’s true parentage:

His nineteen-year-old mother, Florence Spellman, had fallen in love with a handsome, broad-shouldered third-generation Italian who was a pilot in the air force. By the time Florence knew she was pregnant, her lover had been posted abroad. What happened to him, despite Ellison’s efforts in later life to find out, remains a mystery.”

pp. 354

Nature and nurture, such as they are, combined in ways human (perhaps all too human for his liking) within Ellison. His adopted father’s attitude towards life appears to have shaped, in a recursive way, Ellison’s view of the world. Louis Ellison, a Russian Jew who emigrated to escape abject poverty in Crimea, patriotism for the nation that adopted him colored much of his perceptions of America

In Softwar Ellison described how he and his father differed when it came to authority:

“[My adopted father’s] worldview went way beyond Nathan Hale’s, ‘My country right or wrong.’ He believed that our country’s officials, and authority figures in general, were always right and should be obeyed without question. The governor, the mayor, the police are in their jobs for a reason, he’d say; ‘They know things you don’t.’

pp. 355

The younger Ellison, an iconoclast who bristled when authority would so much as require him to take a school test he wasn’t interested in, rejected all deference to authority.

“I thought, ‘Sure, they’re brilliant, just like my teachers.’ I understood his gratitude toward the United States, but I couldn’t accept his blind faith in authority figures. In fact, I had the opposite point of view. I questioned everything. It’s like Mark Twain says, ‘What is an expert anyway? Just some guy from out of town.’ This lack of respect for authority made it just about impossible for me to get along with my father.”

pp. 355

But like virtually all men, the hard line in the sand he draws between his nature and that of the father that nurtured him is fuzzier than he’d like to think.

When Ellison proposed creating a national registry of all citizens after 9/11, it might have been his father, Louis Ellison, rather than the tech magnate who asked: “Who’s ever heard of this information being misused by the government?”

And when it was leaked by Edward Snowden that the NSA was engaged in mass civilian surveillance, he called the revelation essentially a nothingburger.

He claimed that Snowden had yet to identify a single person who had been “wrongly injured” by the NSA’s data collection. “We shouldn’t ban the gathering of data because it might be misused.”

Ellison seems to self identify as the rebel, the anti-authoritarian, the disruptor of the status quo and a questioner of authority.

But his actions, the systems he builds, it all supports the same hierarchies that his father unquestioningly praised. And, ironically, the hierarchies that caused his father to flee to America. From the very first client of Oracle, the CIA, to the billions in contracts with government agencies and intelligence services, Ellison has used technology to entrench the power imbalance of the few, who use his tools to observe and control the many.

Ellison is only the latest in the eternal line of men, blinded by their own ambition, who, like Oedipus, don’t see how they are unconsciously repeating the acts of their fathers — convincing themselves they are their own author, pretending they sprung to life fully formed, engendered only by their will to power.

It is no coincidence that orphans and adoptees comprise many of the protagonists of western hero’s quest journeys. The search for parentage is easy shorthand for the drama of unfolding one’s identity and transcending the binary of self/other.

Later in life, Ellison did come to know more about his biological parentage. Yet still he seems to resist asking what it is that brings him fear and trembling. The Greeks knew that more than our parents, biological or adopted, it is our wounds that birth us. And we can feel that by healing them, we may kill what makes us who we are, causing an ego death we can’t see beyond.

Oedipus, the adopted orphan, tried, like Ellison, to avoid his fate of becoming his own father. Oedipus was warned what the rising sun of insight would bring him, the rosy fingered dawn of catastrophic self-knowledge.
