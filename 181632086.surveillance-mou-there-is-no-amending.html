1. THIS REQUIRES A PUBLIC HEARING—NONE HAS BEEN SCHEDULED

Metro Code 13.08.080(C) requires Council approval “by a resolution adopted after a public hearing” before:

  • Entering agreements with private entities to use surveillance technology

  • Accepting state funds for surveillance technology

  • Acquiring new surveillance technology

What qualifies as “surveillance technology”?

Any electronic device, hardware, or software capable of collecting, recording, analyzing, or sharing information associated with specific individuals or groups.

This explicitly includes:

  • Video/audio monitoring technology and surveillance cameras

  • Software and hardware tools (like LeoSight and Fivecast)

  • Social media monitoring software

This MOU triggers all three requirements:

  • ✓ Private entity (NDP)

  • ✓ State funds ($15 million)

  • ✓ New surveillance software (LeoSight, Fivecast, noise cameras)

No public hearing has been scheduled. This isn’t rocket science.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“We’ll hold the public hearing when NDP actually purchases the technology and donates it to Metro.”

THE REBUTTAL:

This MOU already triggers three separate public hearing requirements:

(C)(2) Entering into an agreement with a private entity to use surveillance technology (the MOU itself); (C)(3) Accepting state funds for surveillance technology (the $15M grant); (C)(4) Acquiring new surveillance technology (LeoSight, Fivecast, noise cameras listed in Exhibit A)

The statute requires the hearing “prior to” these actions—not after.

Additionally: There’s no guarantee NDP will formally “donate” anything. In New Orleans, surveillance purchased by private entities was shared informally with police through access agreements—no formal donation ever occurred. The cameras never appeared on city property rolls, avoiding accountability requirements.

Council cannot approve surveillance funding now on the promise of a hearing later.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“The public hearing happened during the December 4 meeting when people spoke during public comment.”

THE REBUTTAL:

Public comment ≠ public hearing.

BL2017-646 requires a formal public hearing noticed as such, not general public comment. Council held a formal public hearing for FUSUS (software platform, similar to LeoSight) in November 2024 before the December 3 vote. That’s the precedent.

Council’s own practice proves a public hearing is required.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“The title of 13.08.080 says ‘onto public rights-of-way’—this doesn’t apply because it’s not physical cameras on poles.”

THE REBUTTAL:

Contradicted by Council’s own precedent.

FUSUS was a software platform integrating existing cameras—not physical deployment. Council still required a public hearing under 13.08.080(C). If it applied to FUSUS software, it applies to LeoSight/Fivecast software.

Council cannot selectively enforce its own surveillance ordinance.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“We can hold the public hearing later, after approval.”

THE REBUTTAL:

Legally impossible.

BL2017-646: “approval...by a resolution adopted after a public hearing, shall be required prior to...”

The statute requires hearing → then vote. Not vote → then hearing. This isn’t discretionary timing—it’s mandatory sequencing.

The grant was awarded to Nashville District Management Corporation (NDMC), not NDP. These are two separate entities. They have different EIN numbers and their budgets work very differently in a way that is extremely important here.

Metro Ordinance BL2017-580 explicitly requires: “The annual budget [of NDMC] shall be reviewed and approved by the Metropolitan Council”

Council already has line-item budget control over NDMC:

✓ Can reject specific surveillance purchases

✓ Can require policy changes before approving expenditures

✓ Can withhold funds until compliance

✓ Can vote down entire budget and demand revisions

No MOU is necessary to restrict how NDMC spends this grant—Council already possesses that statutory authority.

The only reason to route funds through NDP instead of the actual grant recipient is to eliminate Council’s existing oversight.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

Backwards logic.

Council currently has 100% budget control over NDMC (the grant recipient) via BL2017-580.

You’re trading statutory oversight for a voluntary agreement that can be amended without Council approval. The goal is not oversight, its streamlining surveillance outside of public approval processes.

Missing the point. The problem isn’t if NDP gets the money. No one doubts that is who the DMC will give it to (they have the same President/CEO). The question is whether the money goes straight to NDP or through the DMC first.

“Collaboration” ≠ “routing 100% of funds through the private entity.”

NDMC is the BID entity. Funds should flow to NDMC (where Council has budget authority), with NDP providing services as a vendor under NDMC supervision.

Every other Tennessee city receiving these grants maintained governmental control. Only Nashville is privatizing it.

Ask the sponsors: “If your goal is to limit how these funds are used, why bypass the entity where you already have 100% budget approval authority?”

5. TENNESSEE LAW ALLOWS WARRANTLESS DATA SHARING—AND BANS LOCAL GUARDRAILS

Tennessee Information Protection Act (TIPA) § 47-18-3208(a)(3), effective July 1, 2025:

Allows data controllers/processors to “cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws”

No warrant, no subpoena, no oversight required.

TIPA includes preemption clause: Local municipalities are explicitly banned from creating guardrails to prevent this data sharing.

TIPA Section 4:

“This act supersedes and preempts any conflicting provisions of any public or private act and laws, ordinances, resolutions, regulations, or the equivalent adopted by a home rule municipality, county, including a metropolitan government, or city regarding the processing of personal data by controllers or processors.”

This means: Third-party surveillance tools like Fivecast and LeoSight can share data with law enforcement of their own volition, and Tennessee law prevents Council from stopping it.

Even if Council adds restrictions to the resolution, state law allows vendors to ignore them when sharing with law enforcement.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“We’ll add language to the resolution prohibiting data sharing without Council approval.”

THE REBUTTAL:

State law preempts local restrictions.

Even if Council adds restrictions to RS2025-1659, vendors (LeoSight, Fivecast) can invoke TIPA § 47-18-3208(a)(3) to share data with law enforcement anyway.

State law explicitly prohibits local governments from restricting this data sharing.

Council cannot override state statute with a resolution.

7. AMENDMENTS TO THE RESOLUTION CAN’T CHANGE THE MOU

The MOU is a binding legal agreement already signed by Mayor O’Connell and Tom Turner (NDP CEO).

The MOU text cannot be changed without both parties signing a new version.

By reports, there has been no movement on securing new signatures.

Any amendment to the resolution that doesn’t also amend the MOU itself is legally meaningless—it only changes Metro’s authorization language while leaving the operative agreement unchanged.

This inverts proper democratic process: Executives should negotiate agreements under Council direction, not sign them first and ask permission later.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“We’ve added strong amendments to the resolution that address these concerns.”

THE REBUTTAL:

The resolution isn’t the operative document.

The MOU is the binding legal agreement between Metro and NDP.

The resolution is Metro Council’s authorization to enter that agreement.

Amending the authorization doesn’t change the agreement.

Example:

Resolution amendment: “NDP shall not purchase LeoSight”

MOU Exhibit A: “LeoSight - $X amount”

Which governs? The signed MOU between the parties.

WHAT THEY’LL SAY:

“The Mayor will sign an amended MOU if we pass amendments.”

THE REBUTTAL:

No evidence of new signatures.

Ask sponsors: “Do you have a signed amended MOU reflecting your changes?”

If not, you’re voting on hope, not binding agreement.

Proper process:

  1. Council negotiates terms

  2. Executives draft MOU reflecting those terms

  3. Both parties sign

  4. Council votes on the actual signed agreement

This process inverted all of that.
