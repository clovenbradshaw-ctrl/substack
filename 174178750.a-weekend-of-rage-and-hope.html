I had a busy Saturday. A protest. Stopping by a bench building project. Helping a friend move. And finally, after 35 years, seeing Weird Al Yankovic live in concert.

Last Thursday I told you about a protest against Avelo Airlines that was to take place yesterday, and it went great. Dozens of you made it out to spread a message that even the trucks, buses, and travelers driving by understood at a glance: no one should be profiting off of our government’s abdication of their duties to hold up the constitution.

Media coverage of the event has been splashed across regional outlets around the country, and the coverage made it clear: this wasn't a group of radicals — it was a group of reasonable people demanding that the rule of law flow both directions, and asking our fellow citizens to vote with their dollars and not support ripping apart families and the Constitution at the same time.

An immigration lawyer I’m close with texted me this this morning. A centrist Democrat, former military officer, the scales are falling from their eyes.

There are two levels of American reality. One that we pretend we’re in and the one that others have to live in.

Our immigration policy is a patchwork of laws and regulations that go back a hundred years but which no longer serve the national interest. We choose not to address that.

Instead, ICE has transformed into our first police force now officially exempt from any restraint as they anonymously pursue anyone appearing to be an immigrant. They literally grab people off the streets, break down doors, shatter windows, and beat their subjects into submission. No notice, no warrant, no rights.

Local and state police officers often are given no choice but to identify and stop these human targets.

Immigration courts , as part of the Department of Justice, are now highly restricted, conflicted, and have become acolytes.

Immigrant detention centers are specifically designed to be inhumane.

For the 20 million non-citizen immigrants in the United States (more than half of which have been here more than 15 years), the norms, protections, incentives and human values of our American democracy are being instantly revoked as they are brutally ripped out of the national fabric.

When this supporting layer of our society is finally shredded and mostly thrown away, our country will finally settle back down, only to realize what we thought we once had created is forever gone.

Music Sit-y

On an even more local and community scale, there’s an amazing project that vastly expanded its reach this weekend. You may have noticed benches popping up across the city over the past month. Prior to yesterday, there were about a dozen placed intentionally in areas where the city has either removed public benches, or had neglected to place any near bus stops.

These benches are part of a collectivist project spearheaded by Pastor Robin L. Owen, who you can follow on Substack as “Jesus Urbanist.” Here’s her post about this weekend’s bench build.

Her post concludes with a declaration of what the point of the project is, signed by a variety of community leaders.

Our Public Statement & Demands

… Metro Nashville has time and again shown that the well-being of its residents is not its concern; recently, the city has worked to be actively hostile to many who call this place home. For years, the city has systematically removed benches from public spaces and bus stops. Two-thirds of our city voted for Choose How You Move. We are overwhelmingly in favor of building public infrastructure like benches, but the Nashville Department of Transportation has actively removed benches instead.

We’re sick of hostile design. After benches were removed from Korean Veterans Boulevard in August, we came together to work to resolve this issue of basic human dignity. We are a broad coalition: advocates for the homeless, transit users, cyclists, people of faith, clergy, non-profit workers, parents, artists, new and lifelong Nashvillians from all parts of the city…

Some Context

As you might be aware, the Nashville Department of Transportation has been enacting a program of “hostile architecture” that pretty unambiguously targets our most vulnerable. Public benches are being removed from public spaces, like on Korean Veterans Blvd. in District 19, a few blocks from Broadway. The bench removal on KVB was, as per the Nashville Scene, “at the direction of NDOT, following collaboration with the Nashville Downtown Partnership and downtown Councilmember Jacob Kupin.”

The removal of the benches is purportedly for the sake of expanding “public art” in the form of brutalist concrete balls. It's a pretty dubious claim given that the Metro department in charge of public art, The Metro Arts Commission, was not given any advance notice or prior opportunity to give input — nor was any community notice given.

Involved in it appeared only to be the few, wealthy “stakeholders” that are considered almost exclusively for anything that happens downtown, a buzzword used by Council Members who don’t seem to realize how much they are telling on themselves by deferring to them when asked why they allow such things.

This would be one thing in an environment where Nashville had a well functioning homelessness system with places for unhoused people to be and receive services, but, if my previous articles and the agenda for this Tuesday’s Metro Internal Audit meeting is any indication, Metro has not been using its once-in-a-generation funding for homelessness effectively.

Here’s the thing: people shouldn't have to resort to sitting or sleeping in public spaces, including benches with nowhere else to go. But the solution is a well functioning homelessness service, not to remove access to places to sit.

Coming from a Mayor’s Office that campaigned on reforming the homelessness system to make it more accountable and effective, and whose primary accomplishment thus far has been transit reform, an agenda of stripping Nashville of its right to sit in public says a lot about the actual values at play in 1 Public Square.

These actions, combined with the administration's unwavering support for things like the FUSUS surveillance system, make sense only if their target audience is not the Nashvillians the Mayor spoke to at his victory party—those of us he asked to "Stay in Nashville."

They make much more sense if their purpose is pleasing stakeholders with access to political coffers and power players needed to perform centrist business-as-usual: the Nashville Downtown Partnership, major Trump booster Larry Ellison and his Oracle headquarters moving here. But the real problem is these times are not business-as-usual.

In stable times, centrist fence-sitting meant splitting the difference between elite machinations and human dignity, keeping everyone mildly unhappy. Now, that same approach amounts to collaboration with an occupying totalitarian force.

The vibe is: "We are shocked and horrified by what they're doing to our most vulnerable residents. But no, we will not be doing anything to slow their progress. And in fact, we will use this chaos to accumulate more powers and autonomy, since that seems like the vibe."

I've said this directly to Nashville's highest powers, though it's received like pearls before swine: When you accumulate power to remove the vulnerable from public spaces while dismissing oversight demands as coming from people who are "passionate, but not reasonable enough to be entrusted with real power," you're building your own trap.

When you have unchecked power over where people go, when you circumvent the checks and balances that restrain institutional power, you don't gain strength—you lose it, because once you have that power, you cannot say no when the demand comes from on-high to, metaphorically or quite possibly literally, put people on trains.

Something reassuring about the bench project this weekend, something that gives me hope, is that it was full of normal people.1 Normal, white, Christian, somewhat centrist people realizing: "Oh, this is not the system I thought it was. These are not the leaders we thought we had."

To the leaders in our city who are not living up to the values they were elected on, I have both warning and invitation.

The warning: The forces above you—federal and corporate—will never be satisfied. Shareholders always need more. Authoritarians are never appeased. Bullies who get lunch money come back for watches, then dignity, then neighbors.

The invitation: We who elected you can still offer trust. We're not playing power games—we actually want what we say we want. We speak to you openly, plainly, not in coded games of PR encrypted quid-pro-quo. That’s why we’re able to be funny, to speak off the cuff, admit when we’re wrong, and find solidarity where it’s least expected — because we’re being real. And that power we hold in our personalities, the power to hold sincerity and absurdity in equal measure, it will out compete hollow promises, consultant crafted political platforms, and fear every time.

The river of progress always arrives, eventually, at the ocean. Despite what dams are built by those afraid of change, using the inertia of human efforts to power the turbines of fear and war, the water always arrives at the ocean. When our routes are blocked by land, we become diffuse and dissipate into clouds, and fall from the skies, individuals returning back into the collective, droplets of rain returning to the ocean of being.

And unlike those that wish to quell progress, to halt time itself, to freeze the fear in their chest, the unfathomable terror of being a lone being against the world, we will never turn our backs on those who stand up for righteousness, even if they are late to the party.

But we will not sit idly by as our most vulnerable are sacrificed at the altar of "splitting the difference."

There's a Bible story that comes to mind. Two women claimed the same infant, and King Solomon presided over the dispute. To settle it, he announced he would split the baby in two. One woman agreed to this reasonable compromise. The other was horrified—knowing that splitting a baby's body in two is not recommended by any of the major parenting books—and said the other woman could have the child. She, of course, was the real mother.

Our leaders have been willing to split this city in two, calling it governance—cold to the fact that splitting a city split in two kills it. Yes, for a short time, it becomes a playground for the rich while the poor are told there is no room at the inn. But without the people who make the city possible, those Mayor O'Connell promised could "stay in Nashville," it will die. Like the false mother before Solomon, there is a class of people who would rather proudly carry around half a corpse than admit they are not the true parents of what makes this city thrive. They would rather rule over a dead thing they can possess than nurture a living thing they cannot control.

But here's what they don't understand: We're not waiting for our leaders to choose wisely. To come to Jesus and finally pick a side. We're already building the city we need—one bench, one protest, one act of solidarity at a time. The real parents of this city are in the streets, tools in hand, refusing to let our community be carved up for parts.

The question isn't whether our leaders will join us. It's whether they'll get out of the way.
