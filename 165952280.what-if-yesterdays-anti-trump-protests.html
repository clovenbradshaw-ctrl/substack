One figure who’s stayed with me ever since I first saw Hypernormalisation nearly a decade ago is Vladislav Surkov. He was a longtime Putin political strategist whose background was in the theatre, and he quite literally put the “theatre” into political theatre.

Surkov created a playbook and an ethos that have been essential for Putin’s “managed democracy”—a democracy in name only that allows just enough dissent to appear legitimate, just enough conflict to sustain itself. Surkov was valuable to Putin because he understood something most Cold War-era strategists did not: the psyops of the 20th century—flags, anthems, grand ideologies, propaganda posters—weren’t going to work on a 21st-century population raised on irony and advertising.

The problem Surkov faced was that, since the high points of Stalinist Russia, the psychology of the Russian people had changed. The people had become postmodern, but their politics had not.

Postmodernism is the idea that truth is always relative, that all stories are just stories, and that reality itself is often a performance. It emerged from deep skepticism toward the grand promises of modernity (science, political ideologies, and organized religion), and rather than try to repair the failures of those systems, it systematically critiqued it. And then critiqued those systems of critique, and mocked even the ambition to critique the system. In the postmodern view, there is no objective truth, just competing narratives, each as flawed and absurd as the last.

Decades of disillusionment, collapse, and corruption had trained the Russian populace in this sensibility. Saturated by Western media and countercultural narratives, they had learned to see through the simplistic binaries of good versus evil, East versus West. They had become too cynical to simply take orders from a strongman in a suit, waving a flag and invoking destiny.

But to Putin and Surkov, this widespread cynicism wasn’t seen as a sign of intellectual evolution or political maturity—it was a threat. They didn’t interpret it as society inching toward more functional, distributed decision-making. They saw it as a virus, a corrosive Western export. In the Putinist view, liberal democracy was not an ideal to strive for but a toxic pathogen, its “freedom” synonymous with chaos, fragmentation, and weakness.

So rather than try to reverse the public’s cynicism, Surkov built a system that weaponized it. This is where his strategy of управляемые конфликты—“managed conflict”—became vital for maintaining Putin’s grasp on power. And it sits at the heart of Adam Curtis’s “Hypernormalisation”: a world in which nothing is true, and everything is spectacle.

The playbook of Surkov’s “Managed Conflict” goes like this: Instead of cracking down on political dissidents or doing some CIA-inspired infiltration and neutralization of your opposition from within, fuel the opposition. Maybe you don’t formally reveal it, but you let it leak out. Not to show how masterful and powerful you are (though that’s a value-add), but more to just make any form of solidarity suspect.
