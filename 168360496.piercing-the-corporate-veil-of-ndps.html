In the wake of seemingly non-stop controversies from the Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP), for some reason residents and business owners of the Gulch are allegedly all very excited that the Metro Council is merging their “business improvement district” with that of the Central District — both to be overseen by NDP.

It’s not clear if the people living and working in these business districts (a designated area that opt-in via a one-time vote to pay extra taxes for services in their area) know what Council is unilaterally signing them up for by merging these areas and propping up NDP to gain even more power and revenue.

Last night’s Budget and Finance Committee meeting was a tense showdown between:

  • Nashville’s gilded elite that have been at the self-appointed helm of beautifying Nashville’s various business improvement districts since 1999, and

  • Council Members who mounted a strategic resistance with hardball questions that boiled down to: “wait, so you have like, no oversight on the use of public dollars? And you think that’s… good?”

Metro Council’s Attorney Margaret Darby confirmed what Council Member Sean Parker first raised at the last budget meeting, and I chased down last week through public records searches to fact check: Metro Council stopped approving the budgets for the Central Business Improvement District circa 2004. They left the Downtown Partnership with free reign for 21 years without virtually any oversight on these public funds, and Metro Finance has provided them unauthorized funds in excess of $50M.

While you might be tempted to say this, I will go ahead and say that I think that’s exactly what this is.

A quick diversion into corporate law theory.

Piercing the Corporate Veil: Potential Liability Exposure

"Piercing the corporate veil" is a legal doctrine that strips away the protection of corporate separateness when two entities are actually one organization pretending to be two. Tennessee courts will pierce the veil when entities are so intertwined they lack separate identity (Oceanics Schools v. Barbour, 112 S.W.3d 135 (Tenn. Ct. App. 2003)).

In their 2023 IRS filings, Nashville Downtown Partnership (NDP) and the District Management Corporation (DMC) are a textbook example. They don't just share some staff—they share everyone who matters:

Executive Staff Overlap

  • Thomas D. Turner – President & CEO / Executive Director of both organizations

  • Tamara Dickson – Vice President of Economic Development for both

  • Jeanette Barker – Vice President of Strategic Development for both

  • Jack Malone – Director of Cleaning & Transportation for both

  • David Corman – Director of Safety Services for both

Beyond staff, the board overlap is extensive: major property‑owner directors Will Freeman, Gabe Coltea, Janet Kurtz, Joe Bucher, Richard Fletcher, Kelly Hodges, and Michelle Myers serve on both the Nashville Downtown Partnership and the Nashville District Management Corporation boards.

This complete overlap is made deeply problematic as both entities to violate Metro Code § 2.177.070 for 21 years, spending tens of millions in public assessments without required Council approval. If a court pierces this fictional separation, DMC can no longer hide behind claims of being an "independent advisory board." Instead, it would potentially be fully liable for decades of ultra vires spending, breach of fiduciary duties, and intentional circumvention of statutory oversight, and also on the hook for whatever litigation comes out of the destruction of the Downtown Library.

As the Tennessee Supreme Court warned in Continental Bankers v. Bank of Alamo, 578 S.W.2d 625 (Tenn. 1979), courts will not allow the corporate form to be used as a "subterfuge" to evade legal obligations. Here, that's exactly what's happening—one organization wearing two hats to wield governmental power without governmental accountability.
