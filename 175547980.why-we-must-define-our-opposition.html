Despite what Sen. Blackburn would like you to think, the data is stark: conservative support for Israel’s war in Gaza is eroding. The most politically impactful criticism of how America’s strongest ally in the Middle East is conducting this war, two years in, is not coming from progressives or those of us on the Left who have always opposed it.

It’s now coming from the Right itself. And that shift creates both an opportunity and a danger.

Senator Blackburn’s message embodies a cruel, Manichean dualism that demands Americans choose absolute sides. In her framework, one cannot hold two truths simultaneously. We cannot acknowledge that harm is inflicted and received by human beings on both sides, most of whom genuinely perceive themselves as the aggrieved party. This binary thinking forecloses the nuanced understanding necessary for any meaningful path forward.

And for Nashville, which sits at the heart of the US conservative media ecosystem, it is exceptionally relevant to unpack the terms of why opposition is growing.

Because if we don’t make it explicit that our lack of support is because of the world historical humanitarian crisis unfolding at the hands of the Netanyahu government, others will cease the opportunity to spread antisemitic conspiracy theories — something already well underway in far right media.

How conservative sentiment has changed

Republican approval of Israeli military operations declined from 69% at the start of the conflict to 63% by mid-2025, while a Quinnipiac University poll found Republican sympathy for Israelis dropped 14 percentage points in a single year, from 78% in May 2024 to 64% in June 2025. The more significant shift appears among younger party members, where confidence in Prime Minister Netanyahu among Republicans under age 50 stands at just 32% compared to 70% among those over 50, revealing a 38-point generational divide.

The transformation among younger conservatives represents a fundamental reorientation. Republicans under 50 shifted from viewing Israel favorably by a 28-point margin in 2022—63% favorable versus 35% unfavorable—to an essentially even split in 2025, with 48% favorable and 50% unfavorable. This 30-point swing in three years crosses a critical threshold, moving from net positive to net negative territory. Unfavorable views among Republicans aged 18-49 have jumped from 35% to 50%, meaning half of younger Republicans now hold negative views of Israel.

In stark contrast, Republican support for Trump has remained remarkably stable and resilient. Trump’s approval among Republican voters stood at 91% in July 2025, with 85% of his 2024 voters continuing to approve of his job performance. While there have been modest declines from the 92-95% approval ratings at the start of his second term, these shifts pale in comparison to the collapse in Israel support. Even as Trump’s overall job approval has settled at around 40% nationally, this is driven almost entirely by near-universal Democratic opposition, while Republicans remain near-unanimous in their support at approximately 90%.

The divergence reveals something significant about the nature of conservative political identity. While Trump has maintained lockstep Republican support even through controversial policies and declining overall popularity, Israel has lost half of young Republicans in just three years. This suggests that support for Israel, once considered a foundational pillar of conservative foreign policy, may be more conditional and generationally fragile than support for Trump himself.

Even more striking than the numbers are the individuals breaking from consensus.

Representative Marjorie Taylor Greene, who filed a resolution to censure Representative Rashida Tlaib for criticizing Israel in October 2023, explicitly stated by July 2025 that both the October 7th attacks and the subsequent genocide, humanitarian crisis, and starvation in Gaza are horrific.

It’s the most truthful and easiest thing to say that Oct 7th in Israel was horrific and all hostages must be returned, but so is the genocide, humanitarian crisis, and starvation happening in Gaza.

Tucker Carlson, once a stalwart defender of Israeli policy, has shifted his position to the point where Megyn Kelly has been asked to publicly denounce him. These are not fringe voices on the right. These are figures closely aligned with the MAGA movement, with massive audiences, demonstrating that even the most committed partisans are struggling to maintain their previous positions when confronted with the reality of what is happening in Gaza.

The question is not whether conservative support will continue to decline. The trajectory is clear. The question is what narrative will explain that decline. Right now, two competing explanations are vying to fill the space created by collapsing support.

  • One is rooted in humanitarian concern and international law.

  • The other is rooted in antisemitic conspiracy theories and ancient prejudices.

Whichever narrative prevails will determine whether this moment represents genuine progress in universal human rights or something far more dangerous.

This is why it matters that we establish clearly and explicitly what our opposition to this war represents.

But the people whose job it is to make this explicit have abdicated this responsibility, appealing to an out-of-date understanding of their political base.

And thus it falls to the next rung of representation to define for history why exactly we all are, some only lately (but better late than never), opposed to how this war is being executed.

The Trap of Our Perspectives (And the way out)

A trap has torn apart relationships the world over, creating an endless crevasse of subjectivity where one side cannot see the humanity of the other side. Not even a kind word of support can cross the impenetrable perspectival membrane that argues over precisely who is the victim here.

There is no winning conflict like this. But there is, as always, a third way.

The third way this moment demands is not a compromise between competing narratives but a dialectical synthesis that transcends the victim-victimizer binary entirely. Drawing from the Kabbalah-grounded Jewish philosophical tradition of מחלוקת לשם שמים (machloket l’shem shamayim), dispute for the sake of heaven, this approach recognizes that genuine engagement with opposing truths generates חידוש (chiddush), an emergent insight that could not exist without holding both perspectives in tension.

This is not false equivalence.

It is the recognition that moral clarity requires holding irreconcilable facts simultaneously: the October 7th attacks were acts of terror that inflicted unspeakable trauma and triggered intergenerational Holocaust memory throughout Jewish communities, and Israel’s military response has produced systematic violations of international humanitarian law resulting in the death of a child every hour for two years.

The synthesis emerges not by weighing these truths against each other but by recognizing that both constitute violations of the same universal principles established after World War II to prevent such catastrophes. And, far more importantly, by acknowledging the universal truth inside the holiest of holies of all our world’s religions and enlightenment paths — that the way to know God is through worship of your neighbor.

This framework is essential because it prevents legitimate humanitarian opposition from being captured by antisemitic narratives. When national leadership fails to articulate this synthesis and instead retreats into tribal logic, the vacuum invites voices animated not by humanitarian principle but by conspiracy theories and ancient prejudices.

The third way establishes that opposition to Israeli military policy rests on consistent application of international humanitarian law, not on antisemitic tropes about Jewish power. It acknowledges the reality and legitimacy of Jewish trauma while insisting that trauma cannot be weaponized to justify creating new trauma for others. This distinction will determine whether the erosion of conservative support represents genuine moral progress or something far more dangerous.

It lets us hold these two things together:

It acknowledges that the October 7, 2023 attacks by Hamas were horrific acts of terrorism that killed 1,195 people, including 815 civilians, and resulted in over 250 hostages being taken into Gaza.

These attacks represented the deadliest day for Jews since the Holocaust. Full stop.

And (not “but”, the proper term is: “and”) simultaneously, it recognizes that these attacks cannot justify actions that themselves violate international humanitarian law or collectively punish a population that has had no opportunity to vote against Hamas in nearly nineteen years.

As of October 2025, over 67,000 Palestinians have been killed, including at least 19,424 children. That represents one child killed every hour over a 24-month period. Famine has been officially confirmed in Gaza City. Satellite analysis shows 78% of all structures across Gaza have been destroyed. At least 2,340 people have been killed while seeking food assistance since Israel established its militarized food distribution system in May 2025, while 404 Palestinians have died from malnutrition, including 141 children.

These are not matters of opinion or interpretation. These are documented facts that constitute violations of laws specifically designed to protect civilians in wartime. International legal proceedings at the International Court of Justice and International Criminal Court have established frameworks for criticism based on these violations.

The humanitarian case against the War in Gaza rests on universal principles that were established in the aftermath of the Holocaust and the horrors of World War II precisely to prevent such catastrophes from recurring.

The way out of this trap requires us to hold onto two conflicting truth until they merge together and form a third, higher truth.

This ability to hold multiple truths simultaneously represents the moral maturity that conspiracy-minded narratives deliberately reject. If we truly are worried about anti-Semitism, this is the position we must take.

It is precisely what מחלוקת לשם שמים (machloket l’shem shamayim) demands: that we engage with opposing truths not to determine which one wins, but to discover the חידוש (chiddush), the emergent insight that transcends both positions.

This is what liberal democracy requires, and what the third way demands: universal application of principles. We cannot claim to oppose war crimes only when committed by adversaries while excusing them when committed by allies.

We cannot claim that civilian lives have equal value while accepting actions that produce one dead child per hour for two years.

The synthesis that emerges from genuinely engaging with both horrifying truths is not a flattening, an amoral middle ground. It is higher moral ground that can only be achieved by holding both these things true at once.

When national leaders fail at this fundamental task of showing how the suffering of one of us is the suffering of all of us, others will fill the vacuum with their own agendas.

Right now, much of the emerging critique of the Gaza War from the far right appears tied to antisemitic conspiracy theories, including those surrounding the death of Charlie Kirk.

Figures like Candace Owens, who is against the war, has been documented by the ADL as trafficking in antisemitism for years, and she is not advancing humanitarian arguments about Palestinian civilian casualties. Right wing influencers like her are reviving ancient antisemitic tropes that have historically preceded persecution. This contamination of discourse creates a dangerous dynamic where opposition to Israeli military actions becomes associated with antisemitism rather than universal human rights principles.

This is precisely why the third way matters now more than ever, and why Thursday’s Metro Council vote in Nashville is significant. It represents an opportunity to put on record the principled, humanitarian reasons for opposing this war before voices with darker motivations claim to speak for the growing opposition.

The Council can demonstrate what מחלוקת לשם שמים (machloket l’shem shamayim) looks like in practice: acknowledging the full horror of October 7th while refusing to accept that horror as justification for actions that themselves constitute violations of international law. This is not choosing between Jewish trauma and Palestinian suffering. It is insisting that both demand our moral attention and that neither justifies creating more trauma.

The world is changing around us. Being on the wrong side of political winds can happen far faster than people think. But being on the right side of history is never that complicated when you practice the third way: hold multiple truths simultaneously, apply universal principles consistently, and recognize that the path out of cycles of violence requires transformation, not merely choosing which victims to support.

Unbridled wrath has never been on the side of righteousness, and the dialectical synthesis that emerges from genuinely engaging with opposing truths reveals that righteousness requires us to condemn violations of human dignity regardless of who commits them.
