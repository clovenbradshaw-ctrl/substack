Because as of this morning, we are going to have vastly, vastly less of those resources. And what remains will be used in ways that will hurt the most vulnerable among us first — and the rest of us soon after.

What Just Happened

This morning, the federal government released new rules for how cities can spend federal money on homelessness. Think of it like your family budget suddenly getting cut by two-thirds, and someone telling you the remaining third can only be spent on things that haven’t worked before.

Here’s what changed:

The “safe” funding cities could count on dropped from 90% to 30%. Programs that have been helping people for years — even good ones — now have to compete nationally for what’s left. Most will lose. People will be fired. A non-insignificant number of those fired, already working under subsistence wages, will now become clients of the homeless support agencies they once worked for.

Cities that invested in giving people “housing first” (the model we spent $750k in consulting fees to an out-of-state vendor selected by the city’s COO and sponsored by then Council Member O’Connell) are now told they can only spend 30% of their money that way. If we had actually done housing first, we might be in a better place. But we didn’t. But since all of our programming claims its housing first, we’re in quite a pickle.

Our leaders, acting autonomously, aggregating power only for themselves, frankly, they fucked us.

Here is HUD’s new priority: detention. Treatment and work requirements you cannot refuse, in facilities you functionally cannot leave, miles from anywhere, enforced by police who will arrest you for sleeping anywhere else. Utah is spending $75 million on exactly this model, Louisiana is legislating forced labor for camping violations, and HUD says they’re “encouraged.” They’re calling them “transformational campuses.” The rest of us should call them what they are.

Why This Is Personal for Me

I’ve spent months documenting how Nashville wasted $50 million on a “Housing First strategy” that was corrupted from the start — money hoarded by people who faced no consequences when it failed, money that often harmed the very people it was supposed to help.

Housing First, when done right, can work. Can work extraordinarily well.

But when it’s run by people who profit from its failure, when there’s no accountability, when the community being served has no say in how resources are used — it becomes just another way to funnel public money to private interests.

And now? The federal government has made it nearly impossible to do Housing First at all, even when done right. So what are we going to do with what limited funding we have left.

Why on earth would we trust the same people who misspent $50M to manage our vastly diminished funds.

The Canary in the Coal Mine

Here’s what you need to understand: I’m not doing this work because I only care about homelessness policy.

I’m doing it because the unhoused are the canary in the coal mine.

“Godwin’s Law” states that all internet discourse will devolve to always talking about you-know-who. So let’s look at some context, but I’ll redact who I’m talking about and see if any of this clicks for you.

In 1933 in a country in Europe, there was leadership change. The policies of this new administration impacted many demographics, but an under-discussed one is who was immediately targeted.

The term “asocials” was very vague and used to label a wide range of people, for example poor people, homeless, alcoholics or other drug addicts, prostitutes, homosexuals, people not coming to work or coming late, and people living, in the eyes of the [REDACTED], a so-called nonconformist life. The term “career criminal” often didn’t mean people who committed violent felonies. For example, a teenager from a poor family who was caught shoplifting a few times could be labeled as a habitual or career criminal. There are even cases of the [REDACTED] deporting young men with no previous criminal records to concentration camps because local police officers predicted they would have a future criminal career.

But, after 1933, these ideas became the basis of welfare, healthcare and criminology, and the police gained enormous power over the judicial process. Starting in January 1934, the police could take individuals into preventive detention without a court ruling and deport them to concentration camps, labeling them as habitual or career criminals.

Things did not end well for the “asocial” that were placed into camps. At least 1,200 “asocial” men died in concentration camps between January 1938 and August 1939, and “during 1942-43 some 6,000 ‘asocial’ prisoners were exterminated through work programme’s in concentration camps.” (libapp.shadygrove.umd.edu)

The unhoused are the people with the least power in our society today. Along with immigrants. Along with gender non-conforming people. Along with anyone our society has decided doesn’t matter.

They always get it first. The cruelty, the budget cuts, the criminalization, the scapegoating.

When politicians and profiteers want to test how much they can get away with — how much public money they can waste, how many lives they can destroy, how brazenly they can lie about it — they start with the people nobody will defend.

If we let them do it to the unhoused without consequence, they’ll do it to everyone else next. They’ll do it to your schools. Your healthcare. Your roads. Your water. Every public resource that’s supposed to serve us all.

You watched the glee with which the shut down SNAP. And the handwringing with which our “Democrats” couldn’t figure out how to even make the buses temporarily free in response.

The question isn’t whether you personally have been homeless. The question is whether you’re willing to watch them perfect the playbook on the most vulnerable before they use it on you.

This Will Be a Generational Disaster

For the unhoused themselves, obviously, they’ll die. That’s not metaphor. People will die. Far, far more than necessary.

For the business elites and politicians who think clearing encampments makes the problem disappear — it doesn’t. It just moves people around until they run out of places to move.

For people who don’t want to see poverty in public spaces near their homes — you’re about to see a lot more of it, because the programs that actually moved people indoors are being defunded.

For the social workers and case managers doing this work out of genuine care — they’re about to be laid off or forced to become agents of criminalization.

For all of us who depend on public resources working the way they’re supposed to — this is the test case for how bad it can get.

How Decisions Are Currently Made

As a former Mayor’s Office employee and now an investigative spelunker of Metro’s absurd inefficiencies, let me explain as succinctly as possible how decisions currently are made, and why.

Our chief bureaucrats—the Mayor, the Chief of Staff, the County COO—have corporate backgrounds. They’re running Nashville like a corporation.

A corporation is, by definition, a dictatorship. A CEO with hire/fire power over department heads. Ultimate say. Top-down control. No community input that can’t be ignored.

Except the CEO reports to someone: the investors.

In Nashville, the “investors” aren’t you. They’re the Chamber of Commerce. The real estate lobby. Registered lobbyists with deep pockets and shallow interest in solutions that work long-term. We know their names. We see them at council. Their photos and clients are publicly available by TN state law.

That’s who gets listened to. That’s who decisions are made for.

You can’t run a city like a corporation because a city isn’t trying to maximize shareholder value—it’s trying to serve everyone. Those goals aren’t just different, they’re opposed.

The corporate model isn’t failing. It’s working exactly as designed—just not for us.

Read Economics Nobel Prize Winner Elinor Ostrom’s Governing the Commons and come to the table. Today!1 That’s a link to a .pdf of the book, so you have zero excuses.

If you want to be part of the solution, come prepared to create systems where the people most affected by homelessness—the unhoused themselves, the neighbors who live near shelters, the social workers doing the actual work, the small business owners dealing with the daily reality—have real power over how resources are used.

Not token “input.” Not performative “lived experience” panels that get ignored when decisions are made. Real power. Real accountability. Real consequences for waste and failure.

Come prepared to explain why you should continue controlling resources when you’ve failed to use them effectively. Come prepared to show your work. Come prepared to justify every dollar you’ve spent and every outcome you’ve failed to achieve.

Because if you don’t come to the table voluntarily, we will continue in the long line of everyday people who have entered the hallowed grounds you thought were yours alone to control.

And we will flip the tables and expose you for the false leaders you are.

This isn’t a threat. It’s a promise.

The canary is dying. The camps are being built. The money is running out. The federal government is actively steering what’s left toward detention and forced labor.

How Nashville responds—whether we build our own systems of genuine accountability, or whether we sleepwalk into building our own version of concentration camps for the “asocial”—will define us for generations.

The choice is yours. But the clock is running and we’re watching everything you do. I know you think this is your own little company to run how you please. If that’s the only way you can conceive of things, fine. But remember the money you’re using is not yours. It’s ours. We are your board.

And if you can’t get your head around that, that’s actually ok. We’re already looking at new applicants for your position.

1

Ostrom’s core observations that are relevant:

  • Failure of top-down control: External authorities imposing rules without local knowledge and participation consistently fail.

  • Design principles for success include:

    • Those affected by rules must be able to participate in modifying them

    • Monitoring must be done by community members or by someone accountable to them

    • Graduated sanctions for rule violations (consequences for waste/failure)

    • Recognition that those with the most at stake should have decision-making power

  • Neither pure privatization nor pure government bureaucracy works: She won the Nobel for proving that community-based governance outperforms both market solutions and centralized state control.

  • Polycentric governance: Multiple centers of decision-making, with real power distributed to those closest to the problem, works better than concentrated authority.
