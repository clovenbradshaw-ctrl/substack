The Why //

My ambition with posting the transaction history of the city is not to be the conduit for transparency in Nashville, but to push the city to publish its transaction history itself ‚Äî as many other cities do on platforms like Atlanta and NYC.

Knowing how a city actually uses its taxpayer dollars is the ground floor of accountability. Having a clear understanding of what money was used where, when, and by whom is a requirement for legislators, (award winning üèÜüèôÔ∏è‚åöüê∂) watch dogs like me, the media, and the general public to know what is actually happening in our government.

And for the bureaucrats who are tasked with stewarding these funds, it is not reasonable, or in their own best interest, to take them at their word that they are doing their best with the resources they have. It tends to help everyone when they know there are multiple eyes on the ledger book. It makes sure honest people are rewarded for doing the right thing, and dishonest people go get another job with less scrutiny.

Transparency is a virtuous cycle that rewards the effective use of public funds. But at the same time, opaqueness is a vicious cycle that allows inefficiency to thrive. Opaqueness pushes back against accountability, always claiming that those asking for transparency are acting in bad faith. That‚Äôs not the vibe.

The What //

When I first advised Metro that I wanted to publish all of their transaction history starting in FY26, I was told that they would not be able to provide me this information. I was given various reasons. The reasons did not make sense to me.

The records I got did include the vendor names. They did not include the names of the departments that were performing the listed transactions.

The How //

But back to these records.

So I had 58,000+ transactions in a CSV (spreadsheet) file, with none of the rows identifying what department was performing each transaction -- at least not in a way a human can read. But what I did have was two pieces of information that let me reconstruct this.

In the long accounting string on most of the records is a 2 digit code which identifies the department. I was able to extract the current 2 digit account code for each department from the ~600 page FY26 Metro Budget. Using a JavaScript code written by ClaudeAI I was able to process each record‚Äôs string against this and match a significant portion, but for some reason many of the records did not have this element.

Next I created a pivot table of all of the ‚Äúbusiness units‚Äù listed in the data. A ‚Äúbusiness unit‚Äù is, in essence, a bank account set aside for particular purposes for a particular department. There are about ~500 unique business units listed in the data I got for July and August, and no publicly organized list that identifies these to departments. However the Metro budget does list these all under the section for each department, spaced throughout the very large budget document. It would take a person several days to extract all of these.

Using ChatGPT I was able to feed it chunks of these business units, about 20 at a time, and it looked up these numbers in the budget and linked them to the department and provided the specific use case of that business unit.

This allowed me to create a ‚ÄúJSON dictionary‚Äù that defined each department by its two digit identifier and as a fallback, match using its business unit. This type of ‚Äúdictionary‚Äù file is formatted in a way that is very easy for modern computer scripts to understand, and is fairly legible for humans. Now that I had defined most of the business units that would be used for general fund uses, it will be useful for future exports I receive and for other people or organizations who might need to perform the same data reconstruction as me with Metro‚Äôs Finances.

Using my ‚Äúdictionary‚Äù to decode the accounting strings on each line, I constructed a data table that said which department used what money and how. That is, believe it or not, the short version of what needed to happen for you to see these records today.

Future requests for transaction exports will have the benefit of me doing this work already. Even records requested by others can piggyback off the work I detailed. But all of this is, I think you‚Äôd agree, is quite an unreasonable amount of hurdles to put in front of someone asking a simple question: how is public money being spent?
