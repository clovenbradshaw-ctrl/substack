2. Tennessee law gives Oracle unique power to share your data without warrants, of their own volition.

Tennessee Information Protection Act, Section 47-18-3205(d)(1)(A) states processors can:

“cooperate with law enforcement agencies concerning conduct or activity that the controller or processor reasonably and in good faith believes may violate federal, state, or local laws, rules, or regulations”

What this actually means:

Oracle (the “processor”) can look at your data and decide on their own if they think you might be breaking any law, rule, or regulation - then share your data with police without needing a warrant.

The problem:

  • “Rules or regulations” is undefined - it could mean anything from city ordinances to HOA rules to terms of service

  • Oracle decides what looks suspicious based on their “good faith belief”

  • No judge or court reviews this decision

  • You won’t know your data was shared

  • You can’t challenge it

Normal privacy laws require:

  • A warrant signed by a judge

  • A subpoena with legal justification

  • A court order based on probable cause

  • OR your explicit consent

Tennessee’s law lets Oracle skip all of that. They become judge and jury about what’s suspicious enough to share with law enforcement.

Oracle’s involvement: The Business Software Alliance, where Oracle holds a board seat, actively lobbied for Tennessee’s law and celebrated its passage.
