With all things going on in the world, the “wow, they really are trying to squeeze every drop of surplus labor value out of late stage capitalism via AI” feels like a back burner issue these days.

The analysis of late is bearish on the robits’1 ROI, a minor win for us real human beings still in the labor force.

The findings revealed a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading. Younger participants exhibited higher dependence on AI tools and lower critical thinking scores compared to older participants.

But, regardless of the empirical ROI, many (most?) of us are using AI.

And I find it surprisingly odd that there is no real attempt to make an ethics or etiquette about disclosing this — especially in writing like here on Substack. For a while now, virtually nothing I’ve worked on did not touch, in some way or another, a large language model.

Of late, I’ve used tools from Anthropic and OpenAI to do deep research for me for, providing analysis and citations I can look into on topics that are quite difficult or even impossible to “just Google about.” I’ve used proofreader models in ChatGPT to find my innumerable typos; I’m a verbal learner, I’ve learned, so I often make erroneous homophone replacements (they’re / their; it’s / its). It’s not because I don’t know the difference but because as I’m proofreading I’m listening more than looking. AI is very helpful there.

As the articles explore, this could very well have concerning consequence on my critical thinking skills. But it also seems deeply relevant to my other more general calls for transparency and accountability. Don’t we, you know, have a duty to disclose AI use, in some digestible and meaningful way? But literally, how do you do that?

The newer coding tools have been invaluable to spin up and iterate on interactive charts and full mini-websites that I never would have been able to produce.

But it feels weird to say things definitively like “yes, I made this.” It also feels clunky and pat to say “yeah, I used some AI,” and frankly I’m sick of saying “A-I” all the time. And “ChatGPT” remains the worst named world changing invention in modern history.

So, using AI heavily, I built a thing (or me and the AI did) to help disclose precisely how I’ve used AI tools for a given project.

I’m calling it TRACE.

Transparent Reporting of AI Contributions & Edits

Every AI-assisted piece of work gets a TRACE badge—a visual disclosure that shows at a glance how AI was used. Generate yours with the widget here or check the code on GitHub.

The badge creates both a visual four-quadrant display and a text citation you can insert into any document.

The Four Quadrants

T - TASK: What job did the AI do?

The primary function the AI performed. Reveals the degree of AI involvement, from light touch-ups to full generation.

Categories:

  • Assist: Cleaned up grammar, spelling, style, or formatting

  • Draft: Generated the main body of content

  • Synthesis: Combined or merged multiple provided sources

  • Analysis: Explained patterns, performed calculations, or reasoned through problems

  • Exploratory: Used experimentally without fixed goals

  • Creative: Invented original content, narratives, or artistic elements

R - RESOURCES: What sources were given?

The information foundation that shaped the AI's response. Shows whether the AI used public information, private materials, or only its training data.

Categories:

  • Public: Only openly available information, URLs, or user-created prompts

  • Internal: Private, proprietary, or paywalled materials

  • Model-only: No external inputs provided; relied on pre-trained knowledge

  • Unclear: Source attribution is uncertain or unknowable

A&C - APPROACH & CRAFT: How was the job carried out?

The workflow and techniques used. Describes both the approach (strategy) and craft (skillful execution) of the AI interaction.

Categories:

  • Raw: Published the first output with minimal editing

  • Guided: Structured the task with steps, scaffolding, or context

  • Rewriter: Transformed existing text into a new format or style

  • Augmented: Used external tools, plugins, or environments beyond the base model

E - EVALUATION: How was the output checked?

The level and type of human oversight applied after generation. Indicates verification and quality control measures.

Categories:

  • Unreviewed: No human checked the output before use

  • Skimmed: Quick review for tone and obvious issues only

  • General: Non-expert but knowledgeable person reviewed carefully

  • Expert: Subject-matter expert verified accuracy and quality

  • AI-Check: The model was prompted to critique or improve its own output

Each quadrant works together to create a complete picture of how AI was integrated into the content creation process, promoting transparency and helping both creators and audiences understand the human-AI collaboration involved.

Here is the badge, for example, for this for this project — TRACE itself, and this post.

AI Disclosure (2025-08-28) TRACE: ADSE-PI-GWA-G via Anthropic: Claude Opus 4.1 + OpenAI: ChatGPT-4o + OpenAI: GPT-5 Chat Role: Assist, Draft, Synthesis, Exploratory Data: Public, Internal Method: Guided, Rewriter, Augmented Review: General

I used AI (Anthropic: Claude Opus 4.1 + OpenAI: ChatGPT-4o + OpenAI: GPT-5 Chat) to clean up grammar and style in the text, draft initial content, synthesize information from multiple sources, and explore possibilities through experimental testing using public sources and internal/private data, through guided prompting, by rewriting and transforming text, with augmented tools and capabilities. The output underwent general review.

Here is a conclusion to this article, written mostly by AI that I guided.

Socrates worried that writing would ruin us. And in a way, it did. Memory collapsed. Oral traditions that once carried epics across generations shrank to quaint performances for tourists. People stopped remembering, because remembering was no longer required. Something at the core of human cognition—our ability to hold vast songs in our heads—was lost.

But that ruin rewired us. Once thoughts could be written, they could be compared, cross-referenced, argued over. Laws could outlast kings. Sciences could accumulate across centuries. Bureaucracy could extend across empires. Writing gave us permanence at the cost of presence. We lost memory, but we gained history. We lost the living performance, but we gained the archive.

The same pattern is here again. Large language models will ruin something, and they will rewire something. They will corrode habits of critical thinking in the same way books corroded memory. They will make offloading reasoning easy, and eventually habitual. I see it in classrooms, in colleagues, in myself. As a proud liberal artist trained on the Western canon, I refuse to call the abandonment of critical thinking acceptable. But I also know this: refusing to accept something does not prevent it from happening.

That loss may open new terrain. We will gain architectures of knowledge and prosthetics for thought. We will change what counts as “knowing,” just as writing once did. But the trade will not be clean, and it will not be free. Evolution never is.

That’s why experiments like TRACE matter. Not because they prevent the ruin, but because they mark it. They admit the handoffs: here the ghost drafted, here the hive mind synthesized, here I proofed and steered. Imperfect, bureaucratic, even clumsy—but at least an honest record of what happened.

Because the danger isn’t just in the ruin, or even in the rewiring. It’s in pretending one comes without the other. In mistaking sand for stone. In waking up amid the ruins, unable to remember how we got there.
