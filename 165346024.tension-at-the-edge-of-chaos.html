It’s the kind of line that crystalizes things for me. It’s a true meme in its proper definition, the smallest unit of culture, from which behavior patterns tesselate out from, recreating its essential structure at higher levels of complexity.

In this I hear the essence of the conservative consciousness, distilled elegantly down into 12 short words.

The leader as sin eater.

The leader as Atlas holding the world up.

The leader as cross bearer.

The leader as an island away from common humans, absorbing all the world’s energies (both good and evil), and refracting only glory unto the earth — a lighthouse across the sea of human misery and confusion.

In this headspace, the tensions at the heart of the world are unresolvable.

Efforts to explain the origins of conflict, to uncover nuance and complexity, that is the work of the nefarious other who is only there to usurp the throne. No one who challenges the right order of things is doing so in good faith, because good faith is believing in the right order of things.

It’s a romantic, messianic vision of leadership.

And a simple one. All too simple.

“Vibes” have names, and a logic all their own

There’s a framework of individual/collective development called Spiral Dynamics, developed by Don Beck and Christopher Cowan during the 1990s, building on the work of thinkers like Hegel, Gebser, Piaget, Kohlberg, Maslow and Wilber.

It would label this model of leadership we’ve been discussing, which views the world in terms of Us vs Them, and the leader as having a sacred mission from god, as belonging to the “Blue Stage” of development in Spiral Dynamics terms.

Blue gets conflated with modern Christianity in its us vs them, purity vs defilement fixation. But Blue is historically very pre-Christian, and it resonates much more with the Old Testament than the Gospels. The teachings of Christ, in examples like the Beatitudes, resonate much more with later stages like Green, and the higher level “integral stages” which recombine earlier levels of consciousness by allowing their tensions to surface and release, allowing for emergent complexity and new forms of governance, leadership, and society.

On a much more quantifiable level, The Model of Hierarchical Complexity (MHC), developed by Michael Commons, measure multi-variable task completion. It looks at how complex of a task people can solve in terms of how many variables they hold in their mind at a given time. As we mature, so does our ability to hold conflicts in our minds and see nuanced perspectives. The MHC systematizes this, and asserts that complex problems require complex thinking to resolve them.

This aligns with what’s called Ashby’s Law, which TheStoa.com explains here.

The messianic view of the leader as “holding all tensions” is one where, crucially, the tensions are not actually resolved — they’re suspended in midair, kept out of reach. There’s no catharsis, no digestion, just a kind of psychic constipation. In these systems, alternative facts — meaning information that threatens the dominant frame — don’t get processed. They get buried. The logic goes: if you let them surface, they might unravel the entire sacred order. So the system stays neat, linear, and tidy by sheer repression.

It’s not just censorship — it’s almost developmental. There’s a kind of object permanence issue here, like if we can’t see the problem, it must not exist. This shows up as a “shoot the messenger” reflex when someone points out cracks in the façade. It’s not the rot that’s intolerable — it’s acknowledging it.

That’s why Trump’s line about COVID — “If we stop testing right now, we’d have very few cases, actually” — hits so precisely. On his best days, Trump operates in a Blue frame. The problem isn’t the virus. The problem is talking about the virus. The test result is not data — it’s threat. That’s the move: the crisis isn’t what’s happening, it’s your refusal to pretend it’s not.

Blue doesn’t do well with “facts” in the modernist sense — data points, falsifiable claims, things that can disrupt a system by being true. Blue has a different relationship with truth altogether. It doesn’t discover it, it enforces it. Facts don’t exist independently; they’re subordinate to the moral order. In this worldview, a fact is not a neutral observation — it’s a reaffirmation. A fact is only “real” if it confirms what must be real. Everything else is heresy, sedition, or noise. Blue doesn’t test reality, it maintains it. It keeps the story straight by making it the only story you’re allowed to tell.

So when a Blue system talks about “truth,” it’s not looking for new inputs. It’s stabilizing the frame. It’s re-citing the sacred. It’s making sure the commandments haven’t drifted.

Some systems still operate fairly well this way.

Most militaries operate with a form of this value set, maintaining a command and control hierarchy out of duty to god and country.

This is the value system of the type of “religion” people mean when they say they “spiritual but not religious.” It is very good at organizing the world when there are simple, never resolved dichotomies of Good vs Bad, Us vs Them, Victim vs Predator.

But homelessness, and the system that perpetuates it, and the interconnected networks of harm reduction around it, is very quite complex.

Too complex for this type of leadership.

The Liberal Paralysis

Liberals endlessly annoy me. They annoy me because they’re so close to getting the dynamics at play in society, but like the final boss in a philosophy-centric video game, stand guard at the exit of Plato’s cave of illusions, looking backwards towards the illusionary shadows on the wall, and say things like “we should really do a study before we do anything rash like becoming enlightened.”

The liberal mind operates in what Spiral Dynamics would call the "Orange-Green" transition zone — sophisticated enough to see through Blue's rigid hierarchies, but not quite developed enough to navigate the genuine complexity that comes after deconstructing them. They've learned to critique power, but not how to wield it responsibly. They've discovered systemic thinking, but mistake process for progress.

This is why liberals are perpetually stuck in the procedural purgatory of "stakeholder engagement" and "community input sessions" — endlessly convening focus groups to study problems that require immediate action. They've confused democracy with therapy, governance with group processing.

The liberal consciousness has a pathological need to be liked by everyone, which means they can never actually choose a side when it matters. They've weaponized empathy into a kind of moral paralysis. "We need to understand all perspectives," they say, as if the perspective of the slumlord and the homeless family carries equal moral weight in housing policy discussions.

This manifests as what I call "Both-Sides-ism as Spiritual Practice" — the mystical belief that truth always lives in the exact center between any two competing claims. Tell a liberal that someone says the sky is blue and someone else says it's red, and they'll spend six months forming a committee to explore "the purple possibilities."

The most insidious part is how this masquerades as wisdom. The liberal positions themselves as the enlightened moderate, the reasonable adult in the room, the one person mature enough to "hold complexity." But what they're actually doing is avoiding the discomfort of moral clarity. They've turned intellectual sophistication into an elaborate defense mechanism against having to take definitive action.

This is why liberal institutions are so good at producing beautiful reports that change nothing, hosting meaningful conversations that lead nowhere, and building consensus around the lowest common denominator. They mistake the process of thinking for the courage of deciding.

When confronted with genuine evil — not the cartoonish kind, but the banal bureaucratic evil of systems that slowly grind people into dust — the liberal response is to form a task force. They'll spend two years studying how to respond to a crisis that needed a response two years ago.

They are Hamlet in a world that needs more Fortinbras — forever thinking, rarely acting, convinced that their hesitation is actually profundity.

The Liberal Illusion of the “Golden Mean”

Aristotle spoke of the “Golden Mean”, that the average between two extremes, like Cowardice and Rashness, is where you find Courage.

It’s one of Aristotle’s more intuitive lessons, and one of his most reductive and pernicious. The liberal mindset latches onto any tension it sees and assumes that by just engaging with both sides, they’ll balance each other out, and both sides will feel satisfied. The liberal has no official opinion on the validity or truth value of each perspective, nor their respective complexity or ability to see each other from the other side.

They actually reenforce division by framing society as one large, flat surface on which all sides can fight each other out — with civility, please, please! Never mind that one side might say things like “those people deserve to die!” and the other side says “I’d like healthcare, please.” This is a market economy of ideas, people, and we have to let the invisible hand do all the hard work.

The simple, 2D image of the political spectrum is in itself a product of liberal thinking, with the radical fringes of society be the noisy bunches that keep the peace from flowing that comes from moderation.

We have gone far beyond Aristotle in our comprehension of most things, but we still cling to this illusory binary, trying to squeeze progress out of the nullifcation of opposites.

But if the political spectrum were a number line, and the far left was “-1”, and the far right was “1”, the center point is, the moderation between the two, is “0.” It is nothing.

No growth, no progress towards a better future, no retention of the essential past. It is nothing.

And that is precisely why Aristotle was completely ill equipped to described the fundamental element that makes like possible and makes a better future worth fighting for. The emergence of something new that comes from the synthesis of opposites.

It’s not the nullification of opposites, its the synthesis. It is both/and, not either/or, or as the moderate gives you, neither.

Here’s what Aristotle didn’t want you to know (maybe one of esoteric lessons he told his jealous student Alexander the Great he was sure to keep from every being published) — we do not find transcendence from tension by subtracting one extreme from the other.

Rather, we do what Hegel described, we fuse them both together and sublimate them as an act of multiplication. We take the true essence of both extremes, and slingshot our way into another dimension.

This is “dialectics”, the synthesizing of something new from opposites. We are not interested in the middle ground, between, say, fear and confidence. That would put us at the “zero” point if we were to look at this triangle from its side, and view it as a line.

The mean between fear and confidence is caution, a state a paralysis where we do nothing.

But the synthesis is clarity. And this is important because this is one of the tensions that underlies what it means to be Brave in a real, complex world.

We are beings in placetimes that are complex.

We must live in the four dimensions we actually exist in.

Bravery is what happens when you transcend the tensions between fear and confidence, action and non-action, and your interior self and the exterior world.

Bravery is sum of clarity, grace and integrity.

Bravery = √[(Fear × Confidence)² + (Action × Non-Action)² + (Interior × Exterior)²]

Where:

  • Fear × Confidence = Clarity

  • Action × Non-Action = Grace

  • Interior × Exterior = Integrity

Therefore: Bravery = √(Clarity² + Grace² + Integrity²)

As humans, we are far beyond using two dimensional approaches to solving our problems.

Not because its more efficient to solve our problems using more than two dimensions, not because we have excess critical thinking and we just want bureaucrats to feel bad. It’s because our problems are more complex than those solutions — and I know we all know that.

But it means things about power.

It means that Liberals are going to have to cede power to those with actual solutions. Or they’re going to have to transcend being mere liberals.

We haven’t been saying it because, despite what you may think, we’re actually holding our tongues. But let’s just say, we noticed.

You’re not hiding it from anyone. So let’s just be honest with the fact that we all have self work to do.

It means you may have to pick up a philosophy book, or better yet, listen to a podcast, or do mushrooms, or study how ants find the most efficient routes through their collective intelligence.

It means that hardest work of your life will have nothing to do with the battles you fight out here, but the dragons you slay in there.
