We can know what happens, to some degree, to our security footage when it’s transferred to the government. If correctly enforced, the wheels of bureaucracy grind out transparency and tell us that so and so watched our video feed at X time for Y reason. Reasonable enough, in theory.

But what happened to our camera footage on the way there?

What happens to the data generated by our content when it is uploaded to a for-profit corporation that has contracts across every level of government and surveillance operation across the globe?

Is it possible they might want to, in some way, profit off of it? In the same way that these other surveillance companies translate raw security data from government and private contracts into bulk data to sell?

Cities like Nashville can set rules for how their own law enforcement can access and use data. But what about the company they are recommending you upload it to so they can access it? What are the restrictions and regulations there?

I wanted to find out. So I went looking for the terms and conditions that Nashville businesses would be asked to sign in order to be a part of MNPD's Community Safety Camera Network as a provider of streaming video.

They’re not available.

Axon / Axoff

Metro Nashville’s Council will be discussing once again on Tuesday whether the city should invest $750k into a surveillance integration technology that will allow private business owners to directly feed their security camera footage to MNPD, via a third party called hardware and software called Fusus, owned by the highly controversial weapons and surveillance company Axon.

In a rather usual way, Metro is carrying a lot of water for a specific corporation’s product, encouraging the public to purchase their proprietary hardware and software — not something a government is typically in this business of doing.

This official Nashville.gov website, ostensively an explainer for the Community Camera Safety Network in general, specifically endorses the Fusus product of the Axon Corporation, answering questions as to its safety protocols and policies. Metro can speak to its own proposed contractual relationship with Axon, but it seems highly inappropriate to make statements about what a business’ contractual relationship with Axon would be — something which is Metro has no control over or insight into.

One has to wonder if it was co-written by some of the four registered lobbyists hired by Axon to sell Metro on its products, or whatever Axon product marketer set up this website: connectmetronashville.org.

Created by Axon corporate, the Connect Metro Nashville site features an MNPD logo as the only brand identification at the top of the page — and the MNPD HQ address appears in the footer next to the Axon logo. It’s a strange level of public/private product integration for project that has not actually been approved by Metro Nashville.

Everybody Wants Some Safety

Business owners, on Metro recommending, are allegedly queuing up to by a Fusus box from Axon.

It may not be such a clean boon for them, however.

Business owners in Nashville might not worry about someone tapping into their cameras and calling in an ICE raid or arresting someone in “non gender conforming clothes.” Or they may have had their concerns assuaged that MNPD has enough policies in place to prevent these things from happening.

But what MNPD, and Metro in general, has no control over is what happens to your footage before it is piped over to their system. What happens to the images and sounds from inside your store, your restaurant, your marketing firm or your honkey tonk before it ends up with MNPD?

When there are no calls for service in your area, is Axon watching and recording? If not the literal video, the metadata and AI object identification analysis? If so, what can they do with that footage or metadata? I haven’t gotten clear answers — but plenty of evidence that we won’t like what they could do.

Terms and Conditions Apply

It you look closely at all the official documents about the Community Safety Network (CSN) which is the body that is responsible for setting up and overseeing the integration of business security cameras, Axon and MNPD, they’re not telling you have to work with Fusus. But you don’t have a choice if you want to be in the integrated CSN.

The nature of government partnership and procurement is such that it’s improper to tell private citizens that they should buy equipment and software from a given company. But when it comes to integrating your camera with the CSN, there is not, to my knowledge, another game in town besides Axon’s Fusus.

The CSN policies document has several pages of policies about data sharing with law encforcement, and the bulk of the amendments that have been added since the last time the Council defeated this bill are essentially changes to this document. They provide “guardrails” (a buzzword of this particular legislation’s discussion) for how MNPD may access cameras, must audit access to its tools, and must publish ledgers of its usage of this system.

All well and good. But there is nothing regarding what Axon may do with this footage or metadata generated from it.

Metro’s opinion on this appears to be, in formal legal parlance, “that’s a you problem.”

Metro, likely for liability and logistical reasons, is not getting into the fine details of what your relationship is with Axon. But this is where idioms like “having your cake and eating it too” and “leading lambs to the slaughter” come to mind.

Metro and MNPD can claim their hands are free from excessive intrusion into our data, and perhaps we have reason to believe them. Mayor O’Connell posits this as a local trust issue. Do we trust local law enforcement? Do we trust our local government?

But O’Connell, the decades-long data privacy wonk, knows full well that there’s nothing local about encouraging your public to do business with a $50B global corporation with surveillance and weapons contracts the world over.

The fact is, O’Connell, Metro and no business owners in Nashville can say with certainty what the terms of service will be when they sign up with Fusus.

Axon’s website lists Fusus as having “third party terms”, indicating that the general terms of service for Axon may not apply for Fusus and its online cloud platform fususCORE. But the link to these 3rd party terms only leads to the generic product marketing page of Fusus on their own website, with no terms of service listed.

It’s notable that there are two websites that have an FAQ about how Fusus would work if Nashville went in on it. One is on Nashville.gov, and one is on a site owned and maintained by the Axon corporation at connectmetronashville.org.

These both contain nearly identical information about how it would work. Notably, the non-government site features an MNPD logo and has the MNPD headquarters in the footer, something which this non-lawyer/former government employee finds misleading at best, highly ethically dubious or potentially fraudulent at worst.

And why is this nashville.gov website vouching for the product of a $50B corporation in language that, as a former manager of a demand generation marketing company, was 1000% written by a product marketer for Axon and not an MNPD employee?

It’s one thing to encourage folks to join the Community Safety Camera network. It’s another to put Metro’s name and liability on the line endorsing a specific product by a particular corporation.

Both the Nashville.gov site and Axon controlled (MNPD endorsed) product marketing site make claims that the business owners are in control of their cameras. The Metro site is a bit more explicit about the video content. A promise which is not Metro’s to make.

And here’s something telling. The Metro Council amendments addressing the safety concerns over Fusus make explicit use that Metro shall not make “use of artificial intelligence or machine learning based solutions, excluding the use of such technologies to make an image more interpretable.” This carve-out for AI enhancing makes this a bit moot since inventing pixels that were not already there does not make them more accurate — but indeed, there is consistent language banning the use of AI in these amendments.

But it’s a different story entirelyon the MNPD endorsed, Axon managed site connectmetronashville.org.

Fusus leverages AI to do object recognition to “automatically recognize weapons, vehicles of interest,” and that very ominous “etc.” Metro has no ability to prevent this, to control this — it call only tell MNPD “you can’t access that.” But as per Fusus providing this to other forms of law enforcement or bulk data brokers who want to observe customer data, or corporations that may want oppositional research on their competitors, or a thousand other dangerous uses of surveillance footage — Metro has no control over that.

And that’s not Metro’s fault. Metro can only control what metro does. We live in a data surveillance state of our own making every day. But the least we can do, as the government, is not encourage people to give away to highly worrisome corporations out of a vague promise of “safety”.

I leave you with a parable.

The Constables and the Ledger Keepers

In a quiet little town where most folks couldn’t read or write, the constables did more than just keep the peace—they kept the ledgers. If a person needed to remember a debt, a deal, or a promise, they could go to the constables, who would faithfully write it down. They recorded recipes for beloved family meals, methods for crafting fine leather boots, formulas for dyes that made the town’s cloth richer than any other. It was a service, freely given, just as much a part of town life as guarding the gates or settling disputes.

But then came rumors from distant villages—dark tales of constables who kept more than just public ledgers. Some had begun quietly recording the movements of townsfolk, noting who met with whom, who whispered about change, who spoke too boldly. And in those places, people had disappeared.

Fear crept into the town. The people didn’t want their constables keeping secret records. So they took their concerns to the town council, demanding a solution. The council listened and proposed a simple fix: the constables would no longer record personal affairs. Their ledgers would be reserved for public safety—things like supplies, laws, and town defenses. They could only ask for and receive information directly related to keeping the town secure.

If people still needed to have their personal dealings written down—recipes, craft secrets, trade agreements, personal notes—they would have to go to independent ledger keepers instead.

Naturally, folks were worried. Who could they trust with such important information? But the constables had a plan. They directed townsfolk to particular private ledger keepers, ones they assured were reliable. “These are the best,” they said, leading people down narrow alleyways to dimly lit shops. “These keepers will take care of you.”

The townsfolk, relieved, believed they had solved the problem. But they had been led like lambs to the slaughter.

At first, things seemed normal. People went to the new ledger keepers, recorded their dealings, and went about their business. But then, strange things started happening.

Merchants noticed their competitors from other towns suddenly had an advantage—buyers who used to purchase local goods were going elsewhere, seemingly knowing just what to undercut. Farmers had their supply deals mysteriously underbid before ink had even dried on their agreements. A baker found another town selling her family’s secret spice blend. A weaver discovered her special dye formula, once unique to her trade, being sold in the next village over.

Worst of all, word came from the prince’s court—his enforcers were rounding up those who had spoken against him, calling them traitors. Somehow, the prince’s men knew exactly who had met in secret, what had been discussed, and who had pledged loyalty to improving things for the people.

Fear turned to outrage. People rushed to the town council, their faces red with fury.

“You sent us to them!” they shouted. “This is your doing! The constables may not have written the ledgers, but the regional prince has them now! Merchants are losing their trade, rivals know our business before we do, and our friends are being dragged away! You promised us safety!”

The council only sighed and shrugged.

“Sorry,” they said. “We only control the constables. You willingly gave up that information. And if a tradesperson or the prince was smart enough to buy it, well—that’s just capitalism, baby.”

And so, while the constables’ ledgers remained open and honest, the real ledgers—the ones that truly mattered—had fallen into the hands of those who answered to no one at all.
