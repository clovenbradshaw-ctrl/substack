Nashville, like most cities, has a commercial property false alarm problem.

When I worked in the Mayor’s Office, I had a habit of checking in on the “active dispatch” dashboard, which tells you where MNPD was currently sending its officers and why. I was continually surprised that every time I looked at it, the most common type alert was “non residence burglary alarm.”

Are Nashville businesses under constant attack by roving bands of burglars?

No. They just have really bad alarm systems. So bad, that 96% of these alarms that go off, triggering an officer to have roll up and check on things.

In 2024, there were 28,435 calls for service triggered by commercial burglary alarms.

Only 1,090 formal Incident Reports were filed, however, meaning:

27,345 officer dispatches in 2024 were security system manfuntion created false alarms.

That costs Nashville officer salary pay, car maintenance, and gasoline and most likely contributes to MNPD’s documented response time issues.

The cost of these tens of thousands of wasted calls by MNPD officers is a direct burden put onto taxpayers. But the solution SHOULD NOT be Metro Nashville pay for, and encourage business owners to collaborate in a systemic invasion of privacy every time a commercial burglary alarm goes off (something that happened 77 times per day in Nashville in 2024).

False alarms are a huge problem in Nashville. They’re a huge waste on public resources, and the availability of cheap, poorly installed equipment makes their wasteful calls to the police happen far too often — averaging 3.1 false alarms per hour in 2024.

But other cities have ways to solve this, through a combination of public awareness campaigns and fines of frequent false alarm owners.

Metro Codes allows the city to charge $25 per false alarm (have the rate the state of TN requires) though I have not been able to find any documentation of how often, if ever, this is enforced in Nashville.

A study by the National Fire Academy documented that these fees on owners of systems with frequent false alarms were effective ways to reduce unnecessary calls for service.

False Alarm Response Fees: A Feasibility Analysis
Ronald G. Hassan
Prince George’s County Fire/EMS Department
Largo, Maryland

Summarized findings:

  • Prince George’s County Police Department: Implemented a false alarm ordinance in 1997, leading to a 14% reduction in false alarms and estimated savings of nearly $1 million.

  • Boston Fire Department: Implemented a sliding-scale fee system that reduced false alarms by 44% in three years (from 9,000 to 5,000 annually).

  • Melbourne, Australia: Saw drastic improvements once fees were introduced, forcing property owners to fix faulty alarm systems.

The City of Boulder, CO developed a robust program to reduce false alarms, developed through the 2013 Police Department’s Master Plan,

The City of Boulder has seen a dramatic reduction in false alarm calls since implementing the program, helping to change the status quo where “taxpayers lost an estimated $300,000 in wasted police time.”

Boulder and other research has found the human error is the primary cause of these issues, and that proper installation, training, and consequences for not correctly implementing those first two lead to the best improvements in false alarm rates.

Additionally, a huge reduction in false alarm rates is when business owners purchase a subscription to a “verified alarm system.” Rather than just requesting cop cars directly to a scene when perhaps a baseball rolled off a cabinet or an employee put the alarm code in wrong, alarm companies can can business owners to confirm the legitimacy of the call. Increasingly, companies can monitor your camera feed, and then alert the police.

These are solutions that solve the enormous problem of false alarms in our city, without the government having a direct line into footage of non-public spaces, nor does it incentivize businesses to use particular vendors over another — vendors that, as I’ll later show, have question intellectual property policies in their past and possible current terms of service.

When you compare the actual incident reports of commercial burglaries in 2024 to the number of active dispatches from 2024 for “71As” (the formal police “ten code” for commercial burglaries), the result is shocking.

According to a report I requested from MNPD, there were 28,435 71As in 2024. But there were only 1,090 actual incident reports created for commercial burglaries (stats you can confirm via data.nashville.gov.

That means 2024 had a 96.2% false alarm rate for commercial burglaries. That’s 27,354 false alarms in one year. That’s 27,354 times MNPD paid an offer to go check out a bar, a gas station, a bakery, a honkey tonk, for no reason. 27,354 times gasoline was expended, cars deteriorated, and response time was extended for other, non false alarm calls.
