The Hockey Stick and the Straight Line

Over the last five years, Nashville's spending on homelessness has increased in a textbook "hockey stick" pattern. A slow climb, followed by a sharp spike. Budgets ballooned. The Office of Homeless Services was invented and dramatically scaled up. Developers lined up and MDHA signed contracts for “permanent supportive housing”

You’d think with that level of input, we’d get some increase in output.

Think again.

Either this system is much more complicated than that, and we’re thinking about it too simply. Or it’s quite simple, and for some reason, we’re vastly over complicating it. I think the reality is that it’s both.

The cure for homelessness is homes. Its sickness is the business we’ve built around them.

In Nashville over the last five years, Housing placements, the number of people who go from homeless to housed are flat. That’s according to the figures Metro’s Office of Homeless Services publishes. Once we factor in a bump that came COVID relief funds in 2021, they’re practically identical year over year.

Look at the chart. The green bars shoot skyward. The housing outcomes? They flatline.

Here’s what the data shows us when we zoom in on the first jump, 2020 to 2021:

  • In FY2021, Nashville’s homelessness budget jumped 131%, from $8.8 million to $20.4 million.

  • Housing placements almost doubled. A 92% increase, from 975 people in FY2020 to 1,875 in FY2021.

That year, for once, the system did what the public imagines it’s supposed to do: put more people in housing. Not because Metro suddenly found religion, but because federal COVID relief money came with actual requirements attached. Dollars had to be tied to putting unhoused people inside. So, for one brief season, the numbers moved in relation to the funding put into the system.

It was proof of concept. Money can translate into housing. You can, believe it or not, just pay people’s rent. 400 people were rapidly housed, and it did not cause the renter economy to collapse.

You can also, if you have the will, build social housing. You can do Master Lease Agreements. These are models, in fact really the only models, that work at scale. But when $50M flowed into Nashville’s system, this isn’t what we did.

When ROI means “Relocation of Individuals”

This is how money poured into our homelessness system.

  • Mayor Cooper pledged a $50M investment of COVID relief funds to serve the unhoused, with $25M going to create more permanent supportive housing and $25M going to support services for that population.

  • These funds were not as stringent about how they were to be used, and the two buckets had little oversight about how efficiently they were to be used.

  • In the midst of this, Metro had a huge shakeup in its homelessness leadership, and down-the-line staff resigned in protest as energy went towards closing encampments over housing people sustainably.

  • From FY2020 to FY2024, funding for homelessness related construction and programming more than quadrupled, up $45+ million in 2024.

  • Placements of unhoused people into permanent housing? Basically flat.

  • After the 2020 to 2021 spike, Metro went from 1,875 in FY2021 to 1,902 in FY2024. That’s a statistically insignificant 1.4% increase. They even dropped between 2023 and 2024.

So what does that mean? We’re spending a lot more to get the same output. In FY2021, it cost about $10,874 per person housed. By FY2024, that number ballooned to $24,491 per person housed.

Twice the cost, same result. That’s the kind of stuff CEO’s get fired for — an offense far egregious than philandering at Coldplay concerts.

The lesson: treating homelessness effectively might take more than earmarking funding for “homelessness” and walking away.

This may not be a “set-it-and-forget-it” kind of thing.

Having Our Cake and Leasing It Too

If we build into our system the principle that no one should be forced to live outdoors, then we’re no longer simply talking about charity or social services. We’re talking about setting a floor under the entire real estate market. We’re saying that shelter is a right, not a speculative asset.

I don’t think we all actually agree to that, though. As a whole, we are not willing to make the sacrifices that coming with saying we believe housing is a human right. If shelter is the most basic human need, then this is modern society’s original sin.

Agreeing that everyone should have shelter changes everything. Because the moment we commit to that value, we’re in direct conflict with an economy that has grown fat off of scarcity. Landlords, banks, hedge funds, REITs, and private equity firms don’t profit when housing is abundant, accessible, and affordable—they profit when it is scarce, precarious, and leveraged.

This is why homelessness is the canary in the coal mine. It’s not just about the 3,500+ people without a home Nashville, or the hundreds of thousands across the country without shelter. It’s about the way our entire system treats housing as a vehicle for wealth extraction. The people on the street are simply the visible edge case of a system that’s quietly extracting from everyone else too—through rising rents, predatory mortgages, inflated property values, and cycles of debt.

So the question becomes: do we want an economy organized around the unfettered extraction of rent, or around the provision of shelter? Because we can’t have both.

I’m not naive enough to think that Nashville and Tennessee are ever going to try something actually progressive on purpose. But since we are so intensely, foolhardily, eagerly fucking around with making life unlivable here, we will have the benefit of being the first to find out how poorly that is going to go.

I give my thoughts here not for this administration and power players in the city, because I think they are too far gone, too pot committed, wearing blinders while steering towards the cliff in a nitroglycerine filled F-150 painted the slogan “Progressivism, baby!” .

But we have to know where we are to change where we’re going. And that means we have to look at the numbers that actually matter.

Which figures are actually relevant?

For the “CNBC crowd,” the bottom‑line types, success is measured in key performance indicators: money in, money out, ROI (return on invenstment). For the political class, success is keeping downtown clear and developers happy. For advocates, success is people stably housed. For the public, success is “less visible chaos.” Each group is looking at the same machine but judging it by different dashboards.

So let’s walk through it. The money. The contracts. The placements (or lack thereof). The removals. And let’s ask the real question:

Are we looking at the right metrics—or just the ones that make each group feel like they’re winning?

Because the machine is running. The questions are: who built it? what’s it built to do? and who’s it built to serve?

The $25 Million Developer Dividend

When the city announced $25 million in “gap financing” for permanent supportive housing, the expectation was bold — deliver tangible results. Let’s see what actually happened.

The Damning Pattern

When you line up the charts, the pattern jumps out:

  • FY2021 proved that money can house people—when the rules force it.

  • Since then, money has flowed without strings, and placements froze.

  • Costs per placement more than doubled, while visible homelessness remained unchanged.

The visual is damning:

  • Green bars (funding) shoot up like a rocket—527% higher by FY2024.

  • House icons (placements) stall at ~195% of baseline.

  • Tent icons (PIT counts) stay stubbornly flat, hovering right around the 100% baseline.

That divergence—the year the inputs take off and the outputs stop moving—is the smoking gun.

This isn’t failure. This is design. The system did what it was built to do in 2021 when the feds forced its hand. Since then, it’s gone back to what it’s really designed for: moving money, not housing people.

What can be done? How can we ever come to terms with this? Not only are we all not on the same page, we’re all on very different pages. Here are the perspectives I hear clattering around my mind.


The Dialectics of Homelessness

My family member who watches too much CNBC and calls himself a “day trader”

CNBC Guy: The shortage is about supply. Econ 101. We just don’t have enough units. Let builders build, and affordability follows.

Me: But in cities that built aggressively—Seattle, Austin—rents still outpaced wages.

CNBC Guy: Because demand exploded too. More jobs, more people. Prices rise when mouths outnumber meals.

Me: So the market builds for those with money, while the ones already priced out never catch up.

CNBC Guy: True in the short run. But over time, luxury units “filter down.”

Me: Filter down after how long? A decade? Two? What happens to the guy in the tent tonight?

CNBC Guy: …That’s the weak point, I’ll admit. Waiting doesn’t help him.

Me: So maybe supply matters, but only alongside something else—something that makes housing a right, not just an investment cycle.

CNBC Guy: You’re saying… like pulling part of the system out of the market? I hate the sound of that—but when I look at the guy on the street tonight, I don’t see another option.

My neighbor with the barking dog that’s proud to be called a NIMBY

Neighbor: I don’t oppose housing. I just don’t want my neighborhood ruined. We worked hard for this. Don’t we deserve to protect it?

Me: Protect it from what?

Neighbor: From crime, from blight, from losing the value of what we built.

Me: Are you maybe just afraid of seeing the neighborhood change? I don’t see it becoming dangerous, I’m actually seeing it gentrify and people like you not being able to afford it. Is keeping people down on their luck out of your neighborhood just your way of protecting it? Something you actually can control?

Neighbor: I’m not trying to put people on the street.

Me: But your status seems to depend on their exclusion. Maybe you can be one of the nouveau riche moving into a tall and skinny, but at least you’re not one of those people.

Neighbor: …That sounds harsher than I want it to be. I just want safety.

Me: But what if true safety isn’t gates or zoning, but knowing everyone has a place to be?

Neighbor: I mean, if everyone had housing as a guarantee, my neighborhood wouldn’t be under threat, that’s for sure.

Me: Exactly.

Neighbor: … Well I’m glad you see what I’m saying because I’ve always said it, protecting value isn’t about walls. Maybe it’s about making the world not a game of winners and losers, why can’t we all be in it together?

My friend the Advocate for the Unhoused Who Thinks the Best of Everyone

Advocate: Look, everyone wants fewer people outside. The question is: how?

Me: Do you really think the developer’s ROI and the unhoused person’s dignity are the same goal?

Advocate: I want to believe that—because otherwise the coalition falls apart.

Me: What if the coalition is already false?

Advocate: Then we’ve wasted years chasing compromise.

Me: Are you willing to lose actually being effective to prevent your ego from being bruised? What if the real coalition is between everyone who believes housing is a human right—even if that excludes most developers?

Advocate: You’re saying: stop pretending profit and dignity align.

Me: Yes. Be honest that the profit motive always puts itself before human dignity, that’s what being “profit driven” means. When it’s not, we just call it things like “financial sustainability.” Naming the conflict makes it real, and we can only make progress in the realm of the real, using the logic of the possible.

Advocate: …And the side we have to choose is decommodification, or else we’ll never get past these contradictions. I need to sit with that. That’s heavy.

The Highly Intelligent Conservative I Studied Philosophy With in College

Think Tanker: The problem is regulation. Zoning, rent control, subsidies—they choke supply. Housing is a market. Free it, and affordability follows.

Me: Deregulate, like finance? Healthcare? Energy?

Think Tanker: Those are different. In housing, regulation is the main distortion.

Me: So public housing fails because it’s public, vouchers fail because they’re subsidies, rent control fails because it’s a ceiling.

Think Tanker: Correct. The only solution is market freedom.

Me: Free for whom? Developers, or tenants?

Think Tanker: Affordability comes from supply. Everything else is distortion.

Me: Even when there are empty condos and people in tents two blocks apart?

Think Tanker: [smiles] That’s not the market’s fault. That’s just how it sorts people. Some belong inside, some don’t. The condos aren’t the problem. The tents are the proof.

The Unhoused Person I Buy the Contributor From

I hear all of you. Supply this, zoning that, ROI, coalitions, neighborhoods. You talk like there’s some balance to be found, some win-win waiting around the corner. But while you argue, I’m trying to find a place that doesn’t reek of piss to sleep tonight.

I’ve heard it all before: “build more and it’ll trickle down.” I don’t have ten years to wait for that trickle. “Protect the neighborhood.” From who—me? I’ve lived here longer than you. “Coalition.” I’ve watched those meetings. The only thing that changes is the pastries on the table.

The only time the system worked was during COVID, when they just cut the damn checks. Then they stopped, and here we are again. Back on the sidewalk.

So let me tell you the truth: the only one of you I can stomach is the Think Tanker. Don’t get me wrong — I hope there’s a deep, hot circle of hell waiting for you. But at least you’re honest about how you’ve designed this world. You don’t dress it up in compassion or talk about “eventually.” You admit it’s dog eat dog, and some of us are meant to be eaten.

And that’s why I almost respect it. Because the rest of you? You’re just lying to yourselves while I rot out here.
