Two weeks ago, I helped a woman, “Taylor,” fill out paperwork against her former employer for withholding her paycheck — Latino immigrants are among the most common victims of wage theft. I’ve gotten stiffed working freelance jobs before and it really messed me up. For one it got me into steep credit card debt for years, but more than that it really messed with my trust in others and myself.

Taylor was owed over $3,000 dollars for cleaning services, and when she was asked to get paid for her work (as one does), she was sent away with threats and insults. Virtually penniless and with children to feed, she sought help from a group I volunteer with to get her fairly compensated. We resolve conflicts between workers and their employers to avoid law suits that are costly and time consuming for everyone.

I didn’t know that Taylor was a victim of sex trafficking in Mexico by narco-cartel members. I didn’t know she had fled here not for a better life, but to simply be alive.

I didn’t know at the time Taloy was dealing domestic abuse at home.

I didn’t know at the time that Taylor was newly pregnant. If I did, I would have told her “Felicidades.”

She was very sweet and kind. Quiet and patient with me and my broken Spanglish. The last thing I said to her was probably: “Gracias! No problemo.”

Before January 20, 2025, she would have been a shoo-in for a T Visa for victims of human trafficking or a U Visa for victims of violent crime, according to the immigration lawyer in my family. Taylor is exactly what our asylum system was designed to help. Like the rest of us, who came here as poor, tired, huddled masses — she’s who this country is made for and made by.

On Monday, Taylor was deported to Alabama en route to a port of departure — likely under the new administration’s expanded use of Expedited Removal policies to forcefully eject anyone who comes into ICE’s jurisdiction and has been here less than two years. The most likely outcome is she will be returned to Mexico. Like many immigrants who return under such conditions, she may be murdered by the cartel for speaking against them and escaping their clutches.

That messed me up.

The assumption of those supporting those restrictions is that “guardrails” around how MNPD handles data will prevent the extremes of misuse, and it’ll only be used in the event of investigating legitimate crimes or serious emergencies.

It’s about catching mass shooters and chronic vandalizers, the argument goes. Not about scooping up people like Taylor.

It a reasonable question, but doesn’t get at the nuance of things like immigration law, a thing which I realize I may be more familiar with than a lot of people on Council.

Even before Jan 20, 2025, immigration cases are not about events — things that happened or didn’t happen. The “administrative judges” in immigration court are appointed by the President and the US Attorney General — they are often not lawyers and they exist fully outside the Judicial system. Immigration status cases are not about what someone did, they’re about going through a bureocratic check-list of who someone is and seeing if they fit into any boxes that allow them to stay or require them to leave.

And those boxes have been changing rapidly, through no fault of people like Taylor.

The Executive Branch has little problem changing the boxes that people fall into, quickly reclassifying people’s status as “illegal” when it suits their political whims.

Where this becomes an issue of ICE is not having the authority to deport people — especially with the passage of Federal and State laws like TNs that require compliance from all level of law enforcement.

The problem merely becomes finding people within the proximity of crimes whom they can demand, in that single word mantra of the Gestapo: “Papers?”

But one must find potential crimes by which one has the authority to ask people for their status. Cities like Nashville, with Active Dispatch dashboards, real the tremendous opportunity that is ripe for the picking by agencies like ICE.

Nashville averages 160 “active dispatches” per day, where police are not only contacted via 911 or otherwise, but sent to a particular location to respond to a particular crime or emergency.

The most common dispatch by far, is a “commercial burglary alarm.” There are 64 of those a day, accounting for 40% of the total active dispatches. They happen all over the city, but the highest rates are in Hermitage, Antioch, Woodbine, and South.

But this does not mean that we have an epidemic of commercial burglaries. In fact, 96.17% of these are false alarms — caused, according to ADT, primarily by:

  1. Unaware employees [untrained on how to properly arm and disarm systems]

  2. Improperly maintained equipment [dead batteries, dust, damage, and out of date]

  3. Internal environment problems [e.g. merchandise bumping into motion detectors]

  4. Animals(!) [exclamation point theirs]

A camera system that is connected to publicly owned cameras that can be linked to “calls for service” is a goldmine for an ICE agent that has a quota to make.

With a tool like Fusus, their job can be as simple as looking at the active dispatch dashboard for one of the 74 (almost certainly false alarm) burglary alarms that go off each day, pulling up Fusus cameras in that area, and looking for “people that look illegal.”
