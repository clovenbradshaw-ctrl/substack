Obligatory Magician’s Patter

In most of my published writing, I've shown my work. I cite, I explain, I lay out the stepping stones from premise to conclusion. I show my path of deduction, coming to conclusions only after showing my inductions. You know, journalism / political analysis type stuff.

A friend told me recently to stop. "Stop showing your work," he said. "You should do magic tricks."

That unsettled me.

I never had a magician phase as a kid — no rabbits, no hats. My partner was shocked when she learned that, because apparently I radiate "sleight-of-hand enthusiast." I’m mildly offended, but obviously I get it. But while I never obsessed over card tricks, I do have strong feelings about magic. Not the flowers out of a wand kind — but the social kind. The kind that divides the world into illusions that ensnare people with lies vs. ecstatic performances that lead us to wonder and possibility.

I've been thinking that what we need is more Woodwards and Bernsteins.

People speaking the truth about power to the people, reminding them they, the people, are the power. And its clear, we do need that. But if I'm honest, journalism has been kind of a detour for me. One I’ll probably return to in some form. But I’m maybe too impatient for its results. I do love a newspaper movie. My vibe is very much that Mark Ruffalo scene in Spotlight where he screams they knew! But, I’m finding out quickly, there is a limit to the transformational power of facts — especially as we barrel headlong into a new yet familiar century where the very idea of truth and who is a human are up for debate.

This is not really the age of newspaper men. The idea that a bombshell article drops and the world acknowledges it and adjusts to this information is, we can hope, merely on pause (but slated to return). Some might say that since truth itself is the thing on trial, this is where philosophers or even theologians should step up. These are important players in the grand spiral of consciousness, but they’re more custodians of history, cartographers at best — they’re not the ones who shape it. Where’s the juice these days? I’d argue, with much reason but also, more importantly, from what’s in my heart: that we are entering an era of magicians.

So, let’s try our hand at magic.

The Pledge: I can make you see the world differently. I can do it just by putting letters, one after another, in a certain order. It’s not quite hypnotism, but it could be much more long lasting if you let it. I can shift the underlying values that ground your everyday decisions, actions, big and small, and sculpt your desires to yearn for something better than we've had. Something far better than we're going to be getting for a long while. Something new yet very, very old.

Interested?

This is a Meta-Modern Magic, that means it is Post-Post-Modern. You’re not just audience, you’re not just participant, you are the magic trick. But like all good magicians, I will ask for your consent — not just because its the polite thing to do, but because its necessary for the trick to work.

So, you wanna see a magic trick?

What I'm about to do with you is a magic trick. Not a séance trick — not a “trust me, I have higher powers, I went to Yale and worked at McKinsey" trick. A Houdini trick.

Built on practice, endurance, discipline — and very much achievable by a human. That's the magic. Houdini risked drowning in locked tanks, suffocating in coffins, dislocating shoulders to slip chains — not to pretend he was superhuman, but to prove that humans are capable of more than we believe. He is a kind of Christ figure for modern stage magicians: not a god who hoarded divinity, but one who insists the divine is already human.

That ethic came from somewhere. Houdini — born Erik Weisz — was the son of a rabbi who fled Hungary. He knew, in his bones, that authoritarian order is a cage disguised as stability. His life of escape — from ropes, tanks, prisons — was never just spectacle. It was an answer to the world that tried to bind him.

And governance — the way we run our societies, our democracies, our daily agreements about who decides what — desperately needs Houdini magic right now.

By the time we're done, if you keep your attention in the right place (as all good magic requires), you'll see governance differently. You'll see how consultants, philanthropists, and technocrats have become our séance frauds, how strongmen have risen in reaction, and how the escape is possible if we relearn Houdini's ethic.

But first, we need Sherlock Holmes.

Arthur Conan Doyle — creator of Sherlock Holmes, high priest of reason — became Houdini's friend, then his antithesis.

Doyle misnamed Holmes a "deductive" genius. In fact, Holmes worked by induction: mud on boots, ashes in trays, footprints on carpets. He found patterns in noise, seeing meaning where others saw chaos. Holmes was a master of uncertainty. Doyle thought he had written certainty itself.

But Doyle craved certainty just as the world dissolved into irrationality. The trained medical doctor with a great, learned devotion to reason, his logic could not survive the first great catastrophy. World War I left only corpses, trauma, and emboldened capitalists, its causes too tangled for reason to make sense of its outcomes. And into that void stepped betrayal myths, poisoned nationalism, ancient hatreds recycled as clarity, the same reasons Houdini’s family, like millions more would continue to, had fled Europe for America.

Doyle lived the collapse of his eras reason intimately. His son Kingsley was wounded at the Battle of the Somme and died of pneumonia in 1918. Reason confirmed only futility. So Doyle turned to spiritualism. Psychic mediums offered what logic could not: that Kingsley still spoke, that death was not final.

Doyle believed so deeply, and wanted to share this faith with the world’s great skeptics, that his wife staged a séance for Houdini, "channeling" the escape artists’ mother. But every clue screamed fraud — speaking English instead of her native tongue, "Harry" instead of his real name, a Christian cross scrawled out from a Jewish mother. Holmes would have spotted it instantly. Doyle missed them all.

That is the consultant's trick in miniature: brilliant on the surface, wrong in the details, yet believed — because the client is desperate to avoid the one thing they cannot face.

For Doyle, it was death. For our institutions, it's democracy.

Houdini knew better. He knew the séance was patter, the miracle was practice. What looks impossible can be done in daylight. In 1924, he exposed a famous medium in Boston not by attacking her beliefs, but by recreating her "miracles" on stage — showing exactly how the locked cabinet opened from inside, how the "spirit voices" emerged from hidden speakers. The crowd saw possibility, not divinity.

And that's the lesson for us now: governance has fallen under the same spell. The séance has become the PowerPoint, the Ouija board the KPI dashboard, the ghost raps the "impact metrics."

When a hospital must choose between services, when a city must pick which neighborhoods get resources, when any institution faces scarcity — these are political questions requiring democratic answers. Who decides? By what right? With whose consent?

But democracy is messy. It's slow. It means conflict, compromise, and worst of all for those who like to feel powerful, accountability. It means admitting that someone must choose, and others might choose differently. It means accepting that citizens have the right to be wrong, together. Or more to the point, to be right, together, but without you.

So instead, we hire McKinsey. Or accept help from the Bloomberg Foundation. They arrive with dashboards and experts whose corporate experience is vast but whose government experience extends to renewing their driver's license.

They come from a world that is, by definition, authoritarian — the corporation, where no one votes on policy, no one below them elects the CEO, no one has rights except those granted from above. When was the last time anyone signed a workplace constitution or cast a binding vote on their company's direction? The corporate world is oligarchy dressed in business casual.

To these consultants, democracy isn't a feature of government — it's a bug. Public hearings, community input, elected oversight: these are inefficiencies to be optimized out of existence. Better to leave vital decisions to those with the right diplomas from the right schools, who will make the choices once they get around to it. The messy business of collective governance, where ordinary people have a say in their own fate, is precisely what they're hired to circumvent.

The consultant's real service isn't analysis; it's absolution. They take the burden of democratic choice and transform it into technical necessity. The séance medium helps you avoid accepting death; the consultant helps you avoid accepting democracy. Both let you pretend you're not the one making the choice.

The bereaved Victorians, unable to accept death, turned to mediums who promised the dead still spoke. Our institutions, unable to accept democratic limits, turn to consultants who promise the data speaks for itself. Both conduct the same ritual: denying human responsibility by invoking higher authority.

So with our table set, our setting primed, let's dim the lights, sit at the table, and call the spirits.

The magic begins now.

The Séance of Power

A Dialogue on Stage in Three Acts

Cast

  • Houdini — escaped from his own grave to unmask illusions

  • Sherlock Holmes — brilliant detective, all logic and [in]deduction

  • Consultant — business-casual medium, conjures dashboards

  • Philanthropist — earnest, candlelit aura of benevolence

  • CEO — sharp, controlling, thinks the séance is a board meeting

  • Strongman — booming, candle-flame shadow looms large

  • Citizen — ordinary presence, keeps breaking the spell

  • Worker — tough, skeptical, hands calloused from the real

  • Unhoused — gaunt but unshakable; speaks in plain truths

ACT I — Void, Mark, Instance

(Recognizing absence, naming it, bringing it into form)

(A round table draped in black. Nine candles flicker. Dashboards glow faintly in the darkness. They sit, hands nearly touching.)

Consultant: Spirits of efficiency, reveal yourselves! Through data we shall be guided, through metrics redeemed. Look — here, the patterns show themselves. Bus routes optimizing, permit times accelerating, satisfaction scores rising—

Holmes: (leaning forward with interest) Excellent methodology! You're isolating variables, tracking patterns. I've been known to say it, ‘Data! Data! Data! I can't make bricks without clay.’

Worker: Strange. Your data says buses run on time, but I'm late every day. Your permit times are faster, but I've been waiting three months for approval to fix my mother's roof. Your satisfaction scores rise, but no one asked me if I'm satisfied.

Consultant: But that's merely anecdotal. The system shows 95% efficiency across all measured parameters. Individual experiences, while perhaps personally meaningful, don't negate the aggregate success.

Holmes: You can never foretell what any one man will do, but you can say with precision what an average number will be up to. Individuals vary, but percentages remain constant.

Houdini: (turning a coin over his knuckles) Aggregate success. Measured parameters. You measure what arrives, never what's missing. The mothers who stopped waiting. The workers who stopped trying. Tell me, when you measure a magic trick, what exactly are you measuring? The speed of my hands? The gasps from the audience? Or just what you've decided counts as "magic"?

CEO: Someone has to decide what counts. That's what leadership is — making the hard choices about what matters, what gets prioritized, what gets measured. Without that, you have chaos.

Citizen: But who gave you the right to decide? When did we vote on what gets measured? I keep hearing about these improvements, but my neighborhood looks the same. Worse, actually.

Philanthropist: This is exactly why we need multiple stakeholders at the table! I've spent years building bridges between communities and institutions. I bring different voices together, ensure everyone has representation—

Unhoused: (laughing bitterly) Representation? You bring us to YOUR building, serve us YOUR coffee, ask us to speak for ten minutes at YOUR gala. Then you go back to your board and tell them you've heard from "the community." But you never come to where we actually gather. You never let us set the agenda.

Strongman: And this is the chaos I'm talking about! Democracy fails precisely because everyone thinks their view matters equally. The patient doesn't get to tell the surgeon how to operate. The passenger doesn't fly the plane.

Worker: But we're not patients. We're not passengers. We're the ones keeping your city running. I fix your pipes, drive your buses, clean your offices. Without us, you don't have a city to measure.

Strongman: Of course you matter — as workers. But governance requires vision, strategy, the ability to see the whole picture. Not everyone can do that.

Holmes: (intrigued by the argument) There is a certain logic to expertise creating natural hierarchies. In criminal investigation, for instance, the untrained eye misses critical evidence. Experience and training do matter, and creating a class of experts to make the decisions of society does follow, Socratically speaking.

Houdini: (producing a key from thin air) Interesting theory, detective. But tell me — when I was chained underwater, was it experts that saved me? Or was it knowing exactly how the locks were built? Every medium I've exposed started as an expert. They had certificates, testimonials, wealthy patrons. My mother's ghost supposedly spoke through one. Funny thing — she never spoke English when alive, but death apparently gave her perfect pronunciation.

Holmes: A confounding contradiction, indeed. As I've said before, I don't make exceptions — exceptions disprove rules.

Houdini: I did not teach myself to pick locks so I could be put into water tanks. I learned how to do it so I could get out of them. True experts are the people most invested in saving themselves and others from disaster. Expertise, as our friend the consultant is practicing it, is something else entirely.

Holmes: You're suggesting expertise itself is a kind of... performance?

Consultant: (defensive) We're not performing! We're designating problems — Category 3 non-compliance, no wait, 3-B... Once marked, they exist. Unmarked, they don't. We're identifying inefficiencies, creating solutions—

Holmes: Yes, exactly! To name is to capture. Once we identify the criminal, we can pursue them. Naming is the first step to solving.

Unhoused: You named me "homeless" and suddenly I became a problem to solve, not a person to know. You named my situation "inefficiency" instead of "injustice." Names aren't neutral — they're spells you cast to make the world match your spreadsheet.

Philanthropist: But surely some categorization is necessary? How else do we allocate resources, track progress, ensure help reaches those who need it?

Worker: You track us like packages. Input here, output there. But we're not goods to be processed. We're people trying to live.

CEO: (firmly) And people need systems. I manifested myself from mailroom to boardroom — from nothing into form. I know what it's like at the bottom. But I also know that without structure, without measurement, without accountability, nothing improves. We create forms and frameworks because they work.

Houdini: (standing, chains appearing on his wrists) Forms and frameworks. Like these chains? They're real iron, real locks. The audience sees them, trusts them. But watch — (the chains fall away) — every form is also a deception. The question is: who holds the keys?

ACT II — Boundary, Bridge, Fusion

(Division for clarity, connection across gaps, synthesis)

(The candles burn steadier now. Dashboards multiply, showing different data sets.)

Consultant: Let me show you how the segmented analysis works. We divide the city's challenges into discrete categories — housing here, employment there, transit in this sector. Each has its own metrics, its own solutions. It's the only way to manage complexity.

CEO: (approving) Beautiful. Each department handles their piece. Clean lines of accountability. No overlap, no confusion. In my company, we call it "staying in your lane."

Worker: That's the problem — you've drawn so many lanes, I can't get where I need to go. My rent connects to my job, my job connects to my commute, my commute connects to my kids' school. You split them on your dashboard, but I live them all at once. When the bus is late, I lose my job. When I lose my job, I lose my home. Your lanes are my maze.

Consultant: That's not a maze, that's just... overlap. We have integration protocols for that. Interagency coordination. Cross-sector collaboration.

Philanthropist: Exactly! This is where my foundation comes in. We bridge those gaps. We're the connective tissue between rigid government departments. Everything needs connection. No island should exist. We can move faster, be more flexible, respond to needs as they arise—

Holmes: (seeing a pattern) Like tracking evidence across jurisdictions! Different departments, but one investigation. The mud from Westminster connects to the crime in Whitechapel. Brilliant coordination.

Houdini: But who coordinates the coordinators? You've made yourself the bridge, but that means everyone has to cross through you. You're not connecting people to each other — you're making yourself indispensable to the connection.

Philanthropist: (defensive) Someone has to do it! Government is too slow, too bureaucratic. Communities don't have resources. We fill a vital gap—

Unhoused: You CREATE the gap by filling it. I live in that gap. Neither your side nor mine. The line itself. Government cuts services because you'll provide them. You provide what YOU think I need, with whatever strings YOU attach. Then you wonder why I'd rather sleep outside than in your shelter with your rules.

Strongman: This is what happens with too many cooks in the kitchen! Everyone pulling different directions, no unified vision. One organism — we must melt into unity. Separate fingers must become a fist. One leader, one system, one direction — that's how you get things done.

CEO: (interested) Vertical integration. I've done it in my company. We acquired our suppliers, our distributors. Now everything flows through one decision-making structure. Efficiency went up 40%.

Worker: And we can't breathe at the bottom of your vertical. Every decision flows down, but does our reality ever flow up? You've integrated everything except our voices.

Holmes: (pattern crystallizing) Wait... you're all describing the same structure, just... inverted. Each of you places yourself at the center of your own web. The consultant's data hub, the philanthropist's bridge, the CEO's hierarchy, the strongman's pyramid...

Citizen: And we're always at the edges. No matter how you draw it, we're peripheral.

Houdini: (dealing cards on the table) Now you're beginning to see the trick, detective. Every system puts someone in the middle, dealing the cards. But who shuffled the deck?

Consultant: There's no trick! The methodology is sound! Evidence-based, peer-reviewed, statistically significant—

Holmes: (pressing) Yet, there is nothing more deceptive than an obvious fact. If all your peers review from the same position, if all your evidence comes from the same source, if your statistics only count what you've already decided matters...

CEO: Now you're talking philosophy. I deal in reality. Markets, profits, growth — measurable reality.

Citizen: YOUR reality. Built on OUR labor. Which you measure as "cost."

Strongman: (frustrated) This is exactly the paralysis democracy creates! Everyone arguing, no one leading. You need synthesis, yes — but synthesis through strength, through will, through decision!

Unhoused: You mean submission. You want us to fuse into your vision. But what if we have our own?

Worker: What if the fusion happens between us, not under you? What if we connect to each other without going through your bridges?

Philanthropist: (worried) But without coordination, without structure, it would be—

Citizen: It would be community. Which we had before you “improved” us.

ACT III — Rhythm, Overlay, Spiral

(Oscillation, multiple truths, recursive recognition)

(The table begins rocking — power, resistance, power, resistance. The candles flicker in rhythm.)

Strongman: Look at this instability! This is what democracy gives you — wild swings, no consistency. Election here, protest there, policy reversing every few years. It's chaos!

CEO: Markets swing too, but they self-correct. That's the beauty of it — natural equilibrium through competition. Supply, demand, the invisible hand guiding everything toward efficiency.

Worker: You mean WE correct them. With our layoffs when you need better quarterly numbers. With our foreclosures when you need assets. With our bankruptcies when your bets go bad. Late, on time, late, on time. Hire, fire, hire, fire. My whole life is... (rocks slightly) ...systole, diastole. The invisible hand has very visible fingers, and they're around our throats.

Consultant: (frantically typing) The dashboards are showing contradictory data. Employment is up but wages are down. Housing starts are up but homelessness increased. GDP rises while life expectancy falls. This doesn't make sense—

Holmes: (fascinated and disturbed) Unless... unless they're not contradictory. Multiple truths occupying the same space. The same city being several cities at once...

Philanthropist: That's impossible! Either poverty is rising or falling. Either we're succeeding or failing. You can't have both—

Unhoused: It's both. Poverty falls in your spreadsheet while it rises in my tent. Success in your gala speech, failure on my street corner. You measure one city and live in another.

Houdini: (spinning a glass globe) Every truth depends on where you're standing when you measure it. From above, the globe spins smoothly. From inside, it's earthquake after earthquake.

CEO: This is sophistry! Reality is reality! Profits are profits! You can't argue with numbers—

Citizen: Your numbers count me as employed but not that I'm starving, housed but not that I'm drowning, improving while I'm dying. When we're priced out, we disappear from your data. When we stop looking for work, we're not "unemployed" anymore. We become invisible, and you call it recovery.

(The room seems to spiral. Holmes grips the table, his knuckles white. His face flickers between certainty and recognition.)

Holmes: We're... we're going in circles. The same arguments, the same positions, the same failures, just... rearranged... The evidence suggests... but wait, the evidence suggests... if we return to the evidence...

Consultant: No! Linear progress! Quarter over quarter improvement! The trendlines all go up—

Houdini: Lines and circles, Detective. The recursion is what allows there to be growth, experience, memory, emergence. Remember your Archimedes, Detective. Spirals. Same problems, different heights.

Holmes: Each revolution you think you've escaped, but you're just one floor higher in the same tower prison —

Houdini: But you are wiser for it, Holmes. You see from a higher vantage point. But there is a roof, go to the roof. You are getting closer. Read the spiral, Detective, what does it show you about the tower?

Strongman: (sensing opportunity) This is exactly why you need someone to break the tower of indecision! It is Babel! Cut through the endless debate with decision! Action! Will! “Progress” is folly, all that matters is will and destiny!

Holmes: (revelation crashing over him) No... no, the spiral IS the system. We keep returning because... because we're not solving anything. We're just... we're just... That field. Always that field. I leave it and I'm back in it. I solve it and it's unsolved.

(Holmes's form flickers, wavers, his face shifting)

Holmes: Every deduction I ever made... every pattern I found... I was forcing the evidence to speak. Making the dead tell me stories. Creating order where there was only chaos...

Houdini: (gentle but pressing) What were you trying to resurrect, detective?

Holmes: The evidence suggests... but Spencer said evidence wasn't needed if you'd already decided... Huxley wasn't even curious... They taught me to deduce, then refused to look at my deductions...

Houdini: You’re with us now, aren’t you Arthur. Sherlock isn’t here, it’s Arthur now, isn’t it?

(Holmes's face shifts, wavers, becomes Arthur Conan Doyle — older, haunted)

Arthur Conan Doyle: (suddenly quiet, almost conversational) In Edinburgh we were admonished for any indulgence outside the nascent empiricist canon, such as it was we were inventing the science we call medicine today. But I would sneak Pascal into my private hours, Pensées being my reprieve from Harvey and vivasections. The first rote lesson I taught him was 282, Nous connaissons la vérité, non seulement par la raison, mais encore par le cœur, “We know the truth not only through our reason but also through our heart.”

Houdini: Who did you teach that to, Arthur?

Arthur Conan Doyle: We… we had an hour's talk in a field. There was nowhere else to go. May 1916. He was in his uniform. The Somme stretched out behind him. I told him... I told him...

(His voice cracks. The others begin to flicker, their edges blurring)

Houdini: You can say it, Arthur.

Arthur Conan Doyle: (screaming) MY BOY!

(The scream shatters something. The Consultant's face ripples, becomes younger Doyle)

CEO: (face shifting between CEO and Doyle) Built the empire so he'd never— I formed him into what would die— medical research, he wanted medical research— I wrote "To Arms!"— I encouraged—

(They begin breathing in unison, a horrible synchronization. The Philanthropist rocks forward)

Philanthropist: (now clearly Doyle, but desperate) Every séance, every medium, they promised— I built bridges to nowhere! Séances to silence!— his voice through the veil— but the mediums lied, they all lied—

(The Worker, Citizen, begin swaying together, their faces sliding between themselves and Doyle at different ages)

Worker/Doyle: (exhausted) Two shrapnel bullets in the neck— Breathe, stop breathing, breathe, stop breathing—

Citizen/Doyle: (hollow) Every officer of the Hampshires fell beside him. Their fathers all grieved, they lost boys. But their boys were not mine, not Kingsly. He was going to be a real scientist, a real man, not a pulpist like me, selling murders by the dime.

Consultant/Doyle: (barely there) October... pneumonia... He crossed the only boundary that matters... the flu took him after the war couldn't...

(They're all rocking now, breathing as one organism, supporting each other like mourners, faces shifting constantly — sometimes themselves, sometimes Doyle at 55 writing war propaganda, sometimes Doyle at 59 learning of Kingsley's death, sometimes Doyle conducting séances)

All: (not quite in unison, voices sliding over each other) My boy... my boy... Kingsley... my dear boy...

(The CEO suddenly snaps into focus as himself for a moment)

CEO: (confused) I'm not— I don't have a—

(His face immediately shifts back to Doyle)

CEO/Doyle: Crowned with the sunshine of immortal youth—

(The chant builds, but it's broken, arrhythmic — each voice catching on different memories)

Consultant/Doyle: The model didn't predict—

Philanthropist/Doyle: Spanish flu, October 28th—

Strongman/Doyle: I wrote him there, I wrote them all there—

Worker/Citizen (in horrible harmony) My boy my boy my boy—

(They collapse toward each other, eight aspects of the same grief, each one a different failed attempt to control fate — through detection, data, charity, force, democracy, labor, poverty. All of them the same father who sent his son to war with his words.)

CEO: (touching his face) What... what was that? I felt—

Worker: (shaking) I was somewhere else. A field. There was a young man—

Consultant: (staring at hands) The data... I kept trying to calculate survival rates but they were from 1916...

Philanthropist: (weeping without knowing why) October 28th. Why do I know that date?

Houdini: (gentle, to the broken Doyle) Arthur... they were all you. Every system of control, every attempt at order — they were all you trying to undo that moment in the field.

Arthur Conan Doyle: (barely audible) I wrote "To Arms!" I was in the propaganda bureau. Everyone feels guilt when they lose a child, but mine is real and it can’t be undone. My words... my words sent them marching. Sent him marching.

Strongman: (confused, angry) I’ve sent generations to the slaughter to keep the dream of empire alive! I will not break destiny over one dead … no… no… no…

(He flickers again, briefly Doyle, then back)

Strongman: (horrified) Oh god. Oh god, I felt it. The dizzying certainty that force could fix all things. That strength could—

Unhoused: (as if in a divine trance, now speaking like sweetly in rapture) What’s lost can never be returned. But what’s broken can never be healed, until you let it be broken. There are many rivers that lead to the sea, and there are many ways to arrive at my Mother’s Temple. Your path has been taking reason to its limits, training the mind on the purity of pattern until the illusions of the temporary world fall away, scales dropping from your eyes. This is but one of the infinite paths to ultimate truth. Mine was keeping faith through long suffering, praying to be held, to be held, to be held — not by man and all his failings and grief, but the divine grace in all its infinite compassion and joy. But we arrive at the same place, seeing the pattern of patterns from on high. Welcome, Sir Doyle.

Houdini: The séance was never about speaking to the dead, Arthur. It was about refusing to accept that we are ultimately powerless to keep what we love alive. Your detective, your logic, your perfect mind, that was your mind palace. And when it failed to keep the ones you loved safe, you turned to every other system that humanity builds. Data, hierarchy, charity, force, markets... all of them you, trying to control what can't be controlled.

(They stand, one by one, shaky but present. No longer Doyle's refractions but themselves — or perhaps, for the first time, actually themselves. The table has changed. The nine candles now light a different scene — not a séance, but simply a table where humans sit.)

Citizen: So what now? We've all been... shadows? Puppets in someone else's grief?

Worker: Not puppets. Patterns. The same pattern of control, repeated in different costumes.

Unhoused: But we're also real. Our struggles are real. Even if we started as his projections, we're here now. Separate. Alive.

Houdini: Nine moves happened here tonight. Nine human actions. Nine locks, Arthur. You've been trying to pick them all. You felt them all — the void that had to be acknowledged, the names we gave our grief, the forms we built to contain it, the boundaries we drew to manage it, the bridges we built over it, the fusion we feared might heal it, the rhythm we fell into to avoid it, the overlays that revealed it, the spiral that brought us back to it. But the first lock — the void — isn't about bringing back what's missing. It's about letting absence be absence.

(He spreads his hands, and nine cards appear on the table — simple, worn, human-made)

Houdini: These aren't spirits to channel, forces we can unleash onto our world without care. They are locks binding shackles to our chest. But every lock is human-made. Most especially the ones that grief builds around our hearts.

Consultant: (looking at broken dashboards) The data was supposed to be neutral... but it was all just... A séance...

CEO: Every system we build, trying to prevent loss, becomes its own cage.

Philanthropist: (to Unhoused) What do you actually need? Not what Doyle thought you needed, not what his grief projected... what do YOU need?

Unhoused: To be asked that question. And believed when I answer. And maybe... to exist as more than someone else's lesson about loss.

Strongman: (confused, smaller now) I’ve thought I was the most powerful, but I was a puppet — the dark shadow of enforcement beneath democracy’s unfinished dream of complexity. I was not in control, I was the force that emerges from our psychic slumber, there to tear down false Republics, but not to build something better. That’s not a thing I know how to do yet, all I can point out is that we were abandoning the dream of progress for the fantasy of efficiency. But I am tired of just being the reaction to other people’s failures.

Worker: We make our own order. Not from grief, not from control. But from necessity. From care. You just never saw it because you were too busy being someone else's nightmare of power.

Strongman: We can express will and destiny, but not my will. Not the limited will of nation states, ghostly forms whose shadows are real people left out of the illusion. But I think, perhaps, with more certainty that we ever had before, we can fight for the divine destiny of it all. Not us vs them, but We, the great, non-dual, We of the universe, fighting not to conquer, but to better know itself.

Citizen: We're all waking up from the same dream, dreamt from different beds.

Arthur Conan Doyle: (standing with difficulty) I'm sorry... I made you all to carry what I couldn't...

Houdini: That's what humans do, Arthur. We make systems from our scars. The trick is learning when to escape them.
