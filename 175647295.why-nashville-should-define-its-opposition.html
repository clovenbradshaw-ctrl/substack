The most politically impactful criticism of how America’s strongest ally in the Middle East is conducting this war, two years in, is not coming from progressives or those of us on the Left who have always opposed it.

It’s now coming from the Right itself.
And that shift creates both an opportunity and a danger.

When I saw that the Nashville Metro Council was going to be discussing a resolution to condemn the way Israel has been executing its war in Gaza, I wondered if I had anything to say on the topic. I have been a vocal opponent to the way this war has been executed, and attended my fair share of pro-cease fire protests.

But I still wondered, is this a topic that the Davidson County Metro Council had much business weighing in on?

I know enough about the Israel and Palestine conflict to agree with anyone that says its origins and history are extremely “complicated.” What seems very uncomplicated to me is that the Oct 7th attacks two years ago and the actions of the Netanyahu government and Israeli military since then are horrific.

Holding these two conflicting truths is hard.

But this is something that people across the political aisle are increasingly finding alignment on. Polling is demonstrating a huge decrease in support for the war amongst conservatives, and even some of the most MAGA-aligned political figures are denouncing the actions of the Netanyahu government and the Israeli military.

It’s the most truthful and easiest thing to say that Oct 7th in Israel was horrific and all hostages must be returned, but so is the genocide, humanitarian crisis, and starvation happening in Gaza.

For what it’s worth, the Metro Nashville resolution sticks to the facts about what leading agencies have said about the way Israel’s response to the Oct 7 attacks have been implemented.

WHEREAS, international bodies and agencies including the UN Human Rights Council, The International Criminal Court, The International Association of Genocide Scholars, Genocide Watch, Amnesty International, Human Rights Watch, and human rights activists, including Israeli academics and campaign groups, have each conducted investigations and concluded that the actions of the State of Israel in Palestine constitute war crimes, crimes against humanity, and genocide;

But what business does the Metro Council have weighing in on this? It is decidedly out of their purview in terms of anything they can procedurally do anything about. The words of resolution like this are merely that, words.

But then I saw Marsha Blackburn’s tweet. As a US Senator, she very much has things she can do about this catastrophe, and perhaps more importantly, the words she uses to describe what is happening are deeply troubling.

This helped me realize that this very much is a thing that is relevant to Tennessee and, more to the point, Nashville.

See, while Blackburn’s political backers would like to deny it, political support for the destruction of Palestine and the humans living there is declining — most sharply amongst young Republicans.

A Quinnipiac University poll found Republican sympathy for Israelis dropped 14 percentage points in a single year, from 78% in May 2024 to 64% in June 2025. The Pew Research center shows that the more significant shift appears among younger party members, where confidence in Prime Minister Netanyahu among Republicans under age 50 stands at just 32% compared to 70% among those over 50, revealing a 38-point generational divide.

The Pew Research Center breakdown is very telling for the future of political conservative coalitions that once thought they could count on pro-Israel policies no matter what.

Among younger Republicans:

  • In 2022: 63% favorable vs. 35% unfavorable toward Israel (net +28 margin)

  • In 2025: 48% favorable vs. 50% unfavorable (essentially even split)

  • Result: A 30-point swing in three years, crossing from net positive to net negative territory

  • Half of Republicans aged 18-49 now hold negative views of Israel

In stark contrast, Republican support for Trump remains stable:

  • Trump’s approval among Republicans: 91% (July 2025)

  • 85% of his 2024 voters continue to approve of his job performance

  • Only modest declines from 92-95% approval at the start of his second term

  • Trump’s 40% national approval is driven almost entirely by Democratic opposition

  • Republicans remain near-unanimous in their support at approximately 90%

The divergence reveals something significant about the nature of conservative political identity. While Trump has maintained lockstep Republican support even through controversial policies and declining overall popularity, Israel has lost half of young Republicans in just three years. This suggests that support for Israel, once considered a foundational pillar of conservative foreign policy, may be more conditional and generationally fragile than support for Trump himself.

The most politically impactful criticism of how America’s strongest ally in the Middle East is conducting this war, two years in, is not coming from progressives or those of us on the Left who have always opposed it.

It’s now coming from the Right itself.
And that shift creates both an opportunity and a danger.

The tide of public opinion is turning for the Israeli occupation of Gaza. But the words about why this support is faltering are important to put on the record. Blackburn’s message about the proposed resolution condemning genocide embodies a cruel, Manichean dualism that demands Americans choose absolute sides.

In her framework, one cannot hold two truths simultaneously. It cannot acknowledge that harm is inflicted and received by human beings on both sides, most of whom genuinely perceive themselves as the aggrieved party. This binary thinking forecloses the nuanced understanding necessary for any meaningful path forward.

It’s not clear to me how sincere this belief is, or if it is done for political expediency.

But the problem is, when leaders do not make it explicit that no one, not even our strongest allies, is above the expectations to treat civilians humanely, even those whose governments have committed atrocities, voices start filling the vacuum with the oldest prejudice of Western society: antisemitism.

And that is very much a thing that is connected to Nashville, and is why our local leadership has a duty to speak about why support for the way Israel is conducting the war in Gaza is declining.

What I’m realizing is that this resolution before the Metro Council is not an opportunity for opponents of the war to gain more traction. It’s an opportunity to define why support has been dropping, and will continue to decline.

Because if we don’t say definitively that we are critical of this war because of the humanitarian disaster it has inflicted on an entire people, others will use this political energy as an opportunity to platform their antisemitic conspiracy theories.

And that already is happening. Right here in Nashville.

Nashville, Where Antisemitism Gets a Global Audience

The most prominent example of this dangerous trajectory is Candace Owens, who commands a massive conservative audience while working from Nashville. Owens has over 5 million followers on both X and Instagram, over 5 million YouTube subscribers, and during her time at The Daily Wire was estimated to be making $1.1 million per year for her show.

She relocated to Nashville in November 2020, launching her show “Candace” from Nashville, and proudly declared “I’m a Nashville resident and that just feels amazing to say.”

She is a prominent enough media figure that her move to the city was acknowledged by a non-binding resolution from the State of Tennessee.

When the Gaza war began in October 2023, Owens was broadcasting from Nashville to millions. Her initial commentary quickly descended into classic antisemitic tropes.

In March 2024, she addressed criticism that Kanye West was receiving for saying things like “I’m a Nazi” and “Some of my best friends are Jewish and I don’t trust any of them.” In the (almost) universal pushback that West received, she saw a conspiracy.

“And so what I believe is something that should be explored is whether or not what’s happening is that, just like within all communities, there are gangs. Right? Gangs can form… What if that is what is happening right now in Hollywood, if there is just a very small ring of specific people who are using the fact that they are Jewish to shield themselves from any criticism?… this appears to be something that is quite sinister.”
via Media Matters

Responding to Daily Wire co-founder Ben Shapiro’s criticism, she posted “You cannot serve both God and money” —invoking the antisemitic stereotypes of Jews as money-obsessed.

After The Daily Wire severed ties with her in March 2024, Owens’s rhetoric escalated dramatically. According to the ADL, she engaged in Holocaust denial, questioning whether 6 million Jews died and calling Holocaust education “Soviet indoctrination.”

The ADL reports that she promoted blood libel, claiming Leo Frank’s family, “(a Jewish man lynched in 1913 by a mob in Georgia after being wrongfully accused of murdering a young girl who worked at his factory)” practiced pedophilia and incest “as the sacramental rites”.

Most recently, after Charlie Kirk’s September 2025 assassination, according to NYT she promoted conspiracy theories claiming he’d been pressured by Netanyahu and Jewish donors, contributing to an ecosystem that produced 10,000 posts claiming “Israel killed Charlie Kirk”.

This is why Senator Blackburn’s binary framing is so dangerous, and why the Metro Council resolution matters.

Nashville served as Owens’s platform when the war began—The Daily Wire’s headquarters have been here since 2020. When leaders like Blackburn refuse to acknowledge that humanitarian concerns about civilian casualties are legitimate, they create a vacuum. That vacuum gets filled by voices like Owens, who reach millions with conspiracy theories about Jewish control and power.

By clearly stating that Nashville’s opposition to this war is rooted in humanitarian law and universal human rights—not antisemitism—we inoculate our community against this poisonous trajectory.

Defining Our Reason for Opposition Is More than Mere Words

The rejection of how Israel is doing this war is no longer just something that the Left is discussing.

But is this lack of support because of true humanitarian care for the people of Palestine, or because of dangerous conspiracy theories that need to be denounced by everyone from our Metro Council to our US Senators?

Others on the Right are opposing this war for ostensibly more humanitarian reasons. Tucker Carlson, of all people, is a voice of reason, tweeting:

“The real divide isn’t between people who support Israel and people who support Iran or the Palestinians. The real divide is between those who casually encourage violence, and those who seek to prevent it — between warmongers and peacemakers.”

Critique on the Left isn’t taking Carlson at his word, and thinks he has a subtextual agenda that isn’t all that concerns about the tens of thousands of civilians killed and wounded in Gaza.

But if he has a secret agenda, he’s buried it deep within the words of this tweet, because on its surface he is stating an obvious, universal truth.

None of us want wanton war and destruction. All great acts of injustice are committed by people who were aggrieved and felt justified. That doesn’t make them right.

Senator Blackburn’s tweet, the one that made me consider that maybe this is something Nashville needs to consider, boils down to a moral code that I think all of us can rise far above. She is saying, essentially, “they started it.”

I know enough about this war and the long conflict that preceded it to say its origins are far more complicated than that.

But I also know, and so do you, that the question of whether unrestrained bombing and starvation of an entire population is justified by the actions of their government—a government that hasn’t held elections in 19 years—is not complicated at all.

It’s wrong.

I’ve put a fair amount of thought into it, and here’s where I’m landing about why the Metro Council should weigh in on this topic.

The Metro Council resolution accomplishes something crucial: it plants a flag in the ground declaring that Nashville’s opposition to this war is grounded in universal humanitarian principles—international law, civilian protection, and human rights—and decidedly NOT in antisemitic conspiracy theories.

By explicitly citing findings from the UN, International Criminal Court, and human rights organizations, the resolution creates a documented record of why this community opposes the war’s conduct.

This matters because as opposition the war is irreversibly growing, especially among younger conservatives, the narrative void will be filled by someone.

Either it gets filled by voices articulating legitimate humanitarian concerns, or it gets filled by people like Owens peddling ancient prejudices about Jewish control and power. The resolution is preventative medicine—it inoculates the community by establishing the moral framework before conspiracy theories can take root.

When someone in Nashville starts sharing Owens-style theories, community leaders can point to this resolution and say: “That’s not what we stand for. Here’s what our opposition is actually about.” It transforms the rising tide from an undefined rejection into a principled stance, making it much harder for bad-faith actors to co-opt legitimate humanitarian concerns for antisemitic ends.

To quote the last person I ever thought I’d agree with:

It’s the most truthful and easiest thing to say that Oct 7th in Israel was horrific and all hostages must be returned, but so is the genocide, humanitarian crisis, and starvation happening in Gaza.

Marjorie Taylor Greene
