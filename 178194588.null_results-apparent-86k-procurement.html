When Wallace Studios opened its doors in 2024, the 130-unit1 affordable housing complex on Wallace Road was supposed to represent a solution to Nashville’s homelessness crisis. Developer Adam Rosenberg’s property has since collected over $90,000 in city funds specifically designed to house the city’s most vulnerable residents, plus another $86k of furnishings provided by the city. But court records reveal a disturbing pattern: at least 33 eviction proceedings have been filed against tenants in the complex’s first year alone. And at least 10 of those received subsidies from the city to move into the facility.

OHS didn’t simply financially assist in placing people at Wallace Studios, it helped the facility come online by providing some or all of its furnishings across its 130 units. Invoices show the Office of Homeless Services spent $86,103.61 for furniture for 97 Wallace on February 29, 2024. The expense was billed against Nashville’ landlord engagement funds.

A search of the Metro Nashville contract database yielded no contract with Wayfair, with any Metro department. The procurement threshold for Metro Nashville departments is $25,000. Yet somehow OHS was able to purchase nearly $90k from an online retailer without going through any form of procurement process. A review of OHS’s R12 accounting data reveals this invoice was indeed processed.

What happened at 97 Wallace?

The money trail leads directly to Metro Nashville’s Office of Homeless Services (OHS), which paid “landlord engagement funds”—typically $1,500 per unit—to incentivize Wallace Studios to accept formerly unhoused residents. These payments effectively underwrote lease agreements for individuals who would never have qualified on their own. PDF invoices obtained through public records confirm OHS made these payments to secure housing placements through various referral agencies.

Yet despite Metro’s direct financial involvement in placing these residents, neither the city nor OHS appear anywhere on the lease agreements. More troubling still, no documentation has emerged showing what arrangements, if any, were made to ensure residents could afford rent after the initial placement funds ran out.

The human cost is stark: Of the 33 eviction cases filed since the complex opened, at least 10 involved residents whose placement had been subsidized by Metro Nashville’s landlord engagement funds. These residents found themselves facing eviction from the very housing the city had paid to secure for them.

Documents obtained by public records request demonstrate conclusively that at least ten of these occupants were placed at Wallace Studios with the aid of “landlord engagement funds” were evicted through court proceedings. The documents are provided below in redacted form with the names of the residents disguised.

In early 2025, Nashville Council Member Ginny Welsch filed a public records request targeting a specific concern: How was the Office of Homeless Services (OHS) spending federal “landlord engagement funds”?

These ARPA-funded payments are designed for a narrow purpose—incentivizing landlords to rent to vulnerable populations through security deposits, minor repairs, and sign-on bonuses. But Welsch had received information suggesting these funds were being depleted inappropriately or faster than expected.

What my previous reporting uncovered is that much of this funding was not going to “landlord engagement.” Here is a posting from my site for a “Community Audit” of OHS.

Questionable Temp Coding | $65,577.00 as “Landlord Engagement”

Landlord Engagement is a fund used to incentivize property owners to make their units available without barriers for people in need, a program called the Low Barrier Housing Collective. This fund was reported to have $500k “missing” from it. None of the nine temporary employees that appear to have been billed landlord engagement were recognized by a former staffer who had a leadership role with the Low Barrier Housing Collective.

At least $29,951 was for an employee that has been identified as an admininistrative assistant to Director Calvin.

The public records request did turn up legitimate “landlord engagement” invoices, hundreds of them. Those included the ones for people living at 97 Wallace.
