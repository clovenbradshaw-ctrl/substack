Tennessee's legacy isn't country music or healthcare—it's the policy of cruelty dressed up as responsibility. And now we've shipped it nationwide.

It’s not good for me to be focused on all the things we aren’t doing right. But I feel compelled to harp on things, as my readers know — and its not because I’m a curmudgeon. It’s because it doesn’t seem like its sinking in. Human society is a grand, complex system, maybe the most complex possible. But like even the simplest system, it only functions if it can accept feedback. And Nashville seems extremely good at resisting feedback.

Exactly twenty years ago, Tennessee conducted a social experiment that is still taught in public health and policy courses across the country as a case study of how a single bureaucratic action can cause lasting systemic damage.

Facing a budget shortfall in 2005, Governor Phil Bredesen executed one of the largest Medicaid cuts in U.S. history: over 190,000 Tennesseans were stripped from the TennCare rolls—almost overnight.

The results were swift, severe, and documented:

  • Evictions surged.

  • Homelessness ballooned.

  • Crime spiked by over 20% in some counties.

  • Drug overdoses rose.

  • Mental health ER visits climbed.

  • Rural hospitals shuttered.

Filmmaker Molly Secours, my aunt, chronicled this self-inflicted catastrophe in her Documentary Channel film Faces of TennCare. The film gave voice to real people in Middle Tennessee who had their healthcare yanked away and watched their lives fall apart. My aunt Molly, battling cancer in the wake of TennCare's liquidation, did her best to preserve the lessons of that ill-conceived experiment.

Millions lived through that trauma. Some filmed it. A few have studied it.

But collectively, as a city, as a state, as a country, we learned nothing. Except, perhaps, that “you can get away with it.”

Because now—exactly twenty years later—that same ideology has been exported to the entire nation. Nashville’s nihilistic, libertarian chickens have come home to roost. Cluck-cluck, y’all.

The One Big Beautiful Bill: Cruelty at Scale

On July 4, 2025, President Trump signed into law the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), a sweeping package of tax cuts, benefit rollbacks, and social program restrictions.

Unlike the 2005 TennCare cuts, this isn't just a health policy move—it's a multi-system takedown:

  • Medicaid (TennCare), SNAP, housing assistance, and behavioral health care have all been slashed or restructured.

  • 250,000 Tennesseans are expected to lose healthcare.

  • Tennessee counties are projected to lose $5.3 billion in SNAP funds over ten years.

  • Work requirements, state cost-sharing penalties, and copays have been layered into basic services.

  • Public hospitals and service nonprofits are already laying off staff.

What We Already Know Will Happen & The Catastrophes We Can’t Predict

The data from Tennessee's 2005 TennCare cuts provides a devastating preview of what's coming. When 190,000 Tennesseans lost coverage virtually overnight, researchers documented the cascading effects:

Health outcomes collapsed immediately. Cancer diagnoses shifted to later, less treatable stages with a 3.3 percentage point increase in late-stage breast cancer diagnoses in low-income areas. Emergency rooms became primary care for the newly uninsured, with an 18% spike in preventable condition visits.

Economic devastation rippled for years. Credit scores dropped 1.5+ points on average. Eviction filings increased 27.6% and completed evictions rose 24.5%. Hospitals faced massive financial strain, forcing widespread staff cuts.

Crime rates spiked as predicted. Violent crime increased 20.6% and non-violent crime rose 14.1% in the most affected counties. Domestic violence incidents jumped 25.8%, creating a $232 million economic burden. The loss of mental health and addiction services directly correlated with increased criminal activity.

The effects persisted for over a decade. Studies conducted 5-10 years later found ongoing financial distress, elevated crime rates, and continued housing instability.1 Rural hospitals never recovered, accelerating the current crisis.

This isn't speculation. It's the documented trajectory of what happens when you strip healthcare from vulnerable populations. And now we're scaling that experiment nationwide, and we’re knocking over nearly every pillar holding up our society at once.

The 2025 Experiment: When We Break Everything At Once

Model of interconnected systems failure generated under my moniker “Intelechia.”

Here's the thing about what we're doing now versus what we did in 2005: we're not just pulling one thread this time. We're taking scissors to the whole damn fabric.

Twenty years ago, we ripped 190,000 people off TennCare and watched the predictable horror show unfold—crime spiked, people got evicted, families fell apart. But here's what kept some folks from completely drowning: when they lost their healthcare, they could still get food stamps. When medical debt started piling up, housing assistance might keep a roof over their heads. When mental health crises hit, there were still some services to catch people before they completely broke.

The One Big Beautiful Bill just said "fuck all that." We're yanking healthcare from 250,000 people while simultaneously gutting SNAP benefits worth $5.3 billion and slashing housing assistance and behavioral health services. It's like we looked at 2005 and thought, "You know what went wrong? We left too many life preservers in the water."

Systems theory calls this "simultaneous multi-network failure," but let me translate: when you're drowning and someone throws you an anchor instead of a rope. The math is pretty simple—when multiple systems fail at once, the damage multiplies exponentially, not additively. It's not [healthcare collapse + housing collapse + food insecurity]. It's [healthcare collapse × housing collapse × food insecurity], and that multiplication creates numbers that should terrify anyone who understands feedback loops.

Here's how the cascading nightmare actually works in real life: Your diabetes medication gets cut off because you lose TennCare. You can't afford the $300/month insulin, so you start rationing it. Your blood sugar spikes, you miss work, you get fired. Normally, you might qualify for emergency housing assistance or SNAP benefits to survive while you find another job. But guess what? Those got axed too. So now you're facing eviction with no food assistance and untreated diabetes. When you end up in the ER in diabetic ketoacidosis, the hospital can't get paid because—surprise—you also don't have insurance anymore.

Every safety net we just cut was connected to every other safety net. In 2005, when the healthcare system collapsed, it took time for the damage to spread through these connections. Families had months or years to find alternative support. This time, we're severing all the connections simultaneously. There's nowhere for people to bounce when they fall.

The feedback loops are going to be absolutely savage. When you can't get your mental health medications because you lost TennCare, and you can't afford therapy because behavioral health funding got slashed, and you're hungry because SNAP got cut, and you're about to be homeless because housing assistance vanished—all at the same time—that's not four separate problems. That's one exponentially larger problem where each crisis amplifies all the others.

We already know Tennessee never recovered from 2005. Crime rates are still elevated. Eviction patterns are still fucked. Health outcomes in the communities that got hit hardest are still worse than they were before the cuts. We took a system that was already damaged, already fragile, already barely holding together in some places, and decided to hit it with a sledgehammer while it was down.

What terrifies me most is that we're doing this deliberately. In 2005, at least some people were surprised by how bad the cascade got. This time, we have twenty years of data showing exactly what happens when you break social safety net systems. We filmed it. We studied it. We published papers about it. And then we decided to do it again, but worse.

The systems folks have a term for this: "brittleness by design." We're intentionally eliminating every backup system, every redundancy, every thing that might catch people when they fall. We're creating the most fragile possible social infrastructure and then acting shocked when it shatters under pressure.

This isn't just policy failure—it's systems sabotage. And the people who will pay the price won't be the folks who designed this beautiful fucking disaster. It'll be the same people who always pay: the working families, the chronically ill, the disabled, the people who need multiple systems to work together just to survive.

We're about to find out what happens when you don't just break the safety net—you burn it.

The Wreckage Has Begun

Middle Tennessee is poised to be hit especially hard—not just because of poverty or policy choices, but because we already ran this test. We never rebuilt.

Homelessness is rising: Nashville's 2025 Point-in-Time count showed a 4.1% year-over-year increase. Over 2,180 people were unhoused on a single night. Shelters are at full capacity. SNAP cuts will disproportionately impact working families. Over 40% of rural hospitals are already at risk—TennCare cuts will accelerate closures.

Crime stats are down right now, but we've seen this movie before. In 2005, crime didn't spike immediately—it rose steadily over 18-24 months as people lost access to medication, mental health care, and basic survival tools. When crime explodes in mid-to-late 2026 and the usual suspects demand bigger police budgets, remember: law enforcement funding has grown every year while we systematically demolished the infrastructure that keeps communities stable. The missing ingredients aren't badges and body cameras—they're insulin and rent money and school lunches.

Systems That Can't Hear Get Louder

I focus intensely on homelessness statistics and food insecurity data not because I'm addicted to bad news, but because these are early warning systems our civic body keeps ignoring. When systems can't absorb feedback—when we treat rising homelessness as a "homeless problem" instead of a housing/healthcare/economic problem—those systems double down on their mistakes. They get louder instead of smarter.

The 2005 TennCare cuts taught us this in real time: counties that refused to track social fallout kept making deeper cuts. Counties that absorbed feedback found workarounds and local solutions. Information that doesn't get absorbed by systems gets amplified by systems.

Nashville's response to homelessness—criminalization, sweeps, anti-camping laws—shows a system that can't hear its own feedback loops. And we're about to test that same deafness against a much larger crisis.

Our Chief Export Isn't Music. It's Delusion.

One of the deepest tragedies of the original TennCare collapse is that it never entered Nashville's civic memory. This city rebranded itself instead of rebuilding itself. Rooftop bars replaced clinics. We painted over the grief with butterfly murals and told ourselves it wasn't that bad.

We didn't metabolize what we did. We created a feedback-resistant system that could only absorb information supporting its growth narrative. And so we allowed that cruelty to be exported as national policy.

People like my aunt tried to tell us in 2005 that history, unlearned, doesn't repeat itself. It metastasizes. But we shut our ears and criminalized homelessness. We chose deafness over diagnosis.

When people think of Tennessee, they think of guitars and biscuits. They don't see the place where the modern model of rolling back the safety net was born. But that's our real product: we produce the ideology of austerity cloaked in civility. We frame cruelty as reform. We package poverty as personal failure.

We didn't just let the One Big Beautiful Bill happen. We built it. We tested it on our own neighbors. And now we're selling it to the country—Nashville Hot Chicken flavored. And just like the "Extra Spicy" at Prince's, the pain is the point.
